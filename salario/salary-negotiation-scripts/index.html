Salary Negotiation Scripts That Actually Work: 2026 Guide

Most people know they should negotiate their salary but freeze when it comes time to actually say the words. This guide gives you exact scripts for every common negotiation scenario, from first job offers to promotion conversations, so you never have to improvise under pressure.

March 9, 2026 14 min read

Why Scripts Matter More Than Confidence

Research from Columbia Business School shows that people who prepare specific phrases and practice them negotiate salaries that are 7-12% higher on average than those who "wing it." The reason is not confidence. It is precision. When you know exactly what to say, you avoid the three most common negotiation mistakes: accepting too quickly, making emotional arguments, and failing to anchor the conversation to data.

The scripts below are not magic words. They work because they follow proven negotiation principles: they lead with value, anchor to market data, use collaborative framing, and always include a specific number. Before using any of these scripts, do your homework. Use our Salary Calculator to understand your current total compensation and the salary benchmarking guide to establish your market rate.

Every script below includes the key phrase, an explanation of why it works, and common variations you can adapt to your specific situation.

Script 1: Negotiating a New Job Offer

This is the highest-leverage negotiation you will ever have. Companies expect candidates to negotiate, and hiring managers typically have 10-20% flexibility above their initial offer. Here is how to handle it:

The Initial Response (Buy Time)

"Thank you so much for the offer. I am genuinely excited about this role and the team. I would like to take a day or two to review the full package carefully. Could you send me the details in writing so I can look at everything together?"

Why it works: Never negotiate in the moment you receive an offer. Excitement and pressure lead to suboptimal decisions. Asking for the offer in writing gives you time to research, calculate your net pay, and prepare your counter.

The Counter-Offer

"After reviewing the offer and researching market data for this role in [city/industry], I have found that the range for someone with my experience and skill set is $[X] to $[Y]. Given my background in [specific achievement] and the impact I expect to make in this role, I would like to propose a base salary of $[specific number at or slightly above midpoint]. I believe this reflects both the market rate and the value I will bring to the team."

Why it works: You anchor to a range supported by data, cite specific qualifications, and name a precise number. Research shows that specific numbers (like $138,500 rather than $140,000) are perceived as more researched and yield better outcomes.

If They Say the Salary Is Firm

"I understand the base salary may be fixed at this level. I am still very interested in the role. Could we discuss other components of the package? I am thinking about things like a signing bonus, additional equity, an earlier review cycle, extra PTO, or a professional development budget. What flexibility exists in those areas?"

Why it works: By shifting to non-salary compensation, you open new doors without pushing back on a hard limit. A $10,000 signing bonus, 5 extra PTO days, and $3,000 in education budget can add $15,000 or more in total value. Use our Job Offer Comparison tool to evaluate the full package.

Script 2: Asking Your Current Employer for a Raise

Negotiating with your current employer is different from negotiating a new offer. You have an existing relationship, a track record, and organizational knowledge that gives you leverage. You also have risk: pushing too hard can create awkwardness. These scripts balance assertiveness with diplomacy.

Opening the Conversation

"I would like to schedule time to discuss my compensation. Over the past [time period], I have [specific accomplishment 1], [specific accomplishment 2], and [specific accomplishment 3]. I believe my contributions have grown beyond what my current salary reflects, and I would like to discuss bringing my compensation in line with my impact and the current market rate for this role."

Why it works: You lead with concrete results rather than feelings or tenure. Managers respond to business impact, not "I have been here a long time" or "I need more money." List three specific, measurable achievements before you mention money.

Presenting Your Case

"I have done research on market rates for [role title] with [X years] of experience in [industry/city]. The market range is $[X] to $[Y]. My current salary of $[current] falls [below/at the low end of] this range. Given my track record here and the additional responsibilities I have taken on, I am requesting an adjustment to $[target]. This would bring my compensation to the midpoint of the market range and reflect the expanded scope of my work."

Why it works: Data removes emotion from the conversation. When you can show market rates from multiple sources, your request becomes about alignment rather than personal desire. Use our Salary Calculator to compare your pay to market benchmarks.

When They Say "Not in the Budget"

"I understand budget constraints are real. Can we agree on a specific timeline and milestones for revisiting this? I would like to set a follow-up for [3 months from now] with clear criteria for what would justify the adjustment. In the meantime, are there other ways to bridge the gap, like a one-time bonus, additional PTO, a title change, or a professional development budget?"

Why it works: You acknowledge reality without accepting a permanent "no." By establishing a timeline and milestones, you create accountability and a clear path forward. The alternative compensation request gives your manager a way to say "yes" to something, which maintains positive momentum. Script 3: Negotiating a Promotion

Promotion negotiations often stall because the employee focuses on the title while the employer focuses on the budget. The best approach ties the title change to a business case and positions the salary adjustment as a natural consequence.

Initiating the Promotion Discussion

"I have been performing at the [next level] for the past [time period]. Specifically, I have been [responsibility 1 that is above current level], [responsibility 2], and [responsibility 3]. I would like to discuss formalizing my role as [target title] with compensation that reflects the scope of work I am already doing. Based on market data, [target title] in our industry pays $[X] to $[Y]."

Why it works: By demonstrating you are already doing the job, you reframe the promotion from a request for something new to recognition of existing reality. This is much harder for a manager to deny because the evidence is already visible. Calculate what the new role should pay using our hourly to salary converter or Salary Calculator.

If They Offer the Title Without a Raise

"I appreciate the title change, and I am glad my work has been recognized. I want to make sure the compensation reflects the new expectations. The market rate for [new title] is $[X] to $[Y], and my current salary of $[current] falls below that range. A title without corresponding compensation creates a gap that could make it harder to retain me long-term, and I want us both to feel good about this. Can we discuss an adjustment to at least $[target]?"

Why it works: You frame the salary adjustment as being in the company's interest (retention) rather than just yours. The phrase "I want us both to feel good about this" positions the negotiation as collaborative rather than adversarial.

Script 4: Negotiating as a Remote Worker

Remote workers face a unique challenge: companies may try to adjust pay based on location. These scripts help you push back on geo-adjusted pay cuts or negotiate remote-specific benefits.

Responding to a Location-Based Pay Cut

"I understand the company uses location-based pay bands. I want to point out that my work output, availability, and impact are identical regardless of where I sit. Companies like [Basecamp/Reddit/Airbnb] have moved to location-independent pay because they found that geo-adjustments create retention risk without improving business outcomes. I would like to discuss maintaining my current salary. If that is not possible, could we explore a smaller adjustment with a path to equalization based on performance?"

Script 5: Email Templates

Sometimes the best negotiation happens in writing. Email gives you control over word choice and creates a record. Here are two essential templates.

Counter-Offer Email for a New Job

Subject: [Your Name] - Offer Discussion

"Hi [Hiring Manager], thank you again for the offer to join [Company] as [Role]. I am very excited about the opportunity and the team. After reviewing the full package and researching market compensation for this role, I would like to discuss the base salary. Based on data from [sources], the market range for [Role] with [X years] experience in [location] is $[X]-$[Y]. Given my experience with [relevant skill/achievement], I would like to propose a base salary of $[specific number]. I am confident this reflects both the value I will bring and the current market. I am flexible on how we get there, whether through base salary, a signing bonus, or other components. I look forward to finding a number that works for both of us."

Raise Request Email to Your Manager

Subject: Compensation Discussion Request

"Hi [Manager], I would like to schedule 30 minutes to discuss my compensation. Over the past [period], I have [accomplishment 1 with metrics], [accomplishment 2 with metrics], and [accomplishment 3]. I have also taken on [new responsibility]. Based on my research into market rates for [role] at our level, I believe an adjustment is warranted. I have put together some data I would like to share. Would [date/time] work for a conversation?"

Why email works here: For the initial request, email gives your manager time to prepare rather than being caught off guard. The actual negotiation should happen in a live meeting, but the email sets the stage and signals seriousness. Common Mistakes That Kill Negotiations

Even good scripts fail if you make these tactical errors:

  • Revealing your current salary too early. In many states, it is now illegal for employers to ask. If pressed, redirect: "I would prefer to focus on the value of this role and the market rate rather than my current compensation."
  • Giving a range instead of a number. When you say "$120,000 to $140,000," the employer hears "$120,000." Always anchor with a specific number at the upper end of your range.
  • Negotiating only on base salary. Total compensation includes bonus, equity, benefits, PTO, and perks. A $5,000 base salary increase compounds over time, but a $15,000 signing bonus has immediate impact. Consider both. See what your total package is worth with our total compensation guide.
  • Accepting verbally without written confirmation. Always get the final offer in writing before accepting. Verbal agreements can be "misremembered."
  • Using threats or ultimatums. "Pay me more or I leave" works exactly once and damages the relationship. Frame requests collaboratively: "I want to find a number that works for both of us."

Timing Your Negotiation

When you negotiate matters almost as much as what you say. The best times to negotiate a raise are:

  • After a major accomplishment. Just closed a big deal, shipped a major project, or received positive feedback? That is your window.
  • During annual review season. Budget decisions are actively being made. Managers expect compensation discussions.
  • When you take on new responsibilities. A scope increase without a pay increase is a de facto pay cut relative to your output.
  • When you have a competing offer. This is the strongest leverage, but use it carefully. Only mention a competing offer you would genuinely accept.

The worst times are during company layoffs, budget freezes, right after being hired, or when your manager is dealing with a crisis. Read the room and pick your moment strategically.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best way to start a salary negotiation?

Start by expressing enthusiasm, then pivot to compensation with data. A strong opener: "I am excited about this opportunity. Based on my research into market rates and the value I bring, I would like to discuss the compensation package." Leading with enthusiasm shows engagement while referencing research signals preparation.

Should I negotiate over email or in person?

Both work. Email gives you time to craft precise language and provides a written record, making it ideal for initial counter-offers on new job offers. For raise negotiations with your current manager, a live conversation followed by a summary email is most effective. Use whichever format helps you communicate more clearly.

How much higher should I counter-offer?

Counter 10-20% above the initial offer for new positions, depending on how far below market the offer is. For internal raises, 10-15% above current salary is reasonable with strong justification. Always anchor your counter to market data rather than a percentage. "Market data shows $135,000-$150,000" is stronger than "I want 15% more."
