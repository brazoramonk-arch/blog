$15 an Hour Is How Much a Year? Annual Salary Breakdown

The $15 minimum wage debate has dominated labor policy for a decade. But once the politics are stripped away, there is a practical question worth answering: what does $15/hour actually mean for your annual budget, your taxes, and your ability to cover your bills?

April 12, 2026 12 min read

Key Takeaways

  • $15/hour × 2,080 hours = $31,200 gross per year
  • After federal taxes and FICA, take-home is roughly $25,600–$26,500 in most states
  • The 2026 federal minimum wage is still $7.25/hr — but 17 states plus D.C. have reached $15+
  • MIT's Living Wage Calculator shows $15/hr falls below subsistence level in most U.S. metro areas
  • With dependents, $31,200 gross qualifies for significant Earned Income Tax Credit refunds

Debunking the "$15 Minimum Wage Is Enough to Live On" Myth

The $15/hour federal minimum wage has been the rallying cry of the labor movement for over a decade. When the Fight for $15 campaign started in 2012, that figure represented a meaningful increase over the $7.25 federal floor. In 2026, $15/hour is a different story.

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Price Index, cumulative inflation from 2012 to 2026 has eroded purchasing power by approximately 40%. In real (inflation-adjusted) terms, a 2026 wage of $15/hour is roughly equivalent to $10.71/hour in 2012 dollars — below the original target's real value.

MIT's Living Wage Calculator provides a more grounded framework. It calculates the minimum hourly wage needed to cover basic expenses — housing, food, transportation, healthcare, and taxes — without any discretionary spending. In 2026, the living wage for a single adult with no children ranges from:

  • $18.02/hour — rural Mississippi (lowest in the nation)
  • $22.40/hour — U.S. national average for a single adult
  • $28.50/hour — San Francisco metro area
  • $31.20/hour — New York City metro area

By MIT's measure, $15/hour does not meet the living wage threshold for a single adult anywhere in the continental United States in 2026. This is not a political statement — it is arithmetic based on median rent, food costs, and healthcare premiums in each county.

Tax Credits That Make a Real Difference at $31,200

At $31,200 in gross income, the federal tax burden is already light. But several credits can reduce your tax liability further — or even generate a refund larger than what you paid in:

Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

The EITC is the most powerful tax benefit available to lower-income workers. The 2026 income thresholds and maximum credits:

A single parent earning $31,200 with two children could receive an EITC refund of nearly $7,000 — effectively boosting their annual income by 22%. This is the most significant tax benefit available at this wage level. Many eligible workers fail to claim it; according to the IRS, about 20% of eligible families do not file for the EITC each year.

Child Tax Credit

Workers with qualifying children can claim up to $2,000 per child in Child Tax Credit (CTC). At $31,200 in income, the full $2,000/child credit is available because the phase-out threshold begins at $200,000 for single filers. Up to $1,700 per child is refundable (ACTC), meaning you can receive it as a refund even if your tax liability is zero.

Saver's Credit

Single filers earning under $38,250 in 2026 can claim the Saver's Credit for retirement contributions. Workers at $31,200 who contribute to a 401(k) or IRA can receive a credit equal to 20% of their contributions (up to $2,000 contributed = $400 credit). This is a direct tax credit on top of the tax deduction for the contribution itself.

Strategies to Maximize Income at $15/Hour

If $15/hour is your current rate, these are the practical moves that have the highest ROI for increasing your take-home income:

1. Prioritize Employers with Benefits Packages

Two jobs paying $15/hour are not equal if one includes health insurance and 401(k) matching. Employer-sponsored health coverage is worth $6,000–$8,000 per year in avoided premium costs. A 3% 401(k) match on $31,200 is $936 in free money annually. Total compensation at a benefits-bearing $15/hour job can be equivalent to $18–$19/hour at a bare-wages job.

2. Target Overtime-Eligible Roles

At $15/hour, five hours of weekly overtime adds $11,700 in annual gross income (at $22.50/hour OT rate). Many warehouse, healthcare aide, and retail supervisor roles offer consistent overtime. Eight hours of weekly overtime pushes your gross above $47,000 — nearly 50% higher than the base $15 calculation.

3. Pursue Certifications That Cross the $20 Threshold

The wage gap between $15 and $20/hour is achievable through targeted certification in many fields. According to Payscale's 2025 Compensation Best Practices Report, certifications that reliably lift workers from $15 into $20+ territory include: CompTIA A+ (IT support), Phlebotomy Technician certification, OSHA 30-Hour (construction), QuickBooks ProAdvisor (bookkeeping), and Amazon Web Services Cloud Practitioner.

4. Use Geographic Arbitrage

If you work remotely or have location flexibility, $15/hour in rural Ohio (median 1BR rent: $750) goes significantly further than $15/hour in Boston ($2,400). The Cheapest States to Live In shows which states offer the most purchasing power per dollar of income.

Frequently Asked Questions

$15 an hour is how much a year?

$15 an hour equals $31,200 per year gross, based on a standard 40-hour workweek (2,080 hours annually). After federal income tax and FICA, a single filer with no dependents takes home approximately $25,600–$26,500 depending on their state. Workers with qualifying children may receive thousands more via the Earned Income Tax Credit.

How much is $15 an hour monthly?

$15 an hour equals $2,600 per month gross. After federal and state taxes, take-home ranges from about $2,028 in California to $2,189 in Texas or Florida. The difference is primarily state income tax, which does not apply in seven states including Texas, Florida, and Washington.

Is $15 an hour enough to live on?

$15/hour ($31,200/year) is sufficient for basic living expenses in low-cost rural areas but falls short of a living wage in most U.S. metro areas. MIT's Living Wage Calculator shows single adults need $18–$26/hour in most cities to cover rent, food, transportation, and healthcare without public assistance.

What states have a $15 minimum wage in 2026?

As of 2026, 17 states plus Washington D.C. have minimum wages at or above $15/hour, according to the National Employment Law Project. States include California ($16.50), New York ($16.00+), Washington ($17.13), Massachusetts ($15.00), New Jersey ($15.49), Connecticut ($16.35), and others.

How much federal tax do I pay on $31,200?

A single filer earning $31,200 in 2026 pays $1,706 in federal income tax after the $15,000 standard deduction. The effective federal income tax rate is just 5.5%. FICA taxes add $2,386, bringing total federal deductions to $4,092 — leaving you with $27,108 before state tax.

How much is $15 an hour biweekly?

$15 an hour generates a gross biweekly paycheck of $1,200 (80 hours × $15). After federal and state taxes, most single workers take home roughly $960–$1,035 biweekly, depending on state income tax rates and withholding elections.

What jobs pay $15 an hour in 2026?

Common jobs at $15–$17/hour include retail positions at major employers like Target and Walmart, Amazon warehouse associates ($20.50 minimum), home health aides ($16.57 BLS median), food service supervisors, and entry-level office clerks. Many entry-level healthcare, childcare, and government roles also start in this range.
