How to Benchmark Your Salary: Data Sources & Strategies

Knowing your market value is the foundation of every salary negotiation. This guide covers the best data sources, how to adjust for location and experience, and strategies for building a compelling case for higher pay.

March 7, 2026 12 min read

Why Salary Benchmarking Matters

Most workers have no idea whether they are being paid fairly. Studies consistently show that 60-70% of employees believe they are underpaid, but only about 40% actually are when compared to market data. The remaining 20-30% think they are underpaid simply because they have never benchmarked their salary against reliable data.

Salary benchmarking is the process of comparing your compensation to what other professionals with similar roles, experience, and location earn. Without this data, salary negotiations become guesswork. You might accept an offer $10,000 below market, or you might make an unreasonable counter-offer that costs you a job opportunity.

The compounding effect of accurate benchmarking is substantial. Getting paid $5,000 more per year at age 30 translates to roughly $250,000 in additional lifetime earnings (assuming 3% annual raises). If that extra income is invested at 7% annual returns, the total wealth difference approaches $600,000 by retirement. A single well-researched negotiation can be worth more than half a million dollars over your career.

Top Salary Data Sources

Not all salary data is created equal. Some sources survey employers directly, others rely on self-reported data, and a few verify compensation through documentation. Here are the most reliable sources for salary benchmarking:

1. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)

The BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) program surveys over 1.1 million establishments to produce employment and wage estimates for more than 800 occupations. This is the gold standard for statistically rigorous salary data because it comes directly from employers, not self-reported estimates. Data is available at national, state, and metropolitan levels.

Strengths: Largest sample size, employer-verified, available by region. Limitations: 12-18 month reporting lag, broad occupation categories that may not match your specific role, does not include bonuses or equity compensation.

2. Glassdoor

Glassdoor has amassed millions of salary reports from current and former employees. Users submit their base salary, bonuses, and total compensation, along with company name, title, and location. The platform uses statistical models to produce salary ranges for specific roles at specific companies.

Strengths: Company-specific data, large sample for popular roles, includes bonuses and benefits ratings. Limitations: Self-reported (potential for bias), salary may be outdated, can underreport equity compensation, requires an account to access full data.

3. Levels.fyi

Levels.fyi is the go-to resource for tech industry compensation. Unlike most platforms, it verifies compensation data through offer letters, W-2s, and pay stubs, making it significantly more accurate for technology roles. The platform standardizes job levels across companies (e.g., Google L5 = Facebook E5 = Amazon L6) to enable apples-to-apples comparisons.

Strengths: Verified data, includes equity and bonus breakdowns, excellent for tech roles, standardized leveling. Limitations: Focused primarily on tech/engineering, limited data for non-tech industries, skewed toward large companies.

4. Payscale

Payscale uses a detailed survey methodology, asking users about their specific job duties, skills, certifications, years of experience, and education. This granularity allows for more precise estimates than platforms that only ask for title and location. Payscale's reports include percentile ranges (10th, 25th, 50th, 75th, 90th) for each role.

Strengths: Detailed skill-based matching, percentile breakdown, good for non-tech roles, includes total compensation estimates. Limitations: Smaller sample size than Glassdoor, may have fewer data points for niche roles.

5. LinkedIn Salary Insights

LinkedIn draws salary data from its massive professional network. Because LinkedIn has detailed profile data (industry, company, title, location, experience), its salary estimates can be quite granular. LinkedIn also shows salary ranges in many job postings, reflecting what employers are actually willing to pay.

Strengths: Integrated with job postings, huge dataset, good industry coverage. Limitations: Self-reported, may lag behind market changes, premium features locked behind subscription.

Additional Resources for Niche Industries

Beyond the major platforms, several industry-specific resources provide deeper compensation insights:

  • Blind (TeamBlind): Anonymous professional network popular in tech. Features verified compensation posts with detailed breakdowns of base, bonus, RSU, and signing bonus. Especially strong for FAANG and startup compensation data.
  • Robert Half Salary Guide: Published annually by staffing firm Robert Half. Excellent for accounting, finance, legal, technology, and administrative roles. Includes regional adjustment factors.
  • Salary.com CompAnalyst: Enterprise-grade compensation database used by HR professionals. Individual access is limited, but their public salary wizard provides decent estimates. Best for traditional industries.
  • H1B Salary Database: Public records of salaries for H1B visa holders, available at h1bdata.info. Useful for tech and healthcare roles because employers must report actual wages. Note that H1B salaries may be at or above prevailing wage, which can differ from actual market rates.
  • Dice (Tech): Annual tech salary report with data segmented by programming language, framework, platform, and certification.
  • Mercer and Radford Surveys: Premium compensation surveys used by Fortune 500 companies. Individual access is expensive, but your employer may subscribe. Ask HR if they participate.

Combine data from multiple sources for the most accurate benchmark. No single source is perfect, but triangulating three or more sources gives you high confidence in your market value.

How to Adjust for Location

Salaries vary dramatically by geography, even for identical roles. A software engineer earning $150,000 in San Francisco is not necessarily better compensated than one earning $110,000 in Austin after adjusting for cost of living and state taxes. Here is how to make fair location-based comparisons:

When adjusting for location, focus on purchasing power rather than nominal salary. A $120,000 salary in Austin with no state income tax and affordable housing may deliver more purchasing power than $180,000 in San Francisco with 13.3% state tax and $3,500 monthly rent. For remote workers, many companies use location-based pay bands. Understanding where your city falls on the band helps you negotiate effectively. Some companies pay San Francisco rates regardless of location (GitLab, Basecamp), while others adjust by up to 30% based on local cost of living (Google, Meta).

Understanding Total Compensation

Benchmarking base salary alone gives an incomplete picture. Total compensation (TC) includes all forms of pay and benefits you receive from your employer. Here is how to calculate your TC for accurate benchmarking:

In technology and finance, equity compensation can represent 30-60% of total compensation for mid-to-senior roles. A senior engineer at Google might have a $200,000 base salary but $350,000 in total compensation after RSUs and bonus. Ignoring equity when benchmarking would make this role appear underpaid relative to a $230,000 base salary with no equity.

Step-by-Step Benchmarking Process

Follow this systematic process to benchmark your salary with confidence:

Step 1: Define Your Comparison Profile

Start by identifying the key variables that define your role: job title (use the most common market title, not internal titles), years of experience, industry, company size, geographic location, and specific skills or certifications. The more precisely you define your profile, the more accurate your benchmark will be.

Step 2: Gather Data From Multiple Sources

Query at least three salary data sources for your role. Start with BLS for the authoritative baseline, then check Glassdoor and Payscale for company-specific and skill-adjusted estimates. If you are in tech, add Levels.fyi. Record the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentile figures from each source.

Step 3: Adjust for Your Specifics

Apply location adjustments if the data source uses national averages. Factor in your experience level (entry, mid, senior, lead). Account for specialized skills, certifications, or security clearances that command premium pay. Consider company stage (startup vs. enterprise) and industry (finance typically pays more than nonprofit for the same role).

Step 4: Calculate Your Market Range

After gathering adjusted data from multiple sources, create a market range with three benchmarks: low (25th percentile), mid (50th percentile), and high (75th percentile). Your target should be at least the 50th percentile if you are a solid performer, and 75th or higher if you bring exceptional value, rare skills, or significant tenure.

Step 5: Build Your Case

Document your findings in a clear format. Include the data sources, specific figures, and your adjustments. Pair this data with evidence of your personal contributions: revenue generated, projects delivered, efficiency improvements, or scope expanded. A data-driven case is far more persuasive than simply asking for more money.

Common Benchmarking Mistakes

Even with good data, several common mistakes can lead to inaccurate salary benchmarks:

  • Comparing titles, not responsibilities: A "Product Manager" at a 50-person startup may have completely different scope than a "Product Manager" at Amazon. Compare job descriptions and responsibilities, not just titles.
  • Ignoring total compensation: Base salary comparisons miss bonuses, equity, and benefits that can represent 20-50% of total pay. Always benchmark TC.
  • Using a single data source: Any one source can be skewed by sample bias, outdated data, or methodology limitations. Triangulate from at least three sources.
  • Not adjusting for location: National average data must be adjusted for your city. A salary at the 75th percentile nationally could be at the 25th percentile in San Francisco.
  • Anchoring to your current salary: Your current pay is irrelevant to market value. If you have been underpaid for years, your anchor point is already wrong. Start fresh with market data.
  • Comparing across company stages: Startups typically pay lower base salaries but offer equity, while large enterprises pay higher base with smaller equity grants. Compare within the same stage.
  • Outdating your data: Salary markets can shift 5-15% in a single year, especially in high-demand fields. Use the most recent data available and note the collection date.

The most important thing is to approach benchmarking as an ongoing process, not a one-time exercise. Market rates shift constantly. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most accurate source for salary data?

The BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics is the most statistically rigorous because it surveys 1.1 million employers. For tech roles, Levels.fyi is highly accurate due to verified offer letters and W-2s. The best approach is triangulating data from 3+ sources: BLS for baselines, Glassdoor for role-specific estimates, and Levels.fyi or Blind for tech compensation.

Should I include bonuses and stock options when benchmarking?

Yes, always benchmark total compensation (TC), not just base salary. In many industries, base salary represents only 50-70% of total compensation. Include annual bonuses, signing bonuses (amortized), stock/RSUs (at current value), 401(k) matching, and health insurance contributions for an accurate comparison.

How often should I benchmark my salary?

Benchmark at least once per year, ideally 2-3 months before your annual review. Also benchmark when changing jobs, receiving promotions, relocating, or switching industries. Market rates can shift 5-15% annually in fast-moving fields, so regular benchmarking ensures your pay keeps pace.
