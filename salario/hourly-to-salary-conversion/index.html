Hourly to Salary: How to Convert Your Pay

The complete guide to converting hourly wages to annual salary, with formulas for full-time, part-time, and overtime scenarios.

March 6, 2026 8 min read

Key Takeaways

  • Multiply your hourly rate by 2,080 to get your annual salary for a standard 40-hour workweek
  • Part-time workers should use their actual weekly hours times 52 instead
  • Overtime, PTO, and benefits value can change your effective annual compensation by 20-40%
  • Salaried positions often include benefits worth $10,000-$20,000+ beyond base pay

Why Converting Hourly to Salary Matters

Whether you are comparing job offers, negotiating a raise, or planning your budget, knowing how to convert an hourly wage to an annual salary is essential. Many job postings list pay in different formats, and understanding the conversion helps you make apples-to-apples comparisons between opportunities.

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, about 55.6% of US workers are paid on an hourly basis. If you are one of them, converting your hourly rate to an annual equivalent gives you a clearer picture of your total earning power and helps you evaluate benefits packages, tax implications, and long-term financial goals.

The Standard Formula: 2,080 Hours

The most widely used formula for converting hourly pay to annual salary is straightforward:

Annual Salary = Hourly Rate x 2,080

The number 2,080 comes from multiplying 40 hours per week by 52 weeks per year. This is the standard used by the US government, the Bureau of Labor Statistics, and most employers when calculating salaried equivalents.

Here are common hourly-to-salary conversions for quick reference:

Hourly Rate Weekly (40h) Monthly Annual
$15.00$600$2,600$31,200
$20.00$800$3,467$41,600
$25.00$1,000$4,333$52,000
$30.00$1,200$5,200$62,400
$40.00$1,600$6,933$83,200
$50.00$2,000$8,667$104,000

Monthly figures are calculated by dividing the annual salary by 12, not by multiplying weekly pay by 4. Using 4 weeks per month would undercount because most months have more than exactly 4 weeks.

Adjusting for Part-Time Hours

If you work fewer than 40 hours per week, the 2,080-hour formula does not apply directly. Instead, use this adjusted formula:

Annual Pay = Hourly Rate x Hours Per Week x 52

For example, if you earn $22 per hour and work 30 hours per week:

  • $22 x 30 = $660 per week
  • $660 x 52 = $34,320 per year

Keep in mind that part-time employees often do not receive the same benefits as full-time workers. Health insurance, paid time off, retirement contributions, and other perks can add 20-40% to the value of a full-time position. When comparing a part-time hourly role to a full-time salaried position, factor in the dollar value of benefits.

Many part-time workers also have variable schedules. If your hours fluctuate, use your average weekly hours over the past 3-6 months for a more accurate annual estimate.

Accounting for Overtime

Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), non-exempt employees must receive at least 1.5 times their regular rate for hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek. If you regularly work overtime, your actual annual earnings will be higher than the base 2,080-hour calculation.

Here is how to calculate annual pay with consistent overtime:

Annual = (Rate x 2,080) + (Rate x 1.5 x OT Hours x 52)

For example, if you earn $25/hour and work 5 hours of overtime per week:

  • Base pay: $25 x 2,080 = $52,000
  • Overtime pay: $25 x 1.5 x 5 x 52 = $9,750
  • Total annual earnings: $61,750

Paid Time Off and Actual Working Hours

The 2,080-hour standard assumes you work every week of the year. In practice, most full-time employees receive paid holidays and vacation days. If you are paid hourly without PTO, your actual annual earnings will be lower than the 2,080-hour calculation.

The average American worker receives about 10 paid holidays and 10-15 paid vacation days per year. That is roughly 4-5 weeks of paid time off. To calculate actual working hours for an hourly employee without PTO:

Actual Hours = (52 - Weeks Off) x 40

For example, with 3 weeks of unpaid time off: (52 - 3) x 40 = 1,960 hours. At $25/hour, that is $49,000 instead of $52,000.

Salaried employees typically receive PTO as part of their compensation package, so the 2,080-hour figure represents their actual annual pay even with vacation time. This is one of the hidden advantages of salaried positions that hourly workers should consider.

The True Value of Benefits

When comparing an hourly wage to a salaried position, raw pay is only part of the picture. The average employer-sponsored benefits package adds approximately 30% to total compensation. Here is what to include in your calculation:

  • Health insurance: The average employer contribution is about $6,500 per year for individual coverage and $16,000 for family coverage
  • Retirement matching: A typical 401(k) match of 3-6% of salary can add $1,500-$6,000+ per year
  • Paid time off: Two weeks of PTO at $25/hour is worth $2,000
  • Life and disability insurance: Typically $500-$1,500 per year in employer contributions
  • Education benefits: Tuition reimbursement programs can be worth $2,000-$5,250 per year
  • Other perks: Gym memberships, transit benefits, employee discounts, meal allowances

A salaried position paying $52,000 with a full benefits package could easily be worth $65,000-$70,000 in total compensation. An hourly position at $25/hour without benefits would need to pay significantly more per hour to match.

Hourly vs Salary: Which Is Better?

Neither hourly nor salaried pay is universally better. Each has advantages depending on your situation:

Hourly Advantages

  • Overtime pay for extra hours
  • Clear separation of work and personal time
  • Pay directly reflects hours worked
  • Legal protections under FLSA
  • Flexibility to pick up extra shifts

Salary Advantages

  • Predictable, consistent paychecks
  • Typically better benefits packages
  • Paid time off included
  • Career advancement opportunities
  • No pay reduction for slow weeks

Common Mistakes When Converting Pay

Avoid these frequent errors when converting hourly wages to annual salary:

  1. Using 4 weeks per month: There are 4.33 weeks per month on average. Multiplying weekly pay by 4 underestimates your monthly earnings by about 8%.
  2. Forgetting taxes: Your gross annual salary is not what you take home. Federal income tax, state tax, Social Security, and Medicare reduce your net pay by 20-35%. Use a Net Pay Calculator to estimate your actual take-home pay.
  3. Ignoring benefits value: Comparing raw hourly-to-salary numbers without factoring in health insurance, retirement matching, and PTO gives an incomplete picture.
  4. Not accounting for unpaid time off: Hourly workers without PTO earn less than the 2,080-hour calculation if they take any unpaid days.
  5. Overlooking overtime: If you consistently work more than 40 hours, your annual earnings are higher than the base conversion suggests.

How to Use This for Job Comparisons

When evaluating multiple job offers, create a total compensation comparison:

  1. Convert all offers to annual salary using the formulas above
  2. Add the estimated dollar value of benefits for each position
  3. Subtract estimated commuting costs and work-related expenses
  4. Consider cost-of-living differences if the jobs are in different cities
  5. Factor in growth potential, work-life balance, and career trajectory

Industry-Specific Conversion Considerations

Different industries use different conventions for calculating work hours, which affects how you convert hourly pay to annual salary:

  • Healthcare: Many nurses and technicians work 12-hour shifts on a 3-day-on, 4-day-off schedule. This results in 36 hours per week (1,872 hours per year), not the standard 2,080. Adjust your annual calculation accordingly.
  • Education: Teachers typically work 9-10 months per year. A teacher earning $30/hour for 7 hours per day, 180 school days works 1,260 hours per year, yielding $37,800 in base pay.
  • Construction and trades: Seasonal workers may only work 8-10 months per year. Weather shutdowns, project gaps, and winter slowdowns reduce annual hours well below 2,080.
  • Retail and hospitality: Variable scheduling means weekly hours can fluctuate from 15 to 40. Use a 3-6 month average of actual hours worked for a realistic annual estimate.
  • Tech and consulting: Some contract positions bill 1,880-2,000 hours per year, accounting for holidays and PTO the contractor must fund themselves.
  • Government: Federal employees often use a 2,087-hour work year to account for leap years and calendar variations. This slightly increases the hourly-to-salary conversion compared to the standard 2,080.

Understanding your industry's norms helps you make accurate comparisons and set realistic expectations during job searches and salary negotiations.

When comparing a salaried position to an hourly one in a different industry, always normalize both to the same number of working hours. A $50,000 teaching salary for 1,260 hours of work equates to $39.68 per hour, significantly higher than the $24.04 per hour that the same salary would represent over 2,080 hours.

This perspective shift is crucial. The total annual number may look lower, but the effective hourly rate tells a more accurate story about the value of your time.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I convert my hourly wage to an annual salary?

Multiply your hourly rate by 2,080 (40 hours per week times 52 weeks per year). For example, $25 per hour equals $52,000 per year. For part-time work, multiply your hourly rate by your actual weekly hours, then by 52.

Why is 2,080 hours used for salary conversion?

2,080 represents a standard full-time work year: 40 hours per week multiplied by 52 weeks. This is the standard used by the US Bureau of Labor Statistics and most employers for pay calculations and job postings.

Does the hourly to salary formula include overtime?

No. The standard 2,080-hour formula assumes 40 regular hours per week with no overtime. If you regularly work overtime, you need to add those hours separately at 1.5x your base rate. Use the formula: Annual = (Rate x 2,080) + (Rate x 1.5 x OT hours/week x 52).

How do I convert hourly to salary for part-time work?

Multiply your hourly rate by the number of hours you work per week, then multiply by 52. For example, $20/hour at 25 hours/week equals $26,000 per year ($20 x 25 x 52).
