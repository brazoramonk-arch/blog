How Much Is $30 an Hour Annually? Full Salary Breakdown

$62,400 per year. That is the gross annual figure for a $30/hour full-time worker — and it crosses an important threshold: it puts you above the U.S. median individual income in most datasets. But gross income is just the starting point. After federal taxes, FICA, and state income taxes that range from zero to nearly 13%, your actual take-home pay looks quite different. Here is the complete 2026 breakdown.

April 20, 2026 12 min read

Key Takeaways

  • $30/hour × 2,080 hours = $62,400 gross per year
  • After federal income tax and FICA, a single filer takes home roughly $46,000–$52,176 depending on state
  • No-income-tax states deliver approximately $6,176 more annually than California at this wage
  • $62,400 exceeds the MIT Living Wage for a single adult in all but the highest-cost metros (SF, NYC, DC)
  • Monthly gross is $5,200; biweekly gross paycheck is $2,400

$62,400: Where This Puts You Nationally

The first thing worth establishing about $30/hour is its position in the national income distribution. According to Bureau of Labor Statistics Current Population Survey data for Q4 2025, the median weekly earnings for full-time workers was approximately $1,165 — translating to roughly $60,580 annually. At $62,400, a $30/hour worker sits just above the national median for full-time wage and salary workers.

From a percentile standpoint, $62,400 puts you at approximately the 55th–60th percentile of individual earners in the United States when all workers (including part-time) are included. Among full-time workers specifically, it is solidly median. By any reasonable definition, $30/hour is a middle-class wage in the United States — but whether it feels that way depends enormously on where you live and your household structure.

Federal Tax on $62,400 in 2026: Step by Step

At $62,400, you are in slightly higher tax territory than a $25/hour worker. Here is how federal taxes are calculated:

Taxable Income After Standard Deduction

$62,400 gross − $15,000 standard deduction (2026, single filer) = $47,400 taxable income

Federal Income Tax Calculation

Your $47,400 taxable income falls across two brackets for single filers in 2026. The 22% bracket begins at $48,476 of taxable income, so you are just below that threshold:

Bracket Rate Income in Bracket Tax Owed
10%10%$0 – $11,925$1,193
12%12%$11,926 – $47,400$4,257
Total Federal Income Tax $5,450

Your effective federal income tax rate is 8.7% ($5,450 ÷ $62,400). Your marginal rate is 12%, meaning any additional income up to $48,476 taxable income ($63,476 gross) is taxed at 12%. Income beyond that threshold moves into the 22% bracket.

This is a useful planning detail: if you receive a bonus or overtime that pushes your gross income above ~$63,476, the additional amount over that threshold faces 22% federal tax instead of 12% — a meaningful jump. Structuring pre-tax 401(k) contributions can keep more of your income in the 12% bracket.

FICA: Social Security and Medicare

FICA applies to 100% of your gross wages, with no standard deduction:

  • Social Security: 6.2% × $62,400 = $3,869 (well under the $176,100 wage cap)
  • Medicare: 1.45% × $62,400 = $905
  • Total FICA: $4,774

Tax Planning at $62,400: Strategies Worth Using

At $62,400, you are in a position where strategic pre-tax contributions can meaningfully reduce your federal tax bill while accelerating retirement savings. Here are the most impactful moves:

Traditional 401(k) — Keep Income Out of the 22% Bracket

Your taxable income of $47,400 puts you just $1,076 below the 22% bracket threshold ($48,476 taxable income). Any bonus, overtime, or raise that pushes you over this line means that additional income is taxed at 22% instead of 12% — a 10-percentage-point jump. A 401(k) contribution of even $2,000–$3,000 keeps you safely in the 12% bracket and saves approximately $200–$300 in federal taxes. The 2026 employee 401(k) limit is $24,500.

Maxing out 401(k) contributions at $24,500 would drop your taxable income to $22,900 — saving approximately $2,940 in federal income tax while accelerating wealth-building through pre-tax investment growth. That is a powerful double return on a single financial decision.

Roth vs. Traditional 401(k) Decision

At $62,400 gross in the 12% bracket, the traditional vs. Roth 401(k) decision matters. The conventional guidance is: if you expect to be in a higher bracket in retirement, choose Roth (pay taxes now at 12%); if you expect a lower bracket in retirement, choose traditional (defer taxes). For a $30/hour worker who expects income to grow significantly — particularly those in skilled trades or healthcare with clear advancement paths — Roth contributions lock in the current 12% rate permanently on those funds.

HSA Contributions

If your employer offers a high-deductible health plan (HDHP), maximizing HSA contributions ($4,400 for self-only coverage in 2026) reduces taxable income and saves approximately $528 in federal income tax. Unlike FSAs, HSA balances roll over indefinitely and can be invested — making them a powerful secondary retirement account for healthcare costs.

Childcare Tax Credit

For workers with children, the Child and Dependent Care Credit allows up to $3,000 in childcare expenses for one child (or $6,000 for two+) to be credited directly against tax owed — not just deducted. At $62,400 income, you qualify for a 20% credit rate, generating up to $600–$1,200 in direct tax reduction. Frequently Asked Questions

How much is $30 an hour annually?

$30 an hour equals $62,400 per year before taxes, based on a standard 40-hour workweek for 52 weeks (2,080 hours). After federal income tax and FICA, a single filer in a no-income-tax state keeps approximately $52,176. California residents keep approximately $46,000 after federal and state taxes combined.

Is $30 an hour a good wage?

$30/hour ($62,400/year) is above the U.S. median individual income per BLS Current Population Survey data and meets MIT's Living Wage Calculator threshold for a single adult in most U.S. states. It qualifies as genuinely middle-class income nationally, though high-cost metros like San Francisco and DC require $35–$40+/hour for equivalent purchasing power.

How much is $30 an hour monthly?

$30 an hour equals $5,200 per month gross ($62,400 ÷ 12). After federal and state taxes, monthly take-home is approximately $3,832 in California to $4,348 in Texas. The $516/month difference between those states is purely a function of state income tax policy, not wages or cost of work.

How much federal tax do you pay on $62,400?

A single filer earning $62,400 in 2026 owes approximately $5,450 in federal income tax after the $15,000 standard deduction (effective rate: 8.7%, marginal rate: 12%). Add $4,774 in FICA taxes for a total federal burden of $10,224, leaving $52,176 before state taxes.

What is $30 an hour as a salary?

$30 an hour equates to a $62,400 annual salary at standard full-time hours (40 hrs/week × 52 weeks = 2,080 hours). This sits just above the BLS-reported median annual earnings for full-time workers of approximately $60,580 in Q4 2025, placing a $30/hour worker at approximately the 55th–60th percentile of U.S. income earners.

Can you live comfortably on $30 an hour?

Yes, for a single adult in most U.S. cities. At $52,176 net annually in Texas, a single person can afford rent, a car, and meaningful retirement contributions in cities like Austin, Dallas, or Nashville. The main exceptions are San Francisco, Manhattan, and a handful of other coastal metros where $30/hour still falls below MIT's single-adult living wage estimate.

How much is $30 an hour biweekly?

$30 an hour generates a gross biweekly paycheck of $2,400 (80 hours × $30). After federal income tax and FICA, your biweekly take-home is approximately $1,770–$2,008 depending on your state, filing status, and pre-tax deductions like 401(k) contributions or health insurance premiums.
