Minimum Wage by State 2026: Complete Guide

Updated March 26, 2026  •  18 min read  •  Sources: DOL, BLS, MIT, NELP, CBO

This article was originally published on salario.io. Visit the original for interactive calculators and tools.

The federal minimum wage has been frozen at $7.25 since 2009, but the state and local landscape has shifted dramatically. This guide covers every state's exact 2026 minimum wage, tipped employee cash wage rates, city-level ordinances, living wage gaps (MIT data updated February 15, 2026), CPI-indexed states, industry-specific wage floors, scheduled upcoming increases, and what economic research says about effects on employment and poverty.

1. Federal Minimum Wage in 2026

The federal minimum wage stands at $7.25 per hour in 2026 — set on July 24, 2009. As of March 2026, this 17-year stretch without an increase is the longest in the history of the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), which established the federal wage floor in 1938 at $0.25 per hour. Congress has approved 22 increases over that span.

In real purchasing-power terms, the federal minimum wage has lost approximately 30% of its value since 2009. The 1968 rate of $1.60 represented the historical peak; in 2026 dollars, that equals roughly $14.82 per hour — more than double the current nominal rate.

At $7.25 for a 40-hour week, 52 weeks, a full-time worker earns $15,080 gross per year — below the 2026 federal poverty guideline of $15,650 for a single-person household. The FLSA requires employers to pay whichever is higher — federal or state rate. No federal increase is currently scheduled.

States at the Federal Minimum ($7.25)

Eighteen states default to the federal rate: Alabama, Georgia, Idaho, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, New Hampshire, North Carolina, North Dakota, Oklahoma, Pennsylvania, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Wisconsin, and Wyoming. Georgia ($5.15) and Wyoming ($5.15) have nominal state minimums below $7.25, but most employees are protected by the higher federal rate under the FLSA. Five states — Alabama, Louisiana, Mississippi, South Carolina, and Tennessee — have no state minimum wage statute at all.

3. Tipped Employee Minimum Wages by State (2026)

The federal tipped minimum wage is $2.13 per hour, unchanged since 1991. Employers may claim a tip credit up to $5.12/hr, but total compensation (cash wage + tips) must reach $7.25/hr every pay period. If tips are insufficient, the employer must cover the gap — a legal requirement that enforcement data shows is routinely violated.

4. City and Local Minimum Wages (2026)

Dozens of cities have enacted rates above their state floor. The UC Berkeley Labor Center tracks more than 60 active U.S. city/county ordinances.

Seattle, Washington — $21.30/hr

Effective January 1, 2026, Seattle's minimum wage is $21.30 per hour for all employers, regardless of size (the large/small split ended January 1, 2025). Annual gross earnings at this rate: $44,304. Source: Seattle Office of Labor Standards.

New York City — $17.00/hr

NYC, Long Island, and Westchester County moved to $17.00/hr on January 1, 2026 (upstate NY: $16.00). Starting 2027, rates adjust annually to CPI-W Northeast Region. NYC food service workers: cash minimum $11.35 + up to $5.65 tip credit. NYC service employees: cash minimum $14.15 + up to $2.85 tip credit. Source: NY Department of Labor.

Denver, Colorado — $19.29/hr

Denver's citywide minimum is $19.29/hr effective January 1, 2026, CPI-indexed annually. Tipped food and beverage workers in Denver: $16.27/hr minimum cash wage. Applies to employees performing at least 4 hours/week within city limits. Source: City and County of Denver, Auditor's Office.

5. States With Automatic CPI Indexing

Fourteen states and D.C. have adopted automatic inflation-adjustment mechanisms. Eight states with above-federal minimums lack indexing and will not raise wages in 2026: Arkansas, Delaware, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, Nevada, New Mexico, and West Virginia. (Source: NELP, "Raises from Coast to Coast in 2026," December 2025)

  • Washington: CPI-W indexed since 2001; 2026 rate ($17.13) set by August 2025 CPI data.
  • Oregon: Three-tier CPI system (Portland metro / standard / nonurban); adjusted July 1 annually.
  • Colorado: CPI-indexed statewide ($15.16); Denver has a separate local CPI ordinance ($19.29).
  • Arizona: CPI-indexed since Prop 206 (2016); 2026 rate $15.15.
  • Montana: CPI-indexed; 2026 rate $10.85.
  • Ohio: CPI-indexed (3.5% cap); 2026 rate $11.00.
  • Maine: CPI-indexed since 2016 ballot measure; 2026 rate $15.10.
  • Minnesota: CPI-indexed (2.5% cap); 2026 rate $11.41.
  • New Jersey: CPI-indexed after reaching $15.00; 2026 rate $15.92 (large employers).
  • South Dakota: CPI-indexed since 2014 ballot measure; 2026 rate $11.85.
  • Vermont: CPI-indexed (5% cap); 2026 rate $14.42.
  • Nebraska: Begins CPI indexing from $15.00 per 2022 ballot measure.
  • Florida: $15.00 reached September 30, 2026; CPI indexing begins thereafter.
  • New York: Begins annual CPI-W Northeast Region indexing January 1, 2027.
  • D.C.: CPI-indexed; 2026 rate $17.95.

If the federal minimum had been indexed to CPI since 1968 (peak real value), it would be approximately $14.82/hr in 2026 — more than double the current $7.25.

6. Industry-Specific Minimum Wages

Fast Food Workers — California AB 1228 ($20.00/hr)

Signed September 28, 2023, effective April 1, 2024. Applies to fast food chain employees at restaurants with 60 or more locations nationwide. Sets a minimum of $20.00/hr, adjusted annually by the Fast Food Council. This is $3.10 above California's general 2026 minimum of $16.90.

Healthcare Workers — California SB 525 ($25.00/hr)

SB 525 created a phased minimum wage for covered healthcare facilities. Large health systems (10,000+ FTE employees) reach $25.00/hr on June 1, 2026. Applies broadly to all employees at covered facilities — nurses, janitors, food service workers, security personnel. Smaller facilities reach $25/hr at later dates per the law's schedule.

App-Based and Gig Workers

  • New York City: App-based food delivery workers earn a minimum of ~$17.96/hr of active delivery time under 2023 city ordinance (NYC DCWP).
  • Seattle: The Pay Up ordinance requires rideshare and delivery drivers to earn at least $1.53/min or $0.31/mile while on trip.
  • Colorado: 2023 state legislation sets minimum per-mile/per-minute rates for app-based delivery statewide.

9. Minimum Wage and Poverty Rates

The relationship between minimum wage increases and poverty reduction is real but complicated by household composition, geographic variation in cost of living, and benefits-cliff interactions with public assistance programs.

  • A 2025 review in Social Science Research found a 10% minimum wage increase is associated with a 1.5–5% reduction in poverty rates among affected households.
  • The CBO's 2021 analysis of a $15 federal minimum projected lifting 900,000 workers out of poverty while reducing employment by a median estimate of 1.4 million workers.
  • Many minimum wage earners are not in poverty-level households (e.g., teenagers in middle-income families), while many poor adults work in salaried or informal jobs not covered by minimum wage law — both dynamics limit the poverty-reduction effect of wage floors.
  • The "benefits cliff" means some workers face reduced eligibility for SNAP, Medicaid, or housing subsidies when wages rise, sometimes partially offsetting net income gains.

10. Economic Research on Minimum Wage Effects

Traditional View: Modest Disemployment

David Neumark and William Wascher's meta-analysis found median own-wage elasticities of −0.1 to −0.2: a 10% minimum wage increase reduces employment of affected workers by 1–2%. The CBO uses broadly consistent assumptions, yielding the 1.4 million job-loss estimate for a $15 federal minimum.

Monopsony and Search-and-Matching View: Little or No Disemployment

Cengiz, Dube, Lindner & Zipperer (2019, Quarterly Journal of Economics) studied 138 state-level minimum wage events (1984–2016) and found no significant reduction in low-wage job counts. The interpretation: monopsony power in low-wage labor markets means employers pay below the competitive wage; a minimum wage corrects this, allowing simultaneous wage increases and employment stability. Separately, Dube, Giuliano & Leonard found minimum wages substantially reduce turnover and separation rates — an efficiency gain that partially offsets wage-bill increases for employers.

High-Minimum Natural Experiments

  • Seattle ($13–$15, 2014–2017): University of Washington found hours reductions during the steepest phase; UC Berkeley found minimal restaurant employment loss versus border controls. A 2024 NBER paper (Jardim et al.) found net-neutral employment effects with income gains for affected workers when job transitions are fully accounted for.
  • California AB 1228 ($20 fast food, April 2024): UC Riverside researchers found approximately 2.5–3.5% meal price increases and 0.5–1.5% fast food employment reduction in the first year — smaller than critics predicted, larger than proponents claimed.

Current Consensus

Moderate minimum wage increases (up to roughly 50–60% of local median wage) have small or negligible negative employment effects in most labor markets, meaningfully reduce income inequality, and modestly reduce poverty. Very large, rapid increases in concentrated low-wage markets carry higher disemployment risk. The federal $7.25 is approximately 25–30% of the national median hourly wage — far below any international threshold researchers associate with significant adverse effects.

11. State Preemption Laws: Blocking Local Minimum Wages

Nearly 30 states have enacted preemption laws prohibiting cities, counties, and other local governments from setting minimum wages above the state floor. According to NELP, state wage preemption costs an estimated 350,000 workers approximately $4,000 per year each in foregone wages.

States With Active Minimum Wage Preemption

Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Indiana, Iowa, Kansas, Louisiana, Michigan, Mississippi, Missouri (historically; superseded by 2024 ballot measure statewide), North Carolina, Oklahoma, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Wisconsin, and Wyoming. Several others have implicit preemption via Dillon's Rule.

Notable Cases

  • Alabama (2016): Preemption law passed two days before Birmingham's $10.10 ordinance was to take effect, nullifying the raise for ~40,000 workers.
  • Texas (2023): Sweeping preemption covers minimum wage, heat standards, scheduling, and more. Currently under legal challenge; has chilled local action in Austin, Houston, and San Antonio.
  • Florida: State Statute §218.077 prohibits local minimum wages. The Third DCA upheld this in 2017, invalidating Miami Beach's local ordinance. Florida's statewide rate is protected by a 2020 constitutional amendment, which preemption cannot override.

States Permitting Local Ordinances

California, Colorado, Illinois, Maryland, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, Oregon, and Washington all allow city/county minimum wages above the state floor. The UC Berkeley Labor Center documents more than 60 active U.S. local ordinances as of 2026.
