Minimum Wage by State 2026: Complete Guide

The federal minimum wage has been stuck at $7.25 since 2009, but the landscape at the state and city level has transformed dramatically. This guide covers every state's 2026 minimum wage, tipped employee rules, city-level premiums, and how minimum wage stacks up against the actual cost of living.

March 7, 2026 14 min read

Federal Minimum Wage in 2026

The federal minimum wage stands at $7.25 per hour in 2026, a rate that has not changed since July 24, 2009. This is the longest period without a federal minimum wage increase since the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) was enacted in 1938. Adjusted for inflation, the federal minimum wage has lost approximately 30% of its purchasing power since its last increase.

At $7.25 per hour working 40 hours per week for 52 weeks, a full-time worker earns $15,080 per year before taxes. That places a single worker below the federal poverty line of $15,650 for a one-person household. For a family of four, the poverty threshold is $32,470, meaning the federal minimum wage covers less than half of what is needed.

Despite multiple legislative attempts, including the Raise the Wage Act that would have gradually increased the minimum to $15 by 2025, no federal increase has passed. The result is a patchwork system where workers doing the same job can earn dramatically different wages depending on which state or city they work in.

The FLSA requires employers to pay the higher of the federal or state minimum wage. This means the $7.25 federal rate only effectively applies in states that have not set their own higher minimum. Tipped Employee Minimum Wages

The federal tipped minimum wage is $2.13 per hour for employees who regularly receive more than $30 per month in tips. This allows employers to take a "tip credit" of up to $5.12 per hour, meaning they pay the reduced cash wage as long as the employee's tips bring total hourly compensation to at least $7.25.

If an employee's tips do not bring them up to the full minimum wage in any pay period, the employer is legally required to make up the difference. However, compliance with this requirement is notoriously uneven, and wage theft in tipped industries remains one of the most common labor violations.

States That Require Full Minimum Wage for Tipped Workers

Minimum Wage vs Living Wage

The "living wage" represents the hourly rate a full-time worker needs to cover basic necessities (housing, food, healthcare, transportation, childcare, and taxes) without public assistance. According to MIT's Living Wage Calculator, the national average living wage for a single adult with no children is approximately $21.54 per hour in 2026.

Even the highest state minimum wages fall short of the living wage in most areas. Here is how the gap looks in selected states:

Automatic Adjustment Mechanisms

Several states have implemented automatic minimum wage adjustments tied to inflation or the Consumer Price Index (CPI), eliminating the need for new legislation each time an increase is warranted. These indexed states include:

  • Washington: Indexed to CPI-W (Consumer Price Index for Urban Wage Earners) since 2001
  • Oregon: Indexed to CPI, with three tiers (standard, Portland metro, nonurban)
  • Colorado: Indexed to CPI since 2007, with Denver having a separate local index
  • Montana: Indexed to CPI since 2007
  • Ohio: Indexed to CPI since 2007
  • Arizona: Indexed to CPI since 2020
  • Maine: Indexed to CPI since 2020
  • California: Set to begin CPI indexing after reaching $16.50
  • New Jersey: Indexed to CPI since reaching $15.00
  • Florida: $1/year increases until $15, then CPI-indexed

Indexing ensures the minimum wage maintains its purchasing power without requiring political action each year. States without indexing often see their minimum wages erode in real value between legislative increases, sometimes going decades without adjustment.

If the federal minimum wage had been indexed to CPI since 1968 (when it reached its peak purchasing power of $1.60, equivalent to about $14.50 today), it would be approximately $24 per hour in 2026 dollars. That is more than triple the current $7.25 federal rate.

Industry-Specific Minimum Wages

Some states and cities have enacted higher minimum wages for specific industries, recognizing that certain sectors have unique characteristics that warrant different wage floors:

Fast Food and Restaurant Workers

California's AB 1228 established a $20 per hour minimum wage for fast food restaurant employees at chains with 60 or more locations nationwide, effective April 2024 and adjusted annually. New York has considered similar industry-specific minimums. This is $3.50 higher than the general California minimum wage, creating a two-tier system.

Healthcare Workers

California also enacted SB 525, establishing minimum wages for healthcare workers that will reach $25 per hour by 2026 for large health systems and hospitals. This applies to a broad range of healthcare facility employees, from nurses to janitors working in covered facilities.

Gig Workers and App-Based Drivers

Several cities have enacted minimum earnings standards for gig workers. New York City requires a minimum of approximately $17.96 per hour of active work time for app-based food delivery workers, while Seattle has a similar ordinance covering rideshare and delivery drivers. These laws attempt to ensure gig workers earn at least the equivalent of minimum wage after accounting for expenses.
