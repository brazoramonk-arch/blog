How Much Is $25 an Hour Annually? Full Salary Breakdown

$25 an hour is $52,000 per year before taxes — but your real take-home pay depends heavily on where you live. A Texas worker earning $25/hour keeps roughly $4,300 more per year than a California counterpart doing the exact same job. Here is the complete 2026 breakdown: taxes, monthly income, living wage benchmarks, and what jobs pay $25/hour.

April 21, 2026 12 min read

Key Takeaways

  • $25/hour × 2,080 hours = $52,000 gross per year
  • After federal income tax and FICA, a single filer takes home roughly $39,500–$43,820 depending on state
  • No-income-tax states (Texas, Florida, Nevada) deliver ~$4,300 more annually than California at this wage
  • $52,000 exceeds the MIT Living Wage for a single adult in most mid-cost states, but not in major metros
  • Monthly gross is $4,333; biweekly gross paycheck is $2,000

How $25/Hour Becomes $52,000 a Year

The math behind the annual figure is straightforward, but understanding the assumptions matters for accuracy:

$25 × 2,080 hours = $52,000/year

(40 hours/week × 52 weeks = 2,080 standard annual hours)

This assumes full-time employment without unpaid time off. The Bureau of Labor Statistics uses this 2,080-hour baseline in all hourly-to-annual wage conversions, as does the American Payroll Association (APA). If you work fewer hours, substitute your actual weekly hours:

  • 30 hours/week: $25 × 30 × 52 = $39,000/year
  • 35 hours/week: $25 × 35 × 52 = $45,500/year
  • 40 hours/week: $25 × 40 × 52 = $52,000/year
  • 45 hours/week (5 hrs OT at 1.5x): $52,000 + ($37.50 × 5 × 52) = $61,750/year

Federal Taxes on $52,000 in 2026

At $52,000 gross income, your federal tax liability flows through three steps: the standard deduction reduces your taxable income, progressive brackets determine your income tax, and FICA taxes apply to your full gross wage regardless of deductions.

Step 1: Standard Deduction

The 2026 standard deduction for a single filer is $15,000 (IRS inflation-adjusted from $14,600 in 2025). Your taxable income:

$52,000 gross − $15,000 standard deduction = $37,000 taxable income

Step 2: Federal Income Tax Brackets

Your $37,000 taxable income falls across two federal brackets for single filers in 2026:

Bracket Rate Income in Bracket Tax Owed

12%12%$11,926 – $37,000$3,009

Your effective federal income tax rate is 8.1% ($4,202 ÷ $52,000). You are in the 12% marginal bracket, but your actual rate is significantly lower because the 10% bracket absorbs the first $11,925 of taxable income.

Step 3: FICA Taxes

FICA taxes apply to your full gross wage — there is no standard deduction applied. Per IRS rates for 2026:

  • Social Security: 6.2% on wages up to $176,100 = $3,224
  • Medicare: 1.45% on all wages = $754
  • Total FICA: $3,978

Is $25 an Hour a Good Wage in 2026?

The answer depends on three factors: your household size, your location, and how you define "good." Let's run it through objective benchmarks.

Against the National Median

The Bureau of Labor Statistics' Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) data pegs the national median hourly wage at approximately $23.61 for all occupations. At $25/hour, you are earning above the median — roughly at the 55th–60th percentile of all wage earners. By this measure, $25/hour is a solid, above-average wage, though not exceptional.

The BLS Current Population Survey puts median annual earnings for full-time workers at approximately $60,580 (Q4 2025). At $52,000, you are earning about 86% of the median full-time wage — below median on an annual basis, but this partly reflects the difference between hourly and salaried roles and the distribution of highly-paid salaried professionals at the top.

Against MIT Living Wage Estimates

$25/hour is a genuinely livable wage in most of the South, Midwest, and interior regions of the country. It becomes strained in major coastal metros. For families, the math gets harder: supporting a spouse and two children on a single $25/hour income is not feasible in any major metropolitan area, where childcare alone often exceeds $20,000 per year.

Tax Reduction Strategies at $52,000

At $52,000 gross, you have several meaningful tax reduction options that are specifically valuable at this income level:

Traditional 401(k) Contributions

Contributing to a traditional 401(k) reduces your taxable income directly. The 2026 employee contribution limit is $24,500. If you contribute $5,000 annually, your taxable income drops from $37,000 to $32,000 — saving $600 in federal income tax at your 12% marginal rate. A $10,000 contribution saves $1,200. The compounding growth on pre-tax retirement contributions also makes this one of the highest-ROI financial decisions available to workers in this income range.

Health Savings Account (HSA)

If your employer offers a high-deductible health plan, HSA contributions reduce your taxable income. The 2026 HSA limit for self-only coverage is $4,400. Contributing the maximum reduces federal income tax by approximately $528 at the 12% rate — and HSA funds can be invested and withdrawn tax-free for qualified medical expenses.

Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

Single filers without children do not qualify for EITC at $52,000 (the income cutoff is around $18,591 for childless single filers). However, single parents with children can qualify at this income level. A single parent with one child can receive up to $4,213 in EITC for 2026; with two children, up to $6,960. This is one of the most powerful credits available to lower-to-middle income workers with dependents — but it requires an active filing to claim.

Student Loan Interest Deduction

If you are paying off student loans, up to $2,500 in student loan interest is deductible above-the-line (you can take it even if you use the standard deduction). The deduction phases out between $75,000–$90,000 for single filers, so at $52,000 you are fully eligible. At your marginal rate, a $2,500 deduction saves approximately $300 in federal income tax. Frequently Asked Questions

How much is $25 an hour annually?

$25 an hour equals $52,000 per year before taxes, based on a standard 2,080-hour full-time work year (40 hours/week × 52 weeks). After federal income tax and FICA, a single filer in a no-tax state keeps about $43,820; California residents take home approximately $39,500 after state taxes.

Is $25 an hour a good wage?

$25/hour ($52,000/year) is above the U.S. median hourly wage of ~$23.61 per BLS OEWS data. It exceeds MIT's Living Wage threshold for a single adult without children in most low- and mid-cost states, but falls short in major coastal cities where living costs require $30–$40+/hour for a comfortable single-person lifestyle.

How much is $25 an hour monthly?

$25 an hour equals $4,333 per month gross. After federal and state taxes, expect a monthly take-home of $3,291–$3,652 depending on your state. Texas and Florida workers net approximately $360 more per month than California workers at this income level, purely due to the absence of state income tax.

How much federal tax do you pay on $52,000?

A single filer earning $52,000 in 2026 owes $4,202 in federal income tax after the $15,000 standard deduction (effective rate: 8.1%). Add $3,978 in FICA (Social Security $3,224 + Medicare $754) for a combined federal burden of approximately $8,180, leaving $43,820 before any state income tax.

How much is $25 an hour biweekly?

$25 an hour generates a gross biweekly paycheck of $2,000 (80 hours × $25). After federal and FICA taxes, your biweekly take-home is approximately $1,520–$1,686 depending on your state, filing status, and any pre-tax benefit deductions like 401(k) or health insurance premiums.

Can you live on $25 an hour?

Yes, comfortably in most mid-cost cities. With $3,652/month net in Texas, a single adult can afford a one-bedroom apartment, own a car, and still save meaningfully in cities like Columbus, San Antonio, or Raleigh. In high-cost metros like San Francisco or New York, $52,000 annual gross falls well below the living wage.

What jobs pay $25 an hour?

Common $25/hour jobs include licensed practical nurses ($25.40 BLS median), paralegals ($26.90), surgical technologists ($25.74), electrician apprentices, and insurance claims adjusters ($28.89). Entry-level roles in government, healthcare administration, and technical support also frequently land in this pay range.
