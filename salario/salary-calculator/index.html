The Core Salary Conversion Formulas

Every salary conversion starts from a common reference point: the standard work year of 2,080 hours (40 hours per week multiplied by 52 weeks). This figure is the foundation used by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the IRS, and virtually every payroll system in the United States. While individual work schedules vary, the 2,080-hour benchmark gives you consistent, comparable numbers across roles and pay structures.

Here are the essential conversion formulas compensation analysts and HR professionals use daily:

Based on 2,080 annual hours (40 hrs/week × 52 weeks). Biweekly = annual ÷ 26. Monthly = annual ÷ 12.

To convert an annual salary to an hourly rate, divide by 2,080. A $75,000 salary divided by 2,080 equals $36.06 per hour. This calculation is essential when comparing salaried and hourly job offers — a position offering $70,000 per year is effectively offering $33.65 per hour, which may be less than an hourly role paying $36 per hour, even though the annual number sounds larger.

Salary Ranges by State: Where You Live Matters

The same job title can command wildly different compensation depending on state. According to BLS state occupational wage data, a software developer's median salary ranges from $96,000 in Mississippi to $162,000 in Washington state. These differences reflect local labor market conditions, cost of living, and concentration of high-paying industries.

However, nominal salary differences do not tell the whole story. A $110,000 salary in Austin, Texas delivers more purchasing power than a $145,000 salary in San Francisco after accounting for housing, state income tax (zero in Texas vs. up to 13.3% in California), and overall cost of living differences documented by the Council for Community and Economic Research (C2ER).

2026 Federal Tax Brackets: How Your Salary Is Taxed

A salary calculator must correctly apply progressive tax brackets to estimate take-home pay. The 2026 federal income tax brackets (inflation-adjusted by the IRS under TCJA provisions) apply to taxable income — your gross salary minus the standard deduction and pre-tax deductions like 401(k) contributions.

2026 Federal Income Tax Brackets — Single Filers

Taxable Income Marginal Rate $0 – $11,92510% $11,926 – $48,47512% $48,476 – $103,35022% $103,351 – $197,30024% $197,301 – $250,52532% $250,526 – $626,35035% Over $626,35037%

Standard deduction for single filers: $15,000 in 2026. Taxable income = gross income − standard deduction − pre-tax deductions.

The critical concept here is that marginal rates only apply to income within each bracket — not to all of your income. A single filer earning $75,000 does not pay 22% on the entire $75,000. After the $15,000 standard deduction, taxable income is $60,000. They pay 10% on the first $11,925, 12% on income from $11,926 to $48,475, and 22% on income from $48,476 to $60,000. The effective (average) federal rate is approximately 13.7%, not 22%.

This distinction matters enormously when evaluating raises, bonuses, or additional income. Your marginal rate tells you how much of the next dollar of income goes to taxes. Your effective rate tells you what percentage of your total income is paid in federal income taxes. Both figures are relevant for different decisions.

Pre-Tax Deductions: The Most Powerful Salary Lever

Most salary calculators allow you to enter pre-tax deductions, and this is where smart employees can significantly boost their take-home pay by reducing taxable income. According to IRS data analyzed by ADP, workers who maximize pre-tax deductions can effectively reduce their marginal federal tax rate by 2–6 percentage points, representing thousands of dollars in annual tax savings.

The major pre-tax deductions for 2026 include:

  • 401(k) contributions: Up to $23,500 ($31,000 if age 50+) reduces federal and state taxable income. An employee contributing $10,000 annually in the 22% bracket saves $2,200 in federal income tax alone.
  • Health insurance premiums: Employer-sponsored health insurance paid through a Section 125 cafeteria plan reduces both income tax and FICA. Glassdoor data suggests the average employer-sponsored health plan costs employees $1,800–$4,500 per year after employer contribution.
  • HSA contributions: Up to $4,300 for individual coverage ($8,550 for family coverage) in 2026, per IRS Publication 969. HSA contributions reduce FICA as well as income tax — a triple tax advantage.
  • FSA contributions: Up to $3,300 for healthcare FSAs; dependent care FSAs allow up to $5,000 per household.
  • Commuter benefits: Up to $325/month for transit and parking under IRC Section 132.

Consider a single filer earning $85,000 who contributes $10,000 to a 401(k) and $4,300 to an HSA. Their taxable income drops to $70,700 after the standard deduction — placing more income in the 12% bracket instead of the 22% bracket. The federal income tax saving is approximately $1,540 per year compared to taking no pre-tax deductions.

Salary Growth and Career Benchmarks

Using a salary calculator effectively requires understanding not just your current pay but your compensation trajectory. Payscale's 2026 Compensation Best Practices Report found that the median salary increase for a job change was 14.8%, compared to just 3.9% for staying at the same employer. ADP Research Institute's real-time payroll data consistently shows a 10-percentage-point wage premium for job-switchers.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics Employment Cost Index (ECI) shows wages and salaries rising 4.1% year-over-year in the private sector as of late 2025. However, this average masks significant dispersion — technology, healthcare, and financial services saw increases of 5–8%, while retail and food service remained closer to 3%.

For salary negotiation purposes, understanding the difference between your current market value and your current pay — the compensation gap — is critical. Glassdoor's Know Your Worth data suggests the average worker is paid 4-7% below their market value when they accept a job offer without negotiating, representing a $3,000–$7,000 annual opportunity cost on a $75,000 salary.

Salary vs. Total Compensation: The Complete Picture

Salary calculators convert your base pay across time periods and estimate taxes, but the full value of a compensation package extends well beyond base salary. According to the BLS Employer Costs for Employee Compensation (ECEC) survey, employer costs for benefits average 31.9% of total compensation for civilian workers — meaning a $70,000 salary worker typically receives an additional $31,700 in benefit costs from their employer.

Key non-salary components to quantify include:

  • Employer 401(k) match: A 4% match on a $70,000 salary is $2,800/year in additional compensation.
  • Health insurance: Employer contributions to employer-sponsored plans average $7,000–$14,000 per year for family coverage, per KFF Employer Health Benefits Survey data.
  • Equity (RSUs/options): At tech companies, equity grants can add 20–100%+ to base salary in total annual value.
  • PTO and paid holidays: Three weeks of PTO plus 10 paid holidays on a $70,000 salary is worth approximately $9,360 in additional paid time.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I convert an hourly wage to an annual salary?

Multiply your hourly rate by 2,080 (40 hours × 52 weeks). So $25/hr = $52,000/year. For overtime, add those hours at 1.5× your base rate separately. Our salary calculator handles all pay period conversions automatically.

What is a good salary in the United States in 2026?

The BLS median full-time wage is approximately $62,296. A salary 30–50% above median for your occupation and region is generally strong. Context matters enormously: $70,000 is comfortable in Kansas City but tight in San Francisco. Glassdoor places the average U.S. base salary around $67,000.

How much of my salary is taken out for taxes?

For a single filer earning $60,000, expect FICA (7.65%) plus federal income tax (effective ~10–12%) plus state tax (0–10%). Most middle-income workers take home 70–80 cents per gross dollar. Pre-tax deductions reduce your tax burden further.

What is the difference between gross salary and net salary?

Gross salary is your pre-deduction pay (what appears on your offer letter). Net salary is what hits your bank account after federal tax, state tax, FICA, and voluntary deductions. On a $75,000 gross salary, net pay typically ranges from $52,000–$58,000 depending on state, filing status, and benefit elections.

How do I calculate my monthly salary from annual?

Divide annual by 12 for monthly gross. A $72,000 salary = $6,000/month. For biweekly (26 periods): divide by 26 = $2,769.23/paycheck. Semimonthly (24 periods): divide by 24 = $3,000/paycheck. Note biweekly workers receive 3 paychecks in two months per year.

Do salary calculators account for state income taxes?

Quality calculators like Salario's include all 50 state tax rates. Nine states have no income tax. Others range from 3% flat rates to 13%+ in California. State tax can shift annual net pay by $6,000–$10,000 for a $100,000 earner comparing no-tax states to high-tax states.

How accurate are online salary calculators?

Well-designed calculators using current IRS tables and SSA wage bases are accurate to within 1–3%. Accuracy depends on correct inputs for W-4 elections, pre-tax deductions, and filing status. For precise obligations, consult a CPA or use the IRS Tax Withholding Estimator.
