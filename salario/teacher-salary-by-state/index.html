The Number That Changes Everything: $47,380

That is the salary gap between a public school teacher in California ($101,084) and one in Mississippi ($53,704). Both teachers hold state teaching licenses, likely have bachelor's or master's degrees, and spend their days managing classrooms, writing curriculum, and shaping the next generation. Yet one earns nearly twice the other.

This geographic lottery drives teacher migration, vacancy rates, and — increasingly — legislative urgency. According to FutureEd's 2026 legislative tracker, 59 teacher pay bills have been filed across 19 states in 2026 alone, more than any year in recent memory. Mississippi leads with 16 bills. Indiana is proposing to raise the minimum from $40,000 to $60,000. North Carolina has a bill on the floor for a 10% across-the-board increase.

The national average teacher salary for 2024-25 is $74,177 per the National Education Association — up 3% from $72,030 in 2023-24. But the headline number obscures a stark reality: inflation-adjusted real wages for teachers have fallen 5.1% since 2015-16, according to NEA's own analysis. Teachers are earning more dollars that buy less.

This guide walks through every state, examines the cost-of-living-adjusted rankings that reveal which states actually offer the best purchasing power, and tracks the 2026 legislative changes that could reshape the map.

The Cost-of-Living Flip: Why Iowa Beats New York for Real Teacher Pay

The nominal salary table is a misleading proxy for teacher wellbeing. A teacher earning $95,615 in New York City — where the median one-bedroom apartment rents for over $3,200 per month — has dramatically less disposable income than a teacher earning $62,399 in Iowa, where the median home costs $195,000 and a one-bedroom apartment runs $850.

When you apply the Bureau of Economic Analysis Regional Price Parities, which measure the actual cost of a comparable basket of goods and services in each state, the rankings shift significantly:

Hawaii is a particularly striking example. Nominal salaries look respectable at $74,222 (rank 14), but Hawaii has the highest cost of living in the nation — a COL-adjusted salary equivalent of around $56,341 drops Hawaiian teachers to near the bottom in real purchasing power. This is why Hawaii consistently struggles with teacher retention despite above-average nominal pay.

New Mexico tells the opposite story. After deliberate legislative investment (including a 10% salary increase since 2015-16 and a $5,000 minimum increase enacted in 2025), New Mexico has jumped from 39th to among the top 10 in COL-adjusted teacher pay. The state prioritized teacher compensation as a workforce retention strategy, and the data shows it working.

The Teacher Pay Gap: 27 Cents Short on Every Dollar

Perhaps the most striking data point in teacher compensation research is not the state-by-state range but the comparison to other professions. The Economic Policy Institute and the Center for Economic and Policy Research analyzed 2023 BLS data and found that teachers earn approximately 27% less than other workers with similar education levels, experience, and occupational demands. This is the largest teacher pay penalty recorded since tracking began in the 1970s.

In 1996, teachers earned 93.9 cents for every dollar earned by comparable professionals. By 2023, that fell to 73.4 cents on the dollar. The male teacher pay penalty is particularly severe at 36%, partly because high-earning male-dominated fields like finance and technology have seen faster compensation growth.

One counterweight: teacher benefits. Teachers in most states receive defined-benefit pension plans, employer-subsidized health insurance (88% of state and local government workers have access to employer health coverage per BLS), and summers that allow supplemental income or personal time. The Teacher Shortage and Its Salary Connection

The teacher shortage is structural, not temporary. According to the Learning Policy Institute's 2025 analysis, approximately 365,967 teachers across 48 states are employed in positions for which they are not fully certified. Thirty-one states reported 45,582 outright unfilled positions as of June 2025. That means roughly one in eight teaching positions is either vacant or filled by an unqualified candidate.

The dominant narrative blames retirements, but the Learning Policy Institute data tells a different story: fewer than one in five teachers leaving the profession are retiring. The rest are career-changers, citing inadequate pay, job stress, and lack of support. The 27% pay penalty relative to comparable professions is the largest structural driver.

States that have invested in salary floors are seeing early results. Arkansas, after boosting its minimum to $50,000, reported improved applicant quality in rural districts. New Mexico has seen teacher preparation program enrollment rise following sustained salary investment. The correlation between competitive compensation and teacher supply is well-established in labor economics.

The proposed federal American Teacher Act would establish a national $60,000 minimum salary, which would represent a floor increase for 38 states. As of early 2026, the bill has not passed, but it signals that teacher compensation is increasingly a federal-level policy conversation, not just a state matter.

Texas illustrates the pension trap: no state income tax and a reasonable COL-adjusted salary look attractive, but the Equable Institute gives Texas one of the worst teacher pension grades in the country (44.9%). Since 40% of teachers do not qualify for Social Security — a federal exclusion for many public employees — their pension is their sole retirement income. A weak pension in Texas is a serious long-term financial risk that does not show up in salary comparisons.

Tennessee stands out as one of the genuinely best total-compensation states: above-average COL-adjusted salary, an A-rated pension (88.2% per Equable), and no state income tax. South Carolina similarly combines an excellent pension (94.2%, the highest in the nation) with competitive compensation. These states rarely make the headlines that California or New York generate, but they offer stronger financial outcomes for career educators.

The inflation-adjusted column reveals the structural problem: teacher pay in 2024-25 is essentially flat in real terms versus 2015-16, despite nominal salary growth of 27.8%. The post-2020 inflation surge erased the modest real gains accumulated through the late 2010s. This is the backdrop for the unprecedented 2026 legislative activity — state legislatures are responding to a decade of effective stagnation in teacher purchasing power.

Starting salaries are the one bright spot. NEA data shows the national average starting salary grew by 4.4% in 2024-25, the fastest growth rate in 15 years of tracking. Districts offering $60,000+ starting salaries rose 66% year over year. The entry-level market is tightening as districts compete for a shrinking pool of newly credentialed teachers.
