Overtime Pay Rules 2026: FLSA, Exempt vs Non-Exempt

Everything workers and employers need to know about federal and state overtime laws, salary thresholds, exemptions, and how to calculate overtime pay correctly.

March 6, 2026 11 min read

The Basics: What Is Overtime Pay?

Overtime pay is the premium wage employers must pay workers for hours beyond the standard workweek. Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), the foundational federal labor law in the United States, non-exempt employees must receive at least 1.5 times their regular hourly rate for every hour worked over 40 in a single workweek. This is commonly known as "time and a half."

The FLSA was enacted in 1938 as part of the New Deal and has been updated numerous times since. It establishes three core labor protections: minimum wage, overtime pay, and child labor restrictions. The overtime provision applies to the vast majority of American workers, covering roughly 140 million employees.

Despite its simplicity in concept, overtime law is riddled with exceptions, exemptions, and state-level variations that create confusion for both workers and employers. Misclassifying employees as exempt from overtime is one of the most common wage violations in the United States, resulting in billions of dollars in unpaid wages each year.

The 2026 Salary Threshold: Who Qualifies for Overtime

Not every worker is entitled to overtime pay. The FLSA exempts certain employees based on a combination of their salary level and job duties. The salary threshold is the first test: if your salary falls below it, you are automatically entitled to overtime regardless of your job title or responsibilities.

As of 2026, the key salary thresholds are:

Federal minimum salary threshold $58,656/year ($1,128/week) Highly compensated employee (HCE) threshold $151,164/year California (2x minimum wage, employer 26+) $66,560/year New York (NYC, Large employers) $62,400/year Washington State $67,724.80/year

The Department of Labor has significantly raised the federal threshold over recent years. Prior to 2024, the threshold was just $35,568 per year, which had not kept pace with inflation for decades. The increases mean millions of additional workers now qualify for overtime protection.

Exempt vs Non-Exempt: The Duties Test

Meeting the salary threshold alone does not make an employee exempt from overtime. The employee must also perform job duties that fall under one of the FLSA's white-collar exemptions. The three most common are:

Executive Exemption

The employee's primary duty is managing a department or subdivision. They regularly direct the work of at least two full-time employees (or equivalent). They have the authority to hire and fire, or their recommendations on hiring and firing carry significant weight.

Administrative Exemption

The employee's primary duty involves office or non-manual work directly related to management or general business operations. They exercise independent judgment and discretion on matters of significance. This is the broadest and most frequently litigated exemption.

Professional Exemption

The employee's primary duty requires advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning, customarily acquired through prolonged specialized instruction. This covers doctors, lawyers, engineers, accountants, teachers, and similar professions. Creative professionals (writers, musicians, actors) also qualify.

Two additional exemptions apply to specific roles:

  • Computer Employee Exemption: Systems analysts, programmers, software engineers, and similar roles earning at least $27.63 per hour. The work must involve design, development, documentation, analysis, creation, testing, or modification of computer systems or programs.
  • Outside Sales Exemption: Employees whose primary duty is making sales or obtaining orders away from the employer's place of business. This is the only exemption with no salary threshold requirement.

The critical point is that job titles mean nothing. An employee called a "Manager" who spends 80% of their time stocking shelves and serving customers is likely not exempt, regardless of their title. The duties test looks at what the employee actually does, not what their business card says.

How to Calculate Overtime Pay

For hourly employees, overtime calculation is straightforward. Multiply the regular hourly rate by 1.5 for each hour over 40:

Regular rate: $25.00/hour

Hours worked: 48 (40 regular + 8 overtime)

Regular pay: 40 x $25.00 = $1,000.00

Overtime pay: 8 x $37.50 = $300.00

Total weekly pay: $1,300.00

For salaried non-exempt employees, first calculate the regular hourly rate by dividing the weekly salary by 40:

Annual salary: $50,000

Weekly salary: $50,000 / 52 = $961.54

Regular hourly rate: $961.54 / 40 = $24.04

Overtime rate: $24.04 x 1.5 = $36.06

An important nuance: the "regular rate of pay" is not always your base hourly wage. Under the FLSA, non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, commissions, and certain other pay must be factored into the regular rate before calculating overtime. Discretionary bonuses and gifts are excluded.

State Overtime Laws That Go Beyond Federal

Federal law sets the floor, but many states provide more generous overtime protections. When state and federal laws conflict, the rule that benefits the employee more applies. Here are the key state variations:

California

California has the most employee-friendly overtime laws in the country. Workers receive 1.5x pay for hours over 8 in a single day (not just weekly), double time for hours over 12 in a day, and double time for all hours worked on the seventh consecutive day in a workweek. The state salary threshold is also higher than the federal level.

Alaska

Like California, Alaska requires daily overtime: 1.5x for hours worked over 8 in a day and over 40 in a week, whichever calculation is greater.

Colorado

Colorado requires overtime for hours over 12 in a day or 40 in a week, and it applies the higher overtime amount. The state also has specific rules for overtime in the healthcare and ski industries.

Nevada

Overtime is required for hours over 8 in a 24-hour period for employees earning less than 1.5 times the minimum wage. This daily overtime applies in addition to the federal 40-hour weekly threshold.

Washington State

The state has been gradually raising its exempt salary threshold to 2.5 times the state minimum wage. In 2026, this puts the threshold above $67,000, one of the highest in the nation.

Comp Time: Can Your Employer Give Time Off Instead of Pay?

Compensatory time off (comp time) is the practice of giving employees paid time off instead of overtime wages. This is a common source of confusion because the rules differ dramatically between the public and private sectors.

  • Private-sector employers: Comp time in lieu of overtime pay is generally illegal under federal law. Non-exempt employees at private companies must receive cash wages at 1.5x their regular rate for overtime hours. An employer cannot say "take Friday off instead of getting overtime pay."
  • Public-sector employers: Government agencies may offer comp time at a rate of 1.5 hours of time off for each hour of overtime worked. Employees can accrue up to 240 hours of comp time (480 hours for public safety and emergency response workers).
  • Flex time within a workweek: Private employers can adjust schedules within the same workweek (for example, working 10 hours Monday and 6 hours Friday) without triggering overtime, as long as total hours do not exceed 40 in the week. This is not comp time; it is schedule flexibility.

If your employer is offering comp time instead of overtime pay and you work in the private sector, this may be a wage violation. Document your hours carefully and consider consulting a labor attorney or filing a complaint with your state labor department.

Common Overtime Violations and Red Flags

Wage theft from unpaid overtime is one of the most widespread labor violations in the country. The Economic Policy Institute estimates that workers lose over $15 billion per year to overtime violations alone. Watch for these common practices:

  1. Misclassification as exempt: The most common violation. Employers give workers a "manager" title but their actual duties do not meet the executive exemption test. If you spend more than 50% of your time doing the same work as your non-exempt subordinates, you may be misclassified.
  2. Off-the-clock work: Requiring employees to check email, answer calls, or complete tasks before clocking in or after clocking out. All work time must be compensated, including mandatory pre-shift meetings and post-shift cleanup.
  3. Averaging hours across workweeks: If you work 50 hours one week and 30 the next, you are owed 10 hours of overtime for the first week. Your employer cannot average the two weeks to claim you worked 40 hours per week.
  4. Misclassification as independent contractor: Employers sometimes classify workers as contractors to avoid overtime obligations entirely. The economic reality test looks at factors like how much control the employer exercises, who provides tools and equipment, and whether the worker has the opportunity for profit or loss.
  5. Automatic meal break deductions: Automatically deducting 30 minutes for a meal break even when the employee worked through it. If you are required to remain on-call or available during your break, it is compensable time.
  6. Rounding abuse: While the FLSA allows time rounding to the nearest 5, 10, or 15 minutes, rounding practices that systematically reduce employee hours are illegal.

Overtime for Special Situations

Several employment situations have unique overtime rules that differ from the general FLSA framework:

  • Healthcare workers: Hospitals may use a 14-day work period instead of the standard 7-day workweek, but must pay overtime for hours over 8 in a day and 80 in the 14-day period. This is called the "8 and 80" system.
  • First responders: Police and firefighters have special overtime thresholds. Fire protection employees working a 28-day period receive overtime after 212 hours. Law enforcement on a 28-day period receives overtime after 171 hours.
  • Tipped employees: The regular rate for overtime calculation must include the full minimum wage, not just the tipped wage. If a tipped server earning $2.13/hour plus tips works overtime, the overtime rate is based on the full federal minimum wage ($7.25) or applicable state minimum wage, not the $2.13 base.
  • Multiple jobs for the same employer: If you work two different positions for the same employer, all hours are combined for overtime purposes. An employee working 25 hours in the warehouse and 20 hours in retail at the same company has worked 45 hours and is owed 5 hours of overtime.
  • Piece-rate workers: Workers paid by the piece (per unit produced) are still entitled to overtime. The regular rate is calculated by dividing total piece-rate earnings by total hours worked, then paying the 0.5x premium (since the straight-time is already included in piece-rate pay).

What to Do If You Are Not Getting Paid Overtime

If you believe your employer is violating overtime laws, you have several options. The FLSA has a two-year statute of limitations for standard claims and three years for willful violations, so act promptly.

  1. Document everything: Keep personal records of your hours worked, including start and end times, meal breaks, and any off-the-clock work. Email yourself a log each day as a timestamped record.
  2. Review your classification: Check whether your salary exceeds the threshold and whether your actual duties meet any exemption test. Use the Department of Labor's online resources to assess your classification.
  3. Talk to HR or your employer: Some overtime violations are the result of misunderstanding rather than intentional theft. A conversation with human resources may resolve the issue.
  4. File a complaint with the Department of Labor: The Wage and Hour Division investigates overtime claims at no cost to the employee. You can file online or by calling your local WHD office.
  5. Consult an employment attorney: For significant back-pay claims, an attorney may take your case on contingency. The FLSA allows recovery of up to two years of unpaid overtime plus an equal amount in liquidated damages, effectively doubling your award.

Industry-Specific Overtime Rules

Beyond the general FLSA framework and state laws, several industries have their own overtime regulations that workers and employers need to know:

  • Trucking and transportation: Interstate truck drivers are exempt from federal overtime under the Motor Carrier Act exemption. However, local delivery drivers and those operating vehicles under 10,000 pounds are generally entitled to overtime. Several states have additional protections for drivers within state borders.
  • Agriculture: Farm workers are largely exempt from federal overtime requirements under the FLSA. However, California, New York, Washington, and a few other states have enacted agricultural overtime laws. California phases in agricultural overtime, and as of 2025, farm workers in California receive overtime after 8 hours in a day or 40 hours in a week.
  • Retail and hospitality: Retail and hospitality workers are generally entitled to overtime. However, commission-based retail employees may be partially exempt if more than half their earnings come from commissions and their regular rate exceeds 1.5 times the minimum wage. This is the Section 7(i) exemption.
  • Technology and startups: The computer employee exemption applies to workers earning at least $27.63 per hour in qualifying roles. However, help desk technicians, IT support staff, and hardware repair technicians typically do not qualify, even if their employer classifies them as exempt. This is a frequent area of misclassification litigation.
  • Construction: Construction workers are almost always non-exempt and entitled to overtime. The prevailing wage requirements under the Davis-Bacon Act for federally funded projects add another layer of overtime calculations.
  • Education: Teachers at accredited institutions are exempt from overtime regardless of salary. However, teaching assistants, adjunct professors, coaches, and non-instructional staff may be entitled to overtime depending on their duties and pay level.

Regular overtime can substantially boost your annual income. Here is how consistent overtime hours translate to additional yearly earnings at various wage levels:

For a worker earning $25 per hour, just 5 hours of overtime per week adds $9,375 to their annual income, a 22.5% boost over their base salary of $52,000. At 10 overtime hours per week, the boost rises to $18,750, or 36% more than base pay.

These calculations show why overtime-eligible positions can be financially attractive compared to salaried exempt roles. A non-exempt worker earning $52,000 base with consistent overtime can easily out-earn an exempt colleague making $65,000 with no overtime eligibility.

The Alternative Workweek: 4/10s, 9/80s, and Compressed Schedules

Many employers offer alternative workweek schedules that affect how overtime is calculated. Understanding these arrangements helps you evaluate job offers and maximize your compensation.

  • 4/10 schedule: Working four 10-hour days per week. Under federal law, no overtime is owed because total weekly hours equal 40. However, in states with daily overtime (California, Alaska, Nevada), the 10-hour days may trigger overtime after the 8th hour unless the employer has established an approved alternative workweek schedule.
  • 9/80 schedule: Working nine 9-hour days across two weeks, with every other Friday off. The employer must carefully designate the workweek start time to avoid creating a week with 45 hours. Typically, the workweek starts midday on Friday, splitting the 8-hour Friday across two workweeks of 40 hours each.
  • 3/12 schedule: Common in healthcare, manufacturing, and emergency services. Three 12-hour shifts per week totaling 36 hours. No federal overtime is triggered. Some employers pay a premium to make up for the reduced hours, while others adjust the annual salary down slightly.
  • Rotating shifts: Common in 24/7 operations. Workers may rotate between day, evening, and night shifts. Shift differentials (extra pay for evening and night shifts) must be included in the regular rate when calculating overtime.

In California specifically, employers must follow a formal election process to implement an alternative workweek schedule. Employees vote on the schedule, and if approved, daily overtime thresholds adjust accordingly. Without this formal process, California employees are entitled to overtime after 8 hours regardless of the agreed schedule.

Overtime and Benefits: What Counts, What Does Not

Overtime pay interacts with other compensation and benefits in ways that many workers do not realize. Understanding these interactions helps you accurately calculate your total compensation.

  • 401(k) contributions: Most employer 401(k) plans calculate contributions as a percentage of total compensation including overtime. If you contribute 6% and earn $5,000 in overtime, an additional $300 goes to your retirement account. However, some plans exclude overtime from matching calculations. Check your plan document.
  • Social Security benefits: Overtime earnings count toward your Social Security benefit calculation. Since benefits are based on your 35 highest-earning years, consistent overtime during your career can increase your monthly Social Security check in retirement.
  • Workers compensation: If you are injured on the job while working overtime, workers compensation benefits are typically calculated using your average weekly wage including overtime earnings, resulting in higher benefit payments.
  • Unemployment insurance: Unemployment benefit calculations vary by state but usually consider your total earnings including overtime over a base period. Higher earnings from overtime can result in higher unemployment benefits if you lose your job.
  • Paid time off: Most employers pay PTO, vacation, and sick days at your regular rate, not your overtime rate. However, the hours you take off generally do not count toward the 40-hour threshold for calculating overtime in that workweek.

The Future of Overtime Law

Overtime regulations continue to evolve. Several important developments are shaping the landscape for 2026 and beyond:

  • Automatic threshold adjustments: The Department of Labor has proposed tying the salary threshold to a percentile of earnings, which would automatically increase the threshold every three years. This would prevent the long periods of stagnation that left the previous threshold unchanged from 2004 to 2019.
  • Gig economy classification: The ongoing debate over whether gig workers (Uber drivers, DoorDash delivery workers, Instacart shoppers) are employees or independent contractors has major overtime implications. Employee classification would entitle these workers to overtime protections.
  • Four-day workweek proposals: Several legislative proposals aim to redefine the standard workweek from 40 hours to 32 hours, which would trigger overtime after 32 hours instead of 40. While no federal legislation has passed, some companies are voluntarily implementing this.
  • Remote work tracking: As remote work grows, tracking overtime hours becomes more complex. New legal questions around when remote employees are "on the clock" for activities like checking email at home are creating new overtime compliance challenges.

Workers should stay informed about changes to overtime regulations. The Department of Labor website publishes all proposed and final rules, and industry publications often provide practical analysis of how changes affect specific sectors.

Overtime Strategy: When Extra Hours Make Financial Sense

While overtime protections exist to prevent exploitation, many workers actively seek overtime for the financial boost. Understanding the tax implications and trade-offs helps you decide when overtime is worth it.

  • Overtime is not taxed at a higher rate: A common myth is that overtime pushes you into a higher tax bracket, making the extra hours not worthwhile. While overtime income is subject to the same marginal tax rate as your regular income, only the portion that crosses a bracket threshold is taxed at the higher rate. The rest of your income stays at the same rate.
  • Withholding vs actual tax: Your employer may withhold taxes at a higher rate on overtime pay, making it look like you are being taxed more. However, the actual tax owed is calculated on your annual return. If too much was withheld, you will get it back as a refund.
  • The real cost is time: An hour of overtime at $37.50 minus taxes leaves roughly $27-$30 in your pocket. Decide whether an extra hour of work is worth that amount compared to time with family, rest, or other activities.
  • Strategic overtime: If you have a specific financial goal like paying off debt, building an emergency fund, or saving for a down payment, targeted overtime during a defined period can accelerate your progress without leading to burnout.

Recordkeeping: What You Should Track

Whether you are an employee tracking your hours for your own protection or an employer maintaining compliance, proper recordkeeping is essential. The FLSA requires employers to maintain certain records for each non-exempt employee for at least three years.

As an employee, you should keep your own records even if your employer tracks time. If a dispute arises, having independent documentation strengthens your position significantly. Keep a simple log with the following for each workday:

  • Date and day of the week
  • Time you started and finished work (including remote or off-site work)
  • Meal break start and end times
  • Any work performed before clocking in or after clocking out (emails, calls, prep)
  • Total hours worked that day

Email yourself this log weekly so you have a timestamped record. Many smartphone apps also track work hours automatically. In overtime disputes, courts have consistently given more weight to employee records than employer records when the employer's records are incomplete or disputed.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the overtime pay rate in the US?

Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), the federal overtime rate is 1.5 times (time and a half) your regular hourly rate for all hours worked over 40 in a single workweek. Some states like California also require overtime pay for hours worked over 8 in a single day, and double time for hours over 12 in a day.

What is the exempt salary threshold for 2026?

As of 2026, the federal minimum salary threshold for exempt (overtime-ineligible) employees is $58,656 per year ($1,128 per week). Employees earning below this threshold must receive overtime pay regardless of their job duties. Some states set higher thresholds.

Can salaried employees get overtime pay?

Yes. Being salaried does not automatically make you exempt from overtime. To be exempt, an employee must be paid on a salary basis, earn above the minimum salary threshold, and perform job duties that qualify under executive, administrative, professional, outside sales, or computer employee exemptions.

Is comp time legal instead of overtime pay?

For private-sector employers, comp time in lieu of overtime pay is generally illegal under federal law. Employers must pay non-exempt employees time and a half in cash wages. Public-sector (government) employers may offer comp time at a rate of 1.5 hours off for each overtime hour worked.

What hours count toward overtime?

All hours actually worked count toward the 40-hour overtime threshold. This includes time spent on job-related tasks, mandatory training, required travel during the workday, and on-call time when you must remain on premises. Paid holidays, vacation days, and sick days typically do not count as hours worked for overtime purposes.
