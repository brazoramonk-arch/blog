Heat Pump vs Gas Furnace: Which Saves More Money?

The decision between a heat pump and a gas furnace is no longer just about climate — it is increasingly about economics. With heat pump technology advancing rapidly, federal rebates reaching historic highs, and natural gas prices rising, the math has shifted dramatically in favor of heat pumps for most American homeowners. This guide breaks down the real costs so you can make the right choice.

How Heat Pumps and Gas Furnaces Work

A gas furnace burns natural gas to create heat, which is distributed through your home via ductwork. Even the most efficient gas furnaces waste 2-20% of their fuel as exhaust. A standard-efficiency furnace (80% AFUE) sends 20 cents of every dollar spent on gas up the flue.

A heat pump works differently. Instead of generating heat, it moves heat from one place to another using a refrigerant cycle — the same principle as your refrigerator, but in reverse. In heating mode, a heat pump extracts heat from outdoor air (even cold air contains thermal energy) and moves it inside. In cooling mode, it reverses the process, functioning as an air conditioner.

This fundamental difference is why heat pumps can achieve efficiency ratings above 100%. Moving heat requires far less energy than creating heat through combustion. A modern heat pump with a COP of 3.0 produces three units of heat energy for every one unit of electricity consumed — effectively 300% efficient. Efficiency: COP vs AFUE Ratings

Gas furnaces are rated by Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency (AFUE), which measures what percentage of fuel becomes usable heat. Standard furnaces achieve 80% AFUE, while high-efficiency condensing models reach 95-98% AFUE. An 80% AFUE furnace wastes 20% of its fuel, while a 96% model wastes only 4%.

Heat pumps are rated by HSPF2 (Heating Seasonal Performance Factor) and SEER2 (for cooling). To compare directly with furnaces, use the Coefficient of Performance (COP). A heat pump with HSPF2 of 10 has a COP of approximately 2.9, meaning it produces 2.9 BTUs of heat for every BTU of electricity consumed.

Annual Operating Cost Comparison

The annual cost to heat your home depends on three factors: your system's efficiency, fuel prices in your area, and how much heating your home needs (measured in heating degree days). The average American home requires approximately 50-80 million BTUs of heating energy per year, depending on size, insulation, and climate.

For a typical 2,000 sq ft home requiring 60 million BTUs of heating annually, at average 2026 energy prices of $0.16/kWh electricity and $1.20/therm natural gas, operating costs compare as follows: a 96% AFUE gas furnace costs approximately $750 per year, while a high-efficiency heat pump (HSPF2 10) costs approximately $600 per year. In mild climates where the heat pump operates near its peak COP, savings can reach $300-$500 annually.

However, heat pumps provide both heating and cooling. If you currently have a furnace plus a separate air conditioner, replacing both with a heat pump eliminates the cooling system maintenance cost and often provides more efficient cooling (SEER2 16-22) than a standard AC unit. When you factor in combined heating and cooling costs, the heat pump advantage grows to $400-$800 per year for most homes.

Where gas is cheap (below $0.80/therm) and electricity is expensive (above $0.25/kWh), gas furnaces can still win on operating costs. This applies primarily to parts of the Midwest and Mountain West. Installation Costs

Upfront cost is where gas furnaces traditionally hold an advantage, though federal rebates have narrowed the gap significantly. A new gas furnace costs $3,500-$7,500 installed, with high-efficiency condensing models at the upper end. A ducted air-source heat pump costs $5,000-$12,000 installed, depending on capacity and brand.

Ductless mini-split heat pumps cost $3,000-$5,000 per zone (indoor unit plus portion of outdoor unit). A three-zone mini-split system for a typical home runs $8,000-$15,000. Mini-splits are ideal for homes without existing ductwork, additions, and supplemental heating in specific rooms.

Keep in mind that a heat pump replaces both your furnace and air conditioner. If you would otherwise need a new AC unit ($3,000-$7,000), the net cost of choosing a heat pump over a furnace-plus-AC system is much smaller — often $2,000-$5,000 more before rebates, and sometimes less after rebates. Federal and State Rebates for Heat Pumps

The Inflation Reduction Act created two major incentive programs for heat pumps that make 2026 an especially attractive time to switch.

25C Tax Credit

The Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit (Section 25C) provides up to $2,000 per year for qualifying heat pump installations. The heat pump must meet ENERGY STAR Most Efficient or CEE Tier 1 specifications. This is a non-refundable tax credit that reduces your federal income tax liability. You can claim it every year you make qualifying improvements, not just once. Visit LevyIO's Tax Bracket Calculator to see how this credit affects your tax situation.

HEEHRA Rebates

The High-Efficiency Electric Home Rebate Act offers point-of-sale rebates for income-qualifying households. Households earning less than 80% of area median income (AMI) can receive up to $8,000 for a heat pump, covering up to 100% of costs. Households earning 80-150% of AMI can receive up to $8,000 covering up to 50% of costs. These rebates are applied at the point of sale, reducing your out-of-pocket cost immediately rather than requiring you to wait for a tax refund.

State and Utility Incentives

Most states offer additional rebates that stack with federal incentives. Massachusetts provides up to $10,000 through the Mass Save program. New York offers $1,000-$3,000 through NYSERDA. California provides up to $3,000 through the TECH Clean California program. Many local utilities add $300-$1,500 in additional rebates. Check dsireusa.org and your utility website for current offers.

Performance by Climate Zone

Heat pump performance varies with outdoor temperature. As temperatures drop, the COP decreases because there is less thermal energy available in the outdoor air. Modern cold-climate heat pumps have dramatically improved cold-weather performance, but it is important to match the right equipment to your climate.

Mild Climates (Zones 1-3: Southeast, Southwest, Pacific Coast)

In areas where temperatures rarely drop below 30°F, standard heat pumps operate at peak efficiency nearly all winter. Annual heating COP averages 3.0-4.0, making heat pumps 50-70% cheaper to operate than gas furnaces. The Southeast and Pacific Coast are the strongest markets for heat pumps, with payback periods as short as 3-5 years after rebates.

Moderate Climates (Zones 4-5: Mid-Atlantic, Midwest, Pacific Northwest)

In areas with regular freezing temperatures, cold-climate heat pumps maintain strong efficiency with seasonal COP of 2.5-3.0. Operating costs are roughly equal to or 10-30% lower than a high-efficiency gas furnace. When cooling savings are factored in, heat pumps typically save $300-$600 per year. These zones offer 5-8 year payback periods.

Cold Climates (Zones 6-7: Northern Tier, Mountain West)

In areas where temperatures regularly reach 0°F or below, cold-climate heat pumps from Mitsubishi (Hyper-Heating), Daikin (Aurora), and Bosch maintain heating capacity down to -15°F. Seasonal COP averages 2.0-2.5 with supplemental electric resistance heat adding cost during the coldest periods. Operating costs are roughly comparable to gas furnaces, but heat pumps win when rebates and dual-function (heating plus cooling) value are included.

Pairing a Heat Pump with Solar Panels

One of the strongest arguments for a heat pump is its synergy with solar panels. Since heat pumps run on electricity, you can power them with free solar energy, effectively reducing your heating and cooling costs to near zero. A gas furnace, by contrast, will always require purchasing natural gas regardless of whether you have solar panels.

A typical heat pump adds 3,000-5,000 kWh of annual electricity demand. An additional 2-4 kW of solar panels (costing $5,000-$10,000 before the ITC) can offset this entirely. After the 30% solar tax credit, the incremental solar cost is $3,500-$7,000, which pays for itself in 4-6 years through avoided electricity purchases. After that, your heating and cooling are essentially free for the remaining life of the system.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a heat pump cheaper to run than a gas furnace?

In most climates, yes. Heat pumps operate at 200-400% efficiency, producing 2-4 units of heat per unit of electricity. At average U.S. energy prices, heat pumps cost $800-$1,400 per year to heat an average home, compared to $1,000-$1,800 for gas furnaces. The advantage grows when you include cooling savings.

Do heat pumps work in cold climates?

Modern cold-climate heat pumps work efficiently down to -15°F. Mitsubishi Hyper-Heating, Daikin Aurora, and Bosch models maintain 75-100% capacity at 5°F and are widely used in Maine, Minnesota, and Canada. They are no longer just for mild climates.

How much does it cost to install a heat pump vs a furnace?

A gas furnace costs $3,500-$7,500 installed. A ducted heat pump costs $5,000-$12,000 installed but replaces both furnace and AC. Federal rebates of $2,000-$8,000 make heat pumps cost-competitive or cheaper than furnace-plus-AC systems in most cases.

What rebates are available for heat pumps in 2026?

The federal 25C credit provides up to $2,000. HEEHRA offers $4,000-$8,000 for income-qualifying households. State and utility rebates add $500-$3,000. Combined, rebates can cover 30-60% of installation costs. Use our Heat Pump Calculator to estimate your net cost.
