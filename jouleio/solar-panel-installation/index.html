Solar Panel Installation: Process, Cost & What to Expect

A family in Phoenix signed their solar contract on a Monday. Their panels were physically installed just 18 days later — a single crew worked two days on their 8.4 kW system. But they did not flip the switch and start generating electricity for another 11 weeks, waiting for permits, inspections, and utility approval. That gap surprises almost every first-time solar buyer. This guide walks you through exactly what happens — and when — from the moment you sign to the day your meter starts running backward.

The Full Installation Process at a Glance

The disconnect between installation day and activation day is the single biggest source of confusion — and frustration — for new solar buyers. The physical act of mounting panels on your roof takes 1-3 days for a residential system. But the paperwork, approvals, and utility coordination surrounding that event can add 6-16 weeks. Understanding the full timeline helps you set realistic expectations and avoid being misled by marketing claims.

Phase Timeline Who Does It Your Role
Site assessment & design1-2 weeksInstallerShare utility bills, answer questions
Contract & financing1-3 daysYou + InstallerReview and sign documents
Permit application2-8 weeksInstallerHOA approval if applicable
Equipment delivery1-4 weeksInstallerConfirm install date
Physical installation1-3 daysInstallation crewBe home (or accessible)
Building inspection1-2 weeksLocal building dept.Be home for inspection
Utility interconnection (PTO)1-4 weeksUtility companyWait for PTO letter; then activate

Total elapsed time: typically 8-20 weeks (2-5 months). EnergySage reports that the average time from contract to activation in the U.S. is approximately 90 days. California runs longer at 5-6 months due to stricter permitting; Texas tends to be faster at 3-4 months. Supply chain delays for specific equipment can add another 4-6 weeks during high-demand periods.

Phase 1: Site Assessment & System Design

Before any equipment is ordered, a qualified installer evaluates whether your home is a good solar candidate and designs the system to match your specific situation. This phase involves both an on-site visit and desk-based analysis.

Roof Evaluation

The installer inspects your roof's condition, age, material, pitch, and orientation. South-facing roofs at 15-40° pitch produce the most solar energy in the continental U.S. East and west-facing roofs produce 15-20% less but can still be economically viable, especially with time-of-use electricity rates. Shading from trees, chimneys, or neighboring structures is carefully assessed — even partial shading on one panel can reduce entire string production in systems without power optimizers or microinverters. Roofs with less than 5 years of remaining life should be replaced before installing solar, which adds cost but avoids re-installation fees later.

Electricity Usage Analysis

Your installer will request 12 months of utility bill history to calculate your annual kWh consumption and identify seasonal patterns. This data, combined with local peak sun hours from NREL's PVWatts database, determines the system size needed to offset a target percentage of your usage (typically 80-100%). For a home consuming 12,000 kWh/year in Phoenix (5.5 peak sun hours/day), a 7 kW system is typically recommended. Use our Solar Panel Calculator to estimate your ideal system size before your first installer meeting.

Structural Assessment

Solar panels add approximately 2-4 pounds per square foot to your roof load. Most modern roofs easily accommodate this weight, but older homes or flat roofs may require a structural engineer's sign-off. The installer checks rafter spacing, condition, and whether mounting points can be properly flashed to prevent leaks. Structural upgrades are rare but can add $1,000-$4,000 when needed.

System Design

The design phase produces a single-line electrical diagram, a site plan showing panel placement on the roof, and equipment specifications. This documentation is submitted with the permit application. Most installers also use shade analysis software (like Aurora or Solargraf) to model your system's production hour by hour throughout the year, accounting for your roof's orientation and local shading obstructions.

Phase 2: Permitting & HOA Approval

Permitting is the phase most homeowners underestimate. According to EnergySage, permitting accounts for 25-40% of total project time and is the single most common source of delays. Your installer handles the permit application — you typically just sign documents they prepare. But you may need to personally obtain HOA approval, which can add weeks.

Building Permits

Every solar installation requires a building permit that covers both the structural (roof mounting) and electrical (wiring, inverter, panel) aspects. The application typically includes: system site plan, single-line electrical diagram, equipment specifications, structural attachment details, and the utility interconnection application. Processing time varies enormously — some municipalities have streamlined online systems that approve permits in 1-3 days, while others queue applications for monthly board review meetings, creating 4-8 week delays. States with standardized permitting (California, Massachusetts, New York) tend to be faster at the jurisdiction level despite longer total timelines due to utility queues.

HOA Considerations

If you live in an HOA community, you may need architectural review committee (ARC) approval before installation. Importantly, 24 states have enacted solar access laws that prohibit HOAs from banning rooftop solar, though they may impose reasonable aesthetic requirements (panel color, placement restrictions). Even with legal protections, HOA review processes can add 2-6 weeks. Submit your request early and include the system design documents — most HOA approvals go smoothly when presented professionally.

Utility Interconnection Application

Separately from the building permit, your installer submits an interconnection application to your utility company. This application requests permission to connect your solar system to the grid and enroll in net metering. Major utilities process interconnection applications in 2-6 weeks; smaller rural cooperatives and municipal utilities sometimes take longer. The interconnection approval triggers the utility to schedule installation of your new bidirectional smart meter.

Phase 3: Installation Day — What Actually Happens

Installation day is the most visible part of the process — and the shortest. A crew of 2-4 workers typically arrives around 7-8 AM and works through the day. For most residential systems, installation is complete in 1-2 days. Larger systems or complex roofs may take 3 days.

Morning: Roof Mounting

The crew starts on the roof, locating and marking roof rafters (structural members) for mount points. Each roof penetration is carefully made and immediately sealed with flashing and waterproof sealant — properly done, this is more watertight than the original roofing. Mounting rails (typically aluminum extrusions) are attached to the flashed anchors, and panel clamps are installed along the rails. This structural phase typically takes 2-4 hours for an average-sized system.

Midday: Panel and Inverter Installation

Solar panels are lifted to the roof and secured to the mounting rails with module clamps. If using microinverters, one unit mounts directly beneath each panel on the rail before the panel goes up. If using string inverters, DC wiring from each panel runs through conduit to the inverter location (typically in the garage or utility room). The string inverter is mounted on the wall near your electrical panel. The crew runs conduit from the inverter to your main electrical panel, maintaining code-required separation from other wiring.

Afternoon: Electrical Connections

A licensed electrician makes the final connections at your main electrical panel, adding a dedicated solar circuit breaker. They install the required rapid shutdown device (per NEC 2020, which most states now follow), utility disconnect switch, and production meter. All work is completed to the approved permit drawings. Before leaving, the crew verifies no power flows until the official inspection and PTO are granted — the system will be physically installed but not yet active.

Homeowner tip: Be home on installation day, but you do not need to be present the entire time. The crew will need brief access to your electrical panel and potentially your garage or basement. Plan to be available for a 10-minute walkthrough at the end of the day, when the lead installer explains what was installed and what happens next.

Solar System Components Explained

Understanding what you are buying prevents buyer's remorse and helps you evaluate quotes intelligently. Here are the main components of every residential solar system:

Solar Panels

In 2026, virtually all residential installations use monocrystalline silicon panels, which offer efficiency ratings of 20-23% (versus polycrystalline at 15-17%). Higher efficiency means more watts per square foot — critical for homes with limited roof space. Top manufacturers include SunPower (highest efficiency at up to 22.8%), LG, REC, Panasonic, QCells, and Canadian Solar. NREL sets efficiency benchmarks for all major commercial silicon solar technologies.

Panel warranties typically have two components: a product warranty (10-25 years covering manufacturing defects) and a performance warranty guaranteeing the panel will produce at least 80-90% of its rated output after 25 years. Premium panels like SunPower's guarantee 92% output at 25 years; budget panels may only guarantee 80%. For a 25-year investment, the degradation rate matters more than upfront panel cost in most cases.

Inverters: String, Micro, or Power Optimizer?

The inverter converts DC electricity from your panels into AC electricity for your home. This is the single most important equipment decision affecting system performance.

String inverters connect all panels in a series "string" to one central inverter. They are the lowest-cost option and most reliable due to few components. Weakness: if one panel is shaded or underperforms, it drags down the entire string. Best for unshaded south-facing roofs.

Microinverters (Enphase is the dominant brand) attach individually to each panel, converting DC to AC at the panel level. Each panel operates independently — shading one panel does not affect others. They also provide panel-level monitoring and are the safest option for rapid shutdown compliance. Cost is 10-20% more than string inverters. Best for complex roofs, shading, or systems with multiple orientations.

Power optimizers + string inverter (SolarEdge is the main option) combine panel-level DC optimization with a central string inverter. Similar shade tolerance to microinverters but with a single central inverter point of failure. Good balance of cost and performance. Many battery systems (especially Tesla Powerwall 3) include an integrated hybrid inverter that replaces the need for a separate solar inverter.

Mounting Hardware

Mounting hardware must hold panels securely through 90-mph winds and decades of thermal expansion. Major racking manufacturers include IronRidge, Unirac, and Quick Mount PV. The racking system typically carries a 20-25 year structural warranty. For flat or low-pitch roofs, ballasted systems can be used to avoid roof penetrations, though they require sufficient structural load capacity.

Phase 4: Inspection & Interconnection

After installation, two more approval hurdles stand between you and actual solar production: a local building inspection and utility Permission to Operate.

Building Inspection

Your local building department sends an inspector (or your installer facilitates a virtual inspection in some jurisdictions) to verify the installation matches the approved permit drawings. The inspector checks: roof penetration sealing and flashing, electrical conduit routing and connections, panel mounting and grounding, rapid shutdown device compliance, and code labeling requirements. Inspections typically take 30-60 minutes. If the inspector identifies any issues, your installer corrects them and schedules a re-inspection. Most residential solar systems pass on the first inspection. Processing to schedule the inspection appointment takes 5-15 business days in most municipalities.

Permission to Operate (PTO)

Once the building inspection passes, your installer submits the signed inspection report to the utility. The utility then schedules installation of your bidirectional smart meter (if not already installed) and formally grants Permission to Operate — the official authorization to activate your system. PTO processing takes 1-4 weeks at most utilities, though large utilities in California (PG&E, SCE, SDG&E) have historically had 3-6 week queues during high-volume periods.

Once PTO is granted, your installer either activates the system remotely or walks you through the final switch-on. From this moment, your system is live, your solar monitoring app begins showing production data, and your net metering credits start accumulating. Prices vary by region — installers in the Northeast typically charge $3.80-$4.20/W due to higher labor rates and code compliance costs, while Southwest markets like Arizona and Nevada average $2.80-$3.30/W due to high installation volume and competition. Additional Costs to Budget For

Beyond the base system cost, budget for these potential additions:

  • Roof repair or replacement: $3,000-$12,000+ if needed before installation
  • Electrical panel upgrade: $1,500-$3,500 if your current panel is insufficient (often needed in older homes)
  • Battery storage: $7,000-$15,000 per battery (before 30% ITC)
  • EV charger (Level 2): $1,200-$2,500 installed if added during solar installation (labor savings versus separate installation)
  • Tree trimming: $300-$2,000 to reduce shading from nearby trees

How to Choose a Solar Installer

Your choice of installer matters as much as your choice of equipment. A mediocre installer using premium panels will underperform a skilled installer using standard equipment. Here is how to evaluate your options:

NABCEP Certification

The North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners (NABCEP) PV Installation Professional certification is the solar industry's highest credential, requiring passing a rigorous exam and documenting hundreds of hours of field experience. Ask every installer whether their lead technician or project manager holds NABCEP certification. According to SEIA, companies with NABCEP-certified staff deliver consistently higher-quality installations with lower callback rates.

Local Experience and References

Prefer installers who have completed at least 50-100 installations in your specific metro area. Local experience means familiarity with your utility's interconnection process, your municipal building department's requirements, and common roofing conditions in your region. Ask for 3-5 local references you can contact — not just Google reviews. Ask references specifically about timeline accuracy (did the timeline match what was quoted?) and any post-installation service issues.

Warranty Terms

Solar installers provide a workmanship warranty covering roof penetrations, wiring connections, and installation quality. Industry standard is 10 years; leading companies offer 25 years. This warranty matters because roof leaks and loose connections typically manifest 3-7 years after installation — after short warranties have expired. Confirm the installer will still be in business to honor their warranty (the DOE estimates that 15-20% of solar companies operating today will not exist in 10 years).

Get at Least 3 Quotes

Prices for identical systems vary 15-30% between installers in the same market. According to EnergySage analysis, homeowners who get 3+ quotes save an average of $5,000-$10,000 over the system's lifetime compared to those who accept the first offer. The solar industry has a well-documented pricing issue with high-pressure, single-quote tactics — resist the urgency and compare options systematically.

Red Flags and What Can Go Wrong

Solar is a significant investment, and the industry has its share of bad actors. Here are the warning signs to watch for:

High-Pressure Sales Tactics

"This offer is only good today" is a hallmark of predatory solar sales. Legitimate installers do not have daily pricing — their quotes reflect their actual cost structure, which does not change overnight. Walk away from any salesperson who demands a same-day decision or refuses to provide written documentation to review.

Unrealistic Savings Promises

Be wary of savings projections that assume electricity rates rise 6-8% per year (the historical average is 3-4% per EIA data) or that use production estimates significantly higher than NREL PVWatts for your location. Solar is a strong investment on realistic assumptions — dishonest projections are unnecessary and a sign of a problematic company.

Common Installation Problems

The most common installation issues include: improper roof flashing leading to leaks (most frequent complaint), panels installed with insufficient gap for thermal expansion causing cracking, suboptimal string configurations reducing output, and failure to install rapid shutdown devices required by NEC 2020. These issues are largely preventable with a NABCEP-certified installer and a thorough post-installation inspection.

After Installation: Monitoring, Warranties & Maintenance

Solar panel systems are remarkably low-maintenance, but a small amount of ongoing attention protects your investment and catches issues early.

Monitoring Your System

All modern inverters include wireless monitoring that streams production data to a smartphone app (Enphase Enlighten, SolarEdge app, Tesla app). Check your production data at least monthly for the first year to establish a baseline. Watch for sudden drops in daily production — a 20%+ unexplained decrease warrants a call to your installer. Most monitoring apps also track carbon offset and cumulative savings, which keeps you motivated and helps you validate your financial projections.

Panel Cleaning

In most U.S. climates, rain keeps panels adequately clean. In dusty or dry climates (Arizona, Nevada, Southern California), soiling can reduce output by 5-25% between rainfalls. NREL research indicates annual professional cleaning in arid climates pays for itself in recovered production. Avoid using abrasive cleaners or pressure washers — warm water and a soft brush are sufficient.

Professional Inspection

Have a professional inspect your system every 3-5 years, checking for loose connections, micro-cracks in panels (visible with thermal imaging), conduit integrity, and inverter health. This is typically included in some installer maintenance plans. Use our Home Energy Audit Tool to evaluate your full home energy performance alongside your solar system output.

Inverter Replacement

String inverters typically carry 10-12 year warranties and have an expected lifespan of 10-15 years. You may need to replace the inverter once during your system's 25+ year life, at a cost of $1,500-$3,000. Microinverters carry 15-25 year warranties and typically outlast string inverters. Factor inverter replacement into your long-term ROI calculation — our Solar Payback Calculator includes an inverter replacement cost field.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does solar panel installation take?

Physical installation takes 1-3 days. The full process from contract to activation typically takes 2-6 months, with permitting being the longest phase. Per EnergySage, the U.S. average is about 90 days. California averages 5-6 months; Texas typically 3-4 months. Supply delays can add 4-6 weeks during high-demand periods.

How much does solar panel installation cost in 2026?

Per Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory's 2025 Tracking the Sun report, the median residential solar price in 2024 was $3.50/watt. A 7 kW system costs approximately $24,500 before incentives. After the 30% federal ITC, the net cost is about $17,150. Prices vary from $2.80/W (competitive Southwest markets) to $4.20/W (Northeast).

What happens during a solar site assessment?

A site assessment evaluates your roof condition, orientation, pitch, and shading. The installer analyzes 12 months of utility bills to size the system, uses NREL PVWatts production data for your location, and identifies any structural upgrades needed. Most assessments are free and take 1-2 hours.

Do I need a permit for solar panel installation?

Yes — virtually all residential installations require building and electrical permits. Your installer handles the application. Processing takes 1-8 weeks depending on your municipality. Most states now have standardized solar permit processes to reduce delays, but timeline varies significantly by local jurisdiction.

What is Permission to Operate (PTO) for solar?

PTO is the utility's formal authorization to activate your solar system and connect to the grid. After your building inspection passes, the utility reviews the interconnection and grants PTO — typically within 1-4 weeks. You cannot legally turn on your solar system until PTO is received.

What are the main components of a solar panel system?

A residential solar system consists of monocrystalline solar panels, an inverter (string, microinverter, or power optimizer + string), mounting racking hardware, wiring and conduit, a utility disconnect switch, and a bidirectional production meter. Battery storage is optional but increasingly common, especially in net-billing states.

How do I choose a solar installer?

Look for NABCEP certification (the industry gold standard), at least 50-100 local installations, 10+ year workmanship warranty, and verifiable references. Get at least 3 quotes — EnergySage data shows homeowners who compare quotes save an average of $5,000-$10,000 over the system's lifetime versus those who accept the first offer.

Can I install solar panels myself?

DIY installation is legal in most states but most utilities require licensed electrical contractors for grid interconnection. DIY voids most manufacturer warranties and carries real safety risks from improper roof penetrations or electrical work. Labor savings are typically 10-15% of total project cost — a modest incentive given the risks on a 25-year investment.
