Solar Panels for RV: Best Kits, Sizing & Installation Guide

/* Open with a real scenario */

"We were boondocking in Moab in July with 200 watts of solar and a cheap PWM controller. By 2pm, our batteries were dead and we had to run the generator for four hours in what should have been a pristine canyon experience. Six months later we upgraded to 600W with an MPPT controller and 200Ah lithium. We haven't touched the generator since."

— A common story in RV forums. System sizing and controller choice matter more than panel brand.

The difference between a frustrating RV solar setup and a liberating one usually comes down to three decisions: how much solar wattage you install, which charge controller you choose, and how much battery capacity you have. This guide gives you a framework for getting all three right, based on your actual usage — not generic "starter kit" recommendations.

How Much Solar Do You Actually Need?

The single most common RV solar mistake is undersizing — either the panels, the batteries, or both. Generic marketing advice like "100W is a great starter kit" is technically true and practically useless. The right answer depends entirely on your daily power consumption and where you'll be traveling.

Step 1: Calculate Your Daily Load in Watt-Hours

Add up every load you run in a day, multiplied by its runtime:

Device Typical Draw Daily Runtime Daily Wh
12V compressor refrigerator 40–60W average 24 hrs (cycling) 960–1,440 Wh
LED lighting (6 fixtures) 30–40W 4 hrs 120–160 Wh
Laptop (charging) 45–65W 3 hrs 135–195 Wh
Phone/tablet charging (2) 20–30W combined 2 hrs 40–60 Wh
CPAP machine 30–60W 8 hrs 240–480 Wh
Water pump (intermittent) 60–80W 0.5 hrs 30–40 Wh
TV/entertainment 80–120W 3 hrs 240–360 Wh
Typical Full-Timer Total — — 1,765–2,735 Wh/day

Step 2: Apply the Sizing Formula

Required solar wattage = Daily load (Wh) ÷ Peak sun hours × 1.25 (safety factor)

Peak sun hours vary significantly by location and season. Use these estimates: Southwest desert (Moab, Sedona, Joshua Tree): 6–7 peak hours/day. Pacific Northwest or cloudy climates: 3–4 hours/day. National average: approximately 4.5 hours/day per NREL's PVWatts calculator.

Example: 2,200 Wh/day daily load ÷ 5 peak sun hours × 1.25 = 550W of solar. This is why most serious full-timers run 400–600W minimums, and why the common "100W starter kit" barely handles charging phones and running LED lights.

The 5 Core Components of an RV Solar System

1. Solar Panels

For RV rooftop applications, rigid monocrystalline panels are the gold standard. They offer 19–22% efficiency, 25+ year lifespan, and cost $0.70–$1.50/watt retail — significantly cheaper than flexible alternatives. Standard residential-format panels (typically 400W in ~72" × 40") are too large for most RV roofs; look for 100W, 175W, or 200W compact panels optimized for RV mounting. Brands like Renogy, Battle Born, and SunPower all manufacture RV-appropriate panel sizes.

2. Charge Controller

The charge controller sits between your panels and batteries, managing the charging process to prevent overcharge and battery damage. The choice between MPPT and PWM is critical — covered in detail in the next section. Size the controller based on your total panel wattage and battery voltage. For a 400W system on a 12V battery bank, you need a controller rated for at least 33 amps (400W ÷ 12V = 33A); choose the next size up (40A) for headroom.

3. Battery Bank

Your battery bank determines how long you can run loads when the sun isn't shining. Rule of thumb: store at least 2 days of daily consumption in your batteries to handle cloudy weather without running a generator. For our 2,200 Wh/day example, that means 4,400 Wh (4.4 kWh) of storage — roughly 370Ah at 12V. With lithium (usable to 80% depth), you need ~465Ah. With AGM (usable to 50% depth), you need 740Ah — nearly twice the physical space and weight.

4. Inverter

Converts DC battery power to 120V AC for appliances, laptops, and power tools. Always choose a pure sine wave inverter — not modified sine wave. Modified sine wave can damage variable-speed motors, CPAP machines, and sensitive electronics. Size to handle your peak AC load with ~25% headroom. Common sizes: 1,000W for light users, 2,000W for general use, 3,000W+ for air conditioning.

5. Wiring, Fusing, and Monitoring

Undersized wiring is a fire risk; oversized wiring is just wasted money. Use an online wire gauge calculator (or the NEC table for 12V DC systems) to determine the correct wire gauge for each run based on amps and distance. Every circuit needs a properly sized fuse or breaker within 18 inches of the battery positive terminal. A battery monitor (Victron SmartShunt, Renogy BT-2) is invaluable for tracking state of charge and catching consumption surprises.

MPPT vs. PWM Charge Controllers: The Real Difference

This is the technical decision that most beginners get wrong — or skip entirely by buying whatever came with a kit. The difference in real-world energy harvest between a PWM and MPPT controller is substantial enough to matter for every system over 300 watts.

Feature PWM Controller MPPT Controller
Efficiency 74–81% 94–96%
Energy harvest vs. PWM Baseline 20–35% more (up to 40% in cold)
Lithium battery compatible No (voltage mismatch risk) Yes (required for lithium)
Works with higher-voltage panels No (must match battery voltage) Yes (steps down voltage)
Cost range $20–$100 $150–$500
Best system size Under 300W 300W and above
Recommended for Simple budget weekend setups Full-timers, lithium users, 400W+

Here is the practical math: a 30A MPPT controller costs roughly $150–$200 more than an equivalent PWM unit. On a 400W system producing 5 peak hours per day, MPPT's 25% efficiency advantage harvests about 500Wh more electricity daily. Over six months of traveling (180 days), that's 90 kWh of extra energy — power that would otherwise require running a generator. The price premium pays back in reduced generator fuel costs within a single season for active users.

Top MPPT controller brands: Victron SmartSolar (premium, Bluetooth-enabled), Renogy Rover Elite (excellent value), EPever Tracer (budget option with solid performance). Avoid cheap unbranded controllers from unknown manufacturers — unreliable MPPT tracking and poor thermal management are common failure modes.

Lithium vs. AGM Batteries: 5-Year Total Cost Comparison

The upfront price difference between lithium (LiFePO4) and AGM batteries looks dramatic. The 5-year total cost of ownership tells a different story.

Spec AGM (100Ah) Lithium LiFePO4 (100Ah)
Upfront cost $150–$250 $400–$900
Usable capacity 50Ah (50% DoD) 80–100Ah (80–100% DoD)
Effective Wh per dollar ~$2.50/Wh usable ~$0.80–$1.50/Wh usable
Cycle life 300–500 cycles 3,000–5,000 cycles
Weight (100Ah) ~65 lbs ~25–30 lbs
Charge speed Slow (C/5 to C/10) Fast (up to 1C)
Replacements needed (5 yr) 1–2 replacements 0 replacements
5-year total cost (200Ah bank) ~$900–$1,500 ~$800–$1,800

For occasional weekend use (50–100 cycles per year), AGM remains competitive. For full-time RVers cycling their batteries daily, lithium reaches its cost crossover point within 2–3 years while delivering far superior weight savings, usable capacity, and charge acceptance. The leading lithium brands for RV use are Battle Born (premium, made in the USA), Renogy (value-priced), and Battleborn's competitor EcoFlow (integrated battery/inverter solutions).

Handles: LED lights, phone/laptop charging, a 12V fan, and small electronics. Does not support a compressor refrigerator full-time (you'll need a cooler or shore power). Estimated system cost installed: $800–$2,500.

400–600W panels | MPPT controller | 100–200Ah lithium | 2,000W inverter
Handles everything above plus a 12V compressor refrigerator, CPAP, and comfortable evening power use without generator anxiety. Can go 2–3 cloudy days before needing a recharge. Estimated system cost installed: $3,500–$6,500.

Full-Time Nomad (200+ nights/year)

600–1,000W panels | MPPT controller | 200–400Ah lithium | 3,000W inverter
Handles everything above plus working from home (multiple monitors, video calls), running a microwave, and short-duration air conditioning in moderate weather. This is a serious system — comparable to a small off-grid cabin setup. Estimated system cost installed: $6,000–$12,000+.

Best RV Solar Kits in 2026

Complete solar kits simplify purchasing by bundling the panels, controller, and sometimes cables and mounts. Below is an honest assessment — including limitations — of the leading kits at each tier.

Budget Tier: Renogy 200W Starter Kit (~$300–$350)

Includes two 100W monocrystalline panels, a 30A PWM controller, cables, and connectors. Renogy has sold more RV solar kits than any other brand and has a strong reputation for reliability. Limitation: The included PWM controller caps efficiency. If you plan to expand beyond 300W or use lithium batteries, upgrade to an MPPT controller immediately. Best for first-time buyers wanting a low-risk entry point with good documentation and customer support.

Mid-Range: Renogy 400W Premium Kit with MPPT (~$600–$750)

Four 100W panels plus a 40A MPPT controller, mounting hardware, and Y-branch connectors for series-parallel wiring. This is the kit I would recommend to most full-time-curious RVers as a starting system that can be expanded. Limitation: Still requires purchasing the battery bank separately. At 400W, it sits at the edge of adequacy for year-round full-timers in cloudy regions.

Premium Tier: Go Power! 480W Complete System (~$1,500–$2,000)

Go Power! is the OEM supplier for many RV manufacturers (Airstream, Tiffin, Winnebago). Their complete system includes 480W of mono panels, a 30A MPPT controller, a 2,000W pure sine wave inverter, and a transfer switch. The transfer switch is a meaningful differentiator — it allows seamless transition between shore power and solar/battery without manual switching. Limitation: Significantly more expensive than comparable Renogy configurations. Better suited for buyers who value professional-grade integration and manufacturer support.

All-in-One: EcoFlow DELTA Pro + Solar Panels (~$2,500–$3,500)

EcoFlow's integrated approach combines a 3.6 kWh LFP battery pack with a built-in 3,600W inverter, MPPT charge controller, and a companion app. Pair with 400–800W of rigid panels for a capable full-time system in one cohesive unit. Limitation: Higher cost per kWh than DIY lithium configurations. Less expandable than modular systems. But for buyers who want plug-and-play simplicity without wiring complexity, it is genuinely excellent.

RV Solar Installation: DIY vs. Professional

DIY installation is accessible for most mechanically comfortable RV owners, but several steps deserve professional involvement.

What DIY Handles Well

  • Mounting flat panels to the roof (Z-brackets or tilt mounts)
  • Running cables from rooftop to interior through a weatherproof cable entry
  • Connecting panels in series or parallel to the charge controller
  • Mounting and wiring the charge controller and battery monitor

Where Professional Help Is Worth It

  • High-current battery wiring: 2/0 AWG cables, 250A+ fuses, and busbar connections carry enough current to cause serious fires if improperly installed.
  • Inverter/charger installation: Integrating with the shore power panel requires proper transfer switching and grounding.
  • Penetrating the roof: A poor seal creates water intrusion and rot — the most common RV maintenance nightmare.

Professional installation at an RV solar specialty shop runs $500–$2,000 in labor for a complete system. Mobile RV solar installers (available in major RV hubs like Quartzsite, AZ; Tucson; and Bend, OR) often cost less and come to your campsite. For electrical safety guidelines, the RVIA (Recreation Vehicle Industry Association) publishes installation standards that licensed installers follow.

Can You Run an RV Air Conditioner on Solar Power?

Yes — but this is where most undersized systems spectacularly fail. A standard 13,500 BTU Dometic or Coleman RV AC unit draws:

  • Startup surge: 2,700–3,500W (lasting 0.5–2 seconds)
  • Running power: 1,200–1,500W continuous
  • Hourly consumption: 1.2–1.5 kWh per hour of cooling

At 5 hours of AC use per day, that is 6–7.5 kWh of daily consumption from the AC alone — before you factor in the rest of your electrical load. To sustain this with solar:

  • 1,200W+ of solar panels (to generate enough energy and keep up with consumption in real time)
  • 200Ah+ of lithium batteries (to handle startup surge and power the AC during brief clouds)
  • 3,000W pure sine wave inverter (for both running power and surge capacity)
  • Micro-Air EasyStart or Dometic SoftStart capacitor ($300–$400) to reduce startup surge from 3,500W to ~700W — dramatically reducing battery stress

With the right soft-start device, a 200Ah lithium bank at 24V can reliably start and run a 13,500 BTU unit. Without soft-start, the same battery may fail to sustain the startup surge. This is a $350 upgrade that most serious solar-powered RVers consider mandatory for AC use.

Wiring, Fusing, and Safety Essentials

Solar and battery systems can deliver thousands of watts of power. Improper wiring is the leading cause of RV electrical fires. The key rules:

  • Fuse every positive wire within 18 inches of the battery. This is not optional. A short circuit without a fuse can cause a catastrophic thermal event in seconds.
  • Use marine-grade tinned copper wire for all DC runs — it resists corrosion better than standard automotive wire.
  • Size wire correctly for the amperage and run length. Undersized wire heats up under load, degrades insulation, and can ignite surrounding materials.
  • Use properly rated MC4 connectors on panel wiring — do not use automotive or household connectors for solar panel runs.
  • Install a battery disconnect switch accessible from outside the battery compartment for emergency shutoff.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many watts of solar do I need for my RV?

Calculate your daily load in watt-hours, divide by your location's peak sun hours, and add 25% safety margin. Weekend travelers typically need 200–300W. Full-timers running a compressor fridge, CPAP, and devices typically need 400–600W minimum. Add 800W+ if you want solar-powered air conditioning.

What is the difference between MPPT and PWM charge controllers?

MPPT controllers operate at 94–96% efficiency and harvest 20–35% more energy than PWM controllers (74–81% efficiency). MPPT costs $150–$500 versus $20–$100 for PWM, but pays back within one season for active users. MPPT is required for lithium batteries and systems over 400W.

Should I get lithium or AGM batteries for RV solar?

Lithium (LiFePO4) offers 3,000–5,000 cycles versus 300–500 for AGM, 60–70% weight savings, and 80–100% usable depth of discharge vs 50% for AGM. For full-timers, lithium's 5-year total cost of ownership is comparable to AGM despite 2–3× higher upfront cost. For occasional weekenders, AGM remains cost-effective.

Can I run an RV air conditioner on solar?

Yes, but it requires 800W+ panels, 200Ah+ lithium, and a 3,000W inverter. A Micro-Air EasyStart or similar soft-start device ($300–$400) reduces AC startup surge from 3,500W to ~700W, making solar-powered cooling far more practical. Without soft-start, most smaller battery banks cannot handle the startup load.

How long does RV solar installation take?

A DIY flat-mount installation of 200–400W takes 4–8 hours for an experienced person. A full system including battery bank, MPPT controller, and inverter typically takes 1–2 full days for a first-timer. Professional installation by an RV solar specialist runs $500–$2,000 in labor for a complete system.

Are flexible solar panels worth it for RVs?

Generally no. Flexible panels cost more per watt, degrade faster (often 20–30% output loss within 5 years from heat buildup against the roof), and are harder to replace. Rigid monocrystalline panels mounted with z-brackets — leaving an air gap for cooling — offer better efficiency, longer lifespan, and lower cost per watt. Reserve flexible panels for curved surfaces where rigid panels physically cannot fit.

What size inverter do I need for my RV?

Size to your peak AC load plus 20–25% headroom. A microwave plus TV plus laptop needs about 1,500–1,800W peak — a 2,000W inverter handles this. For air conditioning, budget 3,000W minimum. Always choose pure sine wave over modified sine wave to protect sensitive electronics and CPAP machines.
