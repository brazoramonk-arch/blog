Solar Tax Credit 2026: How Much & How to Claim the ITC

The 30% federal Investment Tax Credit rewrote the economics of residential solar for millions of American households. Then, on July 4, 2025, Congress eliminated it — seven years ahead of schedule. Here is the definitive guide to what happened, who can still claim it, and whether solar still makes financial sense in a post-ITC world.

/* Key Takeaways */

Key Takeaways

  • • The federal solar ITC (Section 25D) was terminated for new residential installations after December 31, 2025 by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act.
  • • If your system was fully installed and operational by December 31, 2025, you claim the 30% credit on Form 5695 with your 2025 tax filed April 2026).
  • • Unused credits from 2022–2025 installations carry forward indefinitely — they do not disappear because the credit ended.
  • • State incentives (NY, MA, NJ, CT) and net metering still make solar viable in high-rate markets even without the federal credit.
  • • Panel prices have fallen 40%+ since 2020 — the ITC's loss is painful but not the death of residential solar economics.
/* Section 1 */

The Credit That Changed Solar — And Then Vanished

For more than a decade, the federal Investment Tax Credit was the single most powerful driver of residential solar adoption in the United States. The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 locked it in at 30% through 2032, creating a decade of policy certainty that led to record-breaking deployment. According to the Solar Energy Industries Association (SEIA), the U.S. installed 32.4 GW of solar capacity in 2023 alone — a year-over-year increase of 55% — with residential solar representing roughly 4 GW of that total.

Then came the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (Public Law 119-21), signed by President Trump on July 4, 2025. Among hundreds of provisions, Section 70403 of the law terminated the residential clean energy credit under Internal Revenue Code Section 25D for any expenditure made after December 31, 2025 — seven years before the IRA's scheduled phase-out. The solar industry lobbied hard against the change. It didn't matter.

The IRS moved quickly to clarify implementation. In Fact Sheet 2025-05, Treasury confirmed that "placed in service" is the operative standard: a system must be physically installed, connected to the grid or home electrical panel, and operational before January 1, 2026. Signed contracts, deposits, and partially-installed systems as of December 31, 2025 do not qualify. The cutoff is absolute.

Despite the credit's termination for new installations, a significant number of homeowners can and should claim it on their 2025 tax returns. According to SEIA data, the U.S. installed approximately 3.8 GW of residential solar during 2025, representing hundreds of thousands of households that completed installations before the cutoff. What Costs Are Included in the Credit Basis

The credit applies to the total installed system cost, and the IRS is fairly generous in defining what counts. Eligible costs include:

✓ Included in Credit Basis

  • Solar panels / photovoltaic modules
  • Inverters (string, microinverter, hybrid)
  • Mounting racks and hardware
  • All wiring and electrical work
  • Labor for installation
  • Permit and inspection fees
  • Sales tax on equipment
  • Battery storage (≥3 kWh capacity)
  • Solar roof tiles/shingles generating power

✗ Not Included

  • Standard roofing materials
  • Structural reinforcements not part of the solar system
  • Utility rebates (subtract these first)
  • Costs covered by public utility subsidies
  • Financing/interest costs
  • Extended warranty or monitoring plans

Critical rule on utility rebates: If you received an upfront rebate from your utility company, subtract that amount from your cost basis before calculating the 30%. However, state tax credits (like New York's 25% credit) do NOT reduce your federal cost basis — you can claim both the full state credit AND the full federal credit independently.

/* Carryforward Rules */

Carryforward Rules: Your Unused Credit Doesn't Disappear

One of the most misunderstood aspects of the solar tax credit is what happens when your credit exceeds your tax liability in the year of installation. Many homeowners incorrectly assume the excess credit is simply lost. It is not.

The Section 25D credit is nonrefundable but fully carryforward-eligible. The IRS confirmed in its OBBB guidance that carryforward balances earned on qualifying 2022–2025 installations remain valid in 2026 and all subsequent tax years until fully consumed. The termination of the credit for new installations does not cancel existing carryforward balances.

This matters practically for households with moderate tax liability. A retired couple with $5,000 in annual federal tax liability who installed a $25,000 solar system in 2025 receives a $7,500 credit. They use $5,000 in 2025, carry $2,500 to 2026, and apply it against their 2026 liability. If their liability is again $5,000, they use the full $2,500 in 2026 and the carryforward is exhausted.

Planning tip: If you have significant unused carryforward from a 2025 installation, consider whether you can accelerate other income into 2026 or 2027 to create tax liability against which to apply it. Roth IRA conversions, deferred compensation distributions, or selling appreciated assets are common strategies. Consult a tax advisor who understands renewable energy credits.

The picture is not uniformly grim. In high-rate states, solar remains a strong financial decision even without federal support. SEIA projects 2026 residential solar capacity additions will decline approximately 18–22% versus 2025 — a significant but not catastrophic contraction, particularly given the decade of installed base that continues producing clean energy and savings.

Panel prices are also an important variable. According to NREL's Q4 2025 Residential Solar Cost Benchmark, installed system costs fell to $2.82/watt — down from $3.10/watt in 2023. This trend is structural and ongoing. By 2028, NREL projects costs could reach $2.50/watt or below, which partially compensates for the lost subsidy. The ITC's departure accelerated these efficiency pressures on installers.

Beyond state credits, consider net metering and time-of-use rate optimization. In states with TOU rates, a solar+battery system can generate additional savings by exporting to the grid during peak-rate hours (often $0.40–0.55/kWh in California) while charging the battery during off-peak hours ($0.15–0.20/kWh). This arbitrage is now the primary financial driver for battery adoption in California given the post-SGIP, post-ITC landscape.

/* Battery Storage Credit */

What About Battery Storage: Did the ITC Apply?

One of the most significant expansions in the Inflation Reduction Act was making standalone battery storage systems eligible for the Section 25D credit — without requiring solar panel pairing. Before 2023, batteries only qualified if installed with solar simultaneously. The IRA removed that restriction for batteries of at least 3 kWh usable capacity.

This meant homeowners who installed a Tesla Powerwall 3 ($15,300 installed) in 2025 as a standalone system received a $4,590 federal credit. For a Franklin aPower 2 ($17,000 installed), the credit was worth $5,100. These were meaningful savings that accelerated battery adoption significantly.

The OBBB terminated this eligibility along with solar. Batteries installed on or after January 1, 2026 are no longer eligible for the residential clean energy credit. The commercial Section 48E credit continues for non-residential installations, but homeowner-owned residential batteries have no federal incentive path in 2026.

State battery incentives remain in some markets. California's SGIP general market budget closed at year-end 2025, but New York's NYSERDA offers $200/kWh upfront for residential batteries up to 25 kWh. For more detail on battery economics, see our home battery buying guide.

/* Commercial ITC */

The Commercial ITC: A Different Timeline

While the residential credit was eliminated, the commercial and utility-scale Investment Tax Credit under Section 48E survives — for now. The OBBB modified its timeline rather than immediately eliminating it. Projects must begin construction by July 4, 2026 and be placed in service by December 31, 2027 to qualify.

This distinction matters for certain homeowners in unusual situations. Community solar subscriptions — where you subscribe to a share of a solar farm rather than installing panels on your roof — may be developed under commercial ITC rules. Farmers installing commercial-scale systems may qualify under 48E with the right structure. These are niche but potentially valuable pathways worth exploring with a solar tax specialist.

For the vast majority of residential homeowners considering rooftop solar in 2026, the commercial ITC is not directly relevant. The residential solar calculation must now stand on its own merits: electricity rate, sun hours, system cost, net metering policy, and state incentives.
