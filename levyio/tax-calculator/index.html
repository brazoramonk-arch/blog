Free Tax Calculator: Estimate Your Federal & State Taxes

Estimating your taxes before you file—or before the year ends—is one of the highest-value financial exercises you can do. A tax calculator translates your income, filing status, deductions, and credits into a concrete number: what you owe, what your effective rate is, and whether you are over- or under-withheld. This guide explains exactly what inputs a tax calculator needs, how the IRS computes federal income tax step by step, what the 2026 standard deduction amounts are, how to read your results, and when running a tax estimate can save you from costly surprises.

/* Article Body */ /* Key Takeaways */

Key Takeaways

  • Federal income tax is calculated on taxable income—your gross income minus above-the-line deductions minus your standard or itemized deduction.
  • The 2026 standard deduction is $15,000 (single), $30,000 (married filing jointly), and $22,500 (head of household), per IRS Revenue Procedure 2025-28.
  • Your marginal rate is the rate on your last dollar of income; your effective rate is total tax ÷ total income—always lower than the marginal rate.
  • Self-employed filers add 15.3% self-employment tax (on net earnings up to $176,100, then 2.9% above) on top of income tax—but can deduct half of SE tax from gross income.
  • Tax credits reduce your bill dollar-for-dollar; deductions only reduce the income that is taxed, making credits more valuable per dollar.
  • IRS Publication 505 governs withholding and estimated taxes; IRS Publication 17 is the comprehensive individual tax guide for each filing year.

What a Tax Calculator Needs: The Essential Inputs

A reliable tax calculator is only as accurate as the information you feed it. The IRS uses Form 1040 and its accompanying schedules to collect every piece of data that affects your tax liability. Understanding what each input does—and why it matters—makes your estimate far more precise than a rough back-of-envelope figure.

The table below maps the key inputs to their function and the relevant IRS authority. Every line on this table corresponds to something you would find on Form 1040 or the Form 1040 Instructions, which run more than 100 pages and are updated each filing season. Using the Income Tax Calculator captures all of these inputs in a single screen.

How Federal Income Tax Is Calculated: Step by Step

The IRS describes the full computation in IRS Publication 17 and in the Form 1040 Instructions. The process has six distinct steps. Working through each one manually—even once—permanently improves your understanding of how every dollar you earn, save, or give affects your tax bill.

Step 1: Calculate Gross Income

Gross income is all income from whatever source derived, unless specifically excluded by the Internal Revenue Code. It includes W-2 wages, self-employment income, freelance payments reported on 1099-NEC, interest and dividends, capital gains, rental income, alimony received under pre-2019 agreements, gambling winnings, and even bartered services. Per IRS Publication 17, Chapter 1, almost everything of economic value counts unless Congress has explicitly excluded it—such as employer-paid health insurance premiums, gifts, and most life insurance proceeds.

Step 2: Subtract Above-the-Line Deductions to Get AGI

Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) is your gross income minus a specific list of "above-the-line" deductions found on Schedule 1, Part II of Form 1040. These deductions are available regardless of whether you itemize. The most common ones include: contributions to a traditional 401(k) (up to $23,500 for 2026), deductible traditional IRA contributions (up to $7,000 if you qualify), HSA contributions ($4,300 single / $8,550 family), the student loan interest deduction (up to $2,500), the self-employment tax deduction (50% of SE tax paid), and self-employed health insurance premiums.

AGI is a critical number that appears on line 11 of Form 1040. Many other tax limits and phase-outs are tied to AGI—including the ability to deduct IRA contributions, the phase-out of the Child Tax Credit, and eligibility for the Roth IRA contribution limit. Reducing your AGI is often more valuable than reducing taxable income after the standard deduction, because AGI reductions ripple through multiple calculations.

Step 3: Subtract the Standard Deduction (or Itemized Deductions)

After computing AGI, you subtract either the standard deduction or the total of your itemized deductions from Schedule A—whichever is larger. For most filers, the standard deduction wins by a wide margin. For 2026, per IRS Revenue Procedure 2025-28, the standard deduction amounts are:

  • Single / Married Filing Separately: $15,000
  • Married Filing Jointly / Qualifying Surviving Spouse: $30,000
  • Head of Household: $22,500
  • Additional amount for age 65+ or blind (single): $2,000
  • Additional amount for age 65+ or blind (married): $1,600 per qualifying person

The standard deduction has increased significantly since the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017. Before TCJA, the 2017 standard deduction was $6,350 for single filers. The higher standard deduction made itemizing worthwhile for far fewer taxpayers. According to the Congressional Budget Office (CBO), approximately 90% of filers now take the standard deduction. Itemizing only makes sense if your Schedule A deductions—mortgage interest, state and local taxes (capped at $10,000 by TCJA under IRC §164(b)(6)), charitable contributions, and unreimbursed medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI—exceed your standard deduction threshold.

For a deeper dive into whether to itemize, see the complete tax deductions guide.

Step 4: Apply the Tax Brackets to Taxable Income

Taxable income is what remains after steps 1–3. This is the number that enters the seven marginal rate brackets. The US tax system is progressive and marginal: each layer of income is taxed only at the rate for that bracket. The 2026 brackets for single filers, per IRS Revenue Procedure 2025-28, are 10% on income up to $11,925; 12% on $11,926–$48,475; 22% on $48,476–$103,350; 24% on $103,351–$197,300; 32% on $197,301–$250,525; 35% on $250,526–$626,350; and 37% above $626,350. For the full breakdown by filing status, see the 2026 tax brackets guide or use the Tax Bracket Calculator.

Step 5: Subtract Tax Credits

After computing your tax from the brackets, you subtract any tax credits you qualify for. Credits reduce your actual tax owed dollar-for-dollar—a $2,000 credit reduces your tax bill by exactly $2,000. Common credits include the Child Tax Credit (up to $2,000 per qualifying child, with up to $1,700 refundable as the Additional Child Tax Credit), the Child and Dependent Care Credit, the American Opportunity Tax Credit (up to $2,500 per student for the first four years of college), and the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), which can be worth over $7,000 for large families with low-to-moderate income. For a full analysis of credits for families, see the Child Tax Credit guide.

Step 6: Compare to Withholding and Estimated Payments

Your final step is subtracting what you have already paid. If your employer withheld federal income tax from your paychecks throughout the year (shown on Form W-2, Box 2), that is a prepayment of your tax liability. If you made quarterly estimated tax payments (using Form 1040-ES), those are also credits against your liability. If your total prepayments exceed your tax liability, you receive a refund. If your liability exceeds prepayments, you owe the difference when you file—and if the underpayment exceeds certain thresholds, you may also owe an underpayment penalty under IRC §6654, as detailed in IRS Publication 505.

/* Section 3: Worked Example */

Worked Calculation Example: Single Filer, $85,000 Gross Income

Let's walk through the complete calculation for a concrete taxpayer. This example assumes a single filer with $85,000 in W-2 wages, a $6,000 traditional IRA contribution (deductible because they are not covered by a workplace plan above the income phase-out), and no itemized deductions exceeding the standard deduction.

Single Filer — $85,000 Gross W-2 Wages, 2026

Step 1 — Gross Income: $85,000

Step 2 — Above-the-line deductions: Traditional IRA contribution $6,000 → AGI = $79,000

Step 3 — Standard deduction (single, 2026): $15,000 → Taxable Income = $64,000

Step 4 — Apply brackets to $64,000:

10% on $0–$11,925 = $1,192.50

12% on $11,926–$48,475 ($36,549) = $4,385.88

22% on $48,476–$64,000 ($15,524) = $3,415.28

Total Federal Income Tax: $8,993.66

Marginal Rate: 22%  |  Effective Rate (on taxable income): 14.1%  |  Effective Rate (on gross income): 10.6%

Note: This filer still owes FICA taxes: 6.2% Social Security on wages up to $176,100 ($5,270) + 1.45% Medicare ($1,232.50) = $6,502.50 withheld by employer. Total federal tax burden: ~$15,496, or 18.2% of gross income.

This worked example reveals several important dynamics. First, the $6,000 IRA contribution saved this filer $6,000 × 22% = $1,320 in federal income tax, because it moved $6,000 out of taxable income. Second, despite being in the 22% marginal bracket, the effective income tax rate on gross wages is only 10.6%—a number most people underestimate when planning cash flow. Third, FICA taxes are a significant, often overlooked component of total federal tax burden. The Income Tax Calculator accounts for all of these layers simultaneously.

/* Section 4: Effective vs Marginal Rate */

Reading Your Results: Effective Rate vs. Marginal Rate

Every tax calculator worth using gives you two rates: your marginal rate and your effective rate. Understanding the difference is essential for making sound financial decisions about raises, Roth conversions, deductions, and timing of income.

Your marginal tax rate is the federal income tax rate that applies to your next dollar of ordinary income. If your taxable income is $90,000 as a single filer, your marginal rate is 22%—that is the bracket you are in. Every additional dollar of ordinary income you earn this year is taxed at 22%, until you hit $103,350. This is the rate that should drive your decisions about Roth vs. traditional contributions, timing a bonus, or taking on additional freelance work.

Your effective tax rate is your total federal income tax divided by your total taxable income (or sometimes gross income—calculators differ on which denominator they use; make sure you know which one you are looking at). The effective rate is always lower than the marginal rate in a progressive system because the lower brackets apply to the bottom layers of income. In the worked example above, the marginal rate was 22% but the effective rate on gross income was only 10.6%.

The Tax Foundation notes in its annual analyses that high-income taxpayers often conflate their marginal rate with what they actually pay, leading to misconceptions about tax burden. The effective rate is what you actually pay; the marginal rate is what the next dollar costs. Both numbers are important—but for different decisions.

A third figure to track is your combined marginal rate—your federal marginal rate plus your state marginal rate. If you live in a high-tax state, the combined rate may surprise you. A California resident in the 24% federal bracket and the 9.3% California bracket faces a combined marginal rate of 33.3% on ordinary income. Use the state tax comparison guide to see how your state stacks up.

/* Section 5: State Tax */

State Tax Considerations

Federal income tax is only part of the picture. Forty-one states plus the District of Columbia impose a state income tax, and the structures vary dramatically. Nine states—Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (on wages only), South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming—impose no tax on ordinary earned income. At the other extreme, California's top marginal rate is 13.3% on income over $1 million, while New York City residents face a combined state and city income tax rate that can approach 14.8% at the top.

State tax calculations differ from federal in several important ways. Some states conform to federal AGI as their starting point; others use their own definitions of income. Some states allow the same standard deduction as the federal return; others have separate deduction amounts or do not allow itemizing at all. States also differ on the treatment of retirement income—many states exempt Social Security benefits and some exempt pension income entirely.

The Tax Foundation's annual State Business Tax Climate Index and its individual income tax data show that state income tax can represent 20–40% of a taxpayer's total income tax burden in high-tax states. For a taxpayer in New York City earning $150,000, state and city income tax could add $10,000–$15,000 on top of the federal bill, dramatically affecting real take-home pay. The Income Tax Calculator lets you select your state to include state income tax in your total estimate.

If you recently moved between states, or work remotely for an employer in a different state, your state tax situation may be more complex—see the state tax comparison guide for details on nexus, domicile, and multi-state filing obligations.

/* Section 6: Self-Employment */

Self-Employment Tax: An Additional Layer

If you earn net self-employment income of $400 or more in a tax year, you are subject to self-employment (SE) tax in addition to income tax. SE tax exists because self-employed individuals are both the employer and the employee for FICA purposes. Employees have their Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%) taxes split equally with their employer; self-employed individuals pay the full 15.3% themselves on Schedule SE.

For 2026, SE tax applies at 15.3% on net self-employment earnings up to the Social Security wage base of $176,100, and at 2.9% (Medicare only) on earnings above that threshold. An additional 0.9% Medicare surtax applies to earned income above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly) under the Affordable Care Act—this applies to both employees and self-employed individuals.

There is a compensating deduction: you may deduct 50% of your SE tax as an above-the-line deduction on Schedule 1, which reduces your AGI and therefore your income tax. However, this deduction does not reduce your SE tax itself. On a net self-employment income of $80,000, SE tax would be approximately $11,304 (after the 92.35% net earnings adjustment per IRS Form SE instructions), and the deductible portion would be $5,652, reducing federal taxable income by that amount.

Self-employed individuals also owe quarterly estimated taxes under IRC §6654 and IRS Publication 505 if they expect to owe $1,000 or more when they file. Failing to make estimated payments on time results in an underpayment penalty, even if you pay the full amount by April 15. The self-employment tax guide covers the full SE tax calculation, deductions available to freelancers, and the quarterly payment schedule.

/* Section 7: Credits vs Deductions */

Tax Credits vs. Tax Deductions: A Critical Distinction

Tax professionals often need to correct a persistent misconception: that a $1,000 deduction and a $1,000 credit are equivalent. They are not. A deduction reduces the income subject to tax; a credit reduces the tax itself. The after-tax value of a deduction depends entirely on your marginal tax rate.

Consider a filer in the 22% marginal bracket. A $1,000 deduction reduces their taxable income by $1,000, which reduces their tax by $1,000 × 22% = $220. A $1,000 tax credit reduces their tax by the full $1,000—more than four times as valuable as the same-dollar deduction. This is why the Child Tax Credit ($2,000 per qualifying child), the EITC, and education credits are so powerful in tax planning.

Credits come in three types: nonrefundable (can reduce your tax to zero but not below), refundable (can generate a refund even if you owe no tax—the EITC and the refundable portion of the Child Tax Credit work this way), and partially refundable (such as the American Opportunity Credit, which is 40% refundable up to $1,000). IRS Publication 17, Chapters 32–37, provides the full eligibility rules and phase-out thresholds for each major credit.

For planning purposes: if you are choosing between a pre-tax deduction (like a traditional 401(k)) and an after-tax option (like a Roth 401(k)), your marginal rate is the key variable. At a high marginal rate, the current-year deduction is more valuable. At a low marginal rate (early career, low-income years), the Roth's tax-free growth may win. The tax deductions guide covers the most valuable deductions available to W-2 employees and self-employed filers.

/* Section 8: Married Filing */

Filing Status and Its Impact on Your Calculator Results

Filing status is one of the most consequential inputs in a tax calculator. It determines which bracket thresholds apply, the size of your standard deduction, and eligibility for certain credits and deductions. Choosing the wrong status—or failing to optimize between Married Filing Jointly and Married Filing Separately—can cost thousands of dollars.

For married couples, the question of filing jointly versus separately arises most commonly when one spouse has significant medical expenses, large miscellaneous deductions, or income-sensitive deductions that phase out at higher combined AGI levels. In most cases, Married Filing Jointly results in a lower combined tax bill because the joint brackets are wider, the standard deduction is double the single amount, and many credits (EITC, education credits, AOTC) are unavailable to Married Filing Separately filers. The MFJ vs. MFS comparison guide walks through the scenarios where filing separately can help.

Head of Household status is available to unmarried filers who paid more than half the cost of maintaining a home for a qualifying person for more than half the year. Head of Household filers get more favorable bracket thresholds and a larger standard deduction ($22,500 vs. $15,000 for single in 2026), making it significantly more valuable than Single status for qualifying filers.

/* Section 9: Common Mistakes */

Common Mistakes When Estimating Taxes

Even experienced filers make errors when running a tax estimate. Here are the most common mistakes to avoid—each one can cause your estimate to be off by hundreds or thousands of dollars:

  • Using gross income instead of taxable income. Your tax is computed on taxable income, not gross wages. Forgetting the standard deduction makes your estimate $1,500–$4,500 too high depending on your bracket.
  • Ignoring self-employment tax. SE tax can add 14.1% to the effective tax rate on net self-employment income. Freelancers who only model income tax frequently underestimate their total bill significantly.
  • Omitting above-the-line deductions. If you contributed to a 401(k) or IRA, those contributions reduce AGI and therefore taxable income. Leaving them out overstates your liability.
  • Applying your marginal rate to all income. The progressive bracket structure means the effective rate is always lower. Applying 22% to $80,000 of taxable income gives $17,600—the actual tax is closer to $13,000.
  • Forgetting capital gains have separate rates. Long-term capital gains are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20%, not at ordinary income rates. Including capital gains in ordinary income overstates tax significantly for investors.
  • Not accounting for the SALT cap. The $10,000 cap on state and local tax deductions (under TCJA) means high-earners in high-tax states cannot deduct the full amount, even if they itemize.
  • Missing the Additional Medicare Surtax. High earners (over $200,000 single, $250,000 married) owe an additional 0.9% Medicare tax and a 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) on investment income above the thresholds. These are separate from the regular income tax calculation.
  • Assuming last year's withholding is adequate. A major income change—raise, new job, sale of stock, freelance income—requires recalculating withholding using the IRS withholding estimator or Form W-4, as described in IRS Publication 505.
/* Section 10: When to Use */

When to Use a Tax Calculator

A tax calculator is not just a pre-filing tool. There are at least nine situations where running an estimate mid-year or before year-end can make a material difference in your financial outcomes.

Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

If you earn self-employment income, freelance income, rental income, significant investment income, or any income without withholding, you are generally required to pay estimated taxes quarterly. For 2026, the due dates under IRS Publication 505 are April 15 (Q1), June 16 (Q2), September 15 (Q3), and January 15, 2027 (Q4). Using a calculator to estimate your annual liability—then dividing by four—lets you size each quarterly payment correctly. Underpaying triggers a penalty under IRC §6654, calculated based on the federal short-term rate plus 3 percentage points.

Withholding Adequacy Checks

If you had a major life change—marriage, divorce, new child, job change, side income, or a large investment gain—your withholding may no longer be calibrated to your actual liability. Running a midyear tax estimate tells you whether you need to file a new Form W-4 with your employer to increase or decrease withholding. This prevents both a large balance-due bill in April (with potential penalties) and a large refund (which is an interest-free loan to the IRS). IRS Publication 505, Chapter 1, provides the safe harbor rules: you generally avoid underpayment penalties if you pay at least 90% of the current year's tax or 100% of last year's tax (110% if your prior-year AGI exceeded $150,000).

Year-End Tax Planning

Running a tax calculator in October or November—while there is still time to act—lets you identify opportunities to reduce your liability before December 31. You might max out your 401(k) or HSA contributions, accelerate charitable donations, harvest capital losses, or defer a year-end invoice. Each of these actions affects taxable income, and a calculator tells you exactly how much each one saves. This is the most high-leverage use of a tax estimate: every dollar of deduction you find in November is real money saved before the year closes.

Roth Conversion Planning

A Roth conversion adds ordinary income to your current-year tax return equal to the converted amount. Running a tax calculator lets you model how much you can convert before pushing into a higher bracket. For example, if your taxable income is $40,000 as a single filer in 2026 and the 12%/22% bracket boundary is at $48,475, you could convert up to $8,475 of traditional IRA assets to Roth at the 12% rate—a strategically advantageous move if you expect higher rates in retirement.

Evaluating a Job Offer or Freelance Rate

When evaluating compensation, knowing your marginal rate lets you compare after-tax income across offers. An employee earning $95,000 in a no-income-tax state versus $100,000 in California may actually take home more in the lower-paying job once state income tax, Medicare surtax, and other factors are considered. A tax calculator makes this comparison concrete. For self-employed rate-setting, incorporating SE tax into the calculation is essential—a freelancer billing $60/hour net has a meaningfully different after-tax outcome than a W-2 employee earning the same gross.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is an online tax calculator?

A well-built tax calculator using current IRS brackets, the correct standard deduction from IRS Revenue Procedure 2025-28, and accurate income and deduction inputs can produce an estimate within 1–3% of your actual liability. Accuracy degrades if you have complex situations: alternative minimum tax exposure, foreign income, multi-state filing, or pass-through business income subject to the Section 199A deduction. For complex returns, use the estimate as a starting point and consult a CPA.

What is the standard deduction for 2026?

Per IRS Revenue Procedure 2025-28, the 2026 standard deduction is $15,000 for single filers and married filing separately, $30,000 for married filing jointly and qualifying surviving spouses, and $22,500 for head of household. Filers age 65 or older or blind receive an additional $2,000 (single) or $1,600 per qualifying person (married), added to the base standard deduction amount.

Do I owe taxes on freelance income even if I did not get a 1099?

Yes. Under IRC §61 and IRS Publication 17, all income is taxable regardless of whether you receive a 1099-NEC. The 1099 reporting threshold ($600 for most payers) is a payer obligation, not a taxpayer exemption. If you earn $400 or more in net self-employment income, you are required to file Schedule SE and pay self-employment tax in addition to income tax. Failing to report income is a tax law violation regardless of 1099 status.

What is the difference between a tax deduction and a tax credit?

A deduction reduces the income subject to tax; its value depends on your marginal rate. A $1,000 deduction saves a 22% bracket filer $220. A credit reduces the actual tax owed dollar-for-dollar; a $1,000 credit saves exactly $1,000 regardless of bracket. Credits are therefore more valuable per dollar than deductions. Refundable credits—like the EITC—can generate a refund even when no income tax is owed.

How do I calculate quarterly estimated tax payments?

Estimate your total annual tax liability using a tax calculator, then subtract expected withholding. Divide the remaining estimated liability by four and pay each installment by the IRS quarterly deadlines (April 15, June 16, September 15, January 15). Per IRS Publication 505, you avoid underpayment penalties by paying at least 90% of the current year's tax or 100% of last year's total tax liability (110% if prior-year AGI exceeded $150,000).

Does a large tax refund mean my withholding is set correctly?

No. A large refund means you over-withheld throughout the year—you gave the IRS an interest-free loan. While some taxpayers prefer refunds for forced saving, optimally you should aim to owe a small amount or break even. Use the IRS Tax Withholding Estimator (described in IRS Publication 505, §1) and update your Form W-4 with your employer to better match withholding to actual liability.

What income is not included in federal taxable income?

Several income types are excluded from federal gross income under the Internal Revenue Code: employer-paid health insurance premiums (IRC §106), qualified HSA distributions for medical expenses (IRC §223), life insurance proceeds (IRC §101), most gifts and inheritances (IRC §102), municipal bond interest (IRC §103), and Roth IRA qualified distributions (IRC §408A). IRS Publication 17 lists all exclusions in detail. State tax treatment of these exclusions may differ.
