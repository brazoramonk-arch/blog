Front-loading your contribution early in the year means more months of tax-free compounding — according to Fidelity's 2025 retirement research, investors who fund IRAs in January historically accumulate 10–15% more over 30 years compared to those who wait until the April deadline.

Earned Income Requirement

A frequently violated rule: your Roth IRA contribution cannot exceed your earned income for the year. Earned income includes wages, salaries, tips, net self-employment income, alimony received under pre-2019 divorce agreements, and non-taxable combat pay. It does not include pension distributions, Social Security, 401(k) withdrawals, rental income, or investment income (dividends, capital gains, interest).

A 28-year-old who earns only $4,200 from part-time work can contribute a maximum of $4,200 — not $7,500. A retiree receiving only Social Security and pension income cannot contribute to a Roth IRA at all. One important exception: a spousal IRA allows a non-working spouse to contribute based on the working spouse's earned income, provided they file jointly and the working spouse has sufficient earned income. The same $7,500 limit applies per spouse.

What to Do When You Exceed the Income Limit

High earners above $168,000 (single) or $252,000 (MFJ) have two primary options to still access Roth-like benefits:

Option 1: Backdoor Roth IRA

The two-step process involves making a nondeductible contribution to a Traditional IRA (no income limit applies to nondeductible contributions), then converting that Traditional IRA to a Roth. The conversion is taxable only on any pre-tax balance in the IRA at the time of conversion, per the pro-rata rule (IRC Section 408(d)(2)). If you have no other pre-tax IRA funds, the conversion is essentially tax-free. See our complete backdoor Roth guide for step-by-step instructions.

Option 2: Roth 401(k) or After-Tax 401(k)

If your employer offers a Roth 401(k), there are no income limits — you can designate any portion of your elective deferral as Roth regardless of income. The 2026 employee deferral limit is $24,500 ($31,000 for those 50+, or up to $35,750 for the SECURE 2.0 super catch-up for ages 60–63). Check our 401(k) contribution limits guide for the full breakdown.

Excess Contribution Penalties and How to Fix Them

If you contribute more than the allowed amount — whether because your income was higher than expected, you changed jobs mid-year, or you forgot to account for all IRA contributions — the excess is subject to a 6% excise tax per year under IRC Section 4973 for every year the excess remains in the account.

The fix is to withdraw the excess contribution along with the net income attributable to it before the tax filing deadline (April 15, 2027 for a 2026 contribution). The earnings are taxable and may be subject to the 10% early distribution penalty if you are under 59½. If you miss the April deadline, you must file Form 5329 with your return and pay the 6% penalty, but you still correct the excess for future years by withdrawing it or applying it to the following year's contribution limit.

A common cause of unintentional excess contributions: income spikes late in the year. A bonus in December, a profitable stock sale, or an unexpected consulting payment can push MAGI over the threshold after you have already contributed the full $7,500. If your income is variable, consider waiting until year-end to confirm your MAGI before contributing — or contribute throughout the year and be prepared to recharacterize or withdraw if needed.

Roth IRA vs. Traditional IRA: The 2026 Decision Framework

The Roth vs. Traditional choice is fundamentally a tax-rate bet. A Roth IRA makes sense when you expect your tax rate in retirement to equal or exceed your current rate — you pay tax now at the lower rate, then enjoy tax-free withdrawals later. A Traditional IRA makes sense when you expect to be in a lower bracket in retirement. For a detailed comparison with worked examples and a side-by-side table, see our Traditional IRA vs Roth IRA guide.

One structural advantage of the Roth IRA that has nothing to do with tax rates: no Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs). Under SECURE 2.0, Roth 401(k) accounts also eliminated RMDs starting in 2024, but for Traditional IRAs, RMDs begin at age 73. If you do not need the money at 73, the RMD forces you to take taxable income whether you want it or not. The Roth IRA has no such obligation, making it a superior vehicle for estate planning and legacy wealth transfer.

A lesser-known Roth advantage: tax diversification. If you hold both Traditional and Roth accounts in retirement, you can choose which to tap each year to manage your taxable income — staying within a target bracket, avoiding Social Security taxation thresholds, or managing Medicare IRMAA surcharges. According to the Tax Foundation's 2025 analysis of retirement income sources, retirees with tax-diversified portfolios pay an estimated 18–22% less in lifetime taxes than those concentrated entirely in pre-tax accounts.

Roth IRA Contribution Limits: The 5-Year Rules

Roth IRA withdrawals have two distinct 5-year rules that catch many taxpayers off guard:

5-Year Rule #1 (for qualified distributions): To withdraw earnings tax-free, your Roth IRA must have been open for at least 5 tax years, AND you must be 59½ or older, disabled, or using the first-home exception. The 5-year clock starts January 1 of the first year you make any Roth IRA contribution. If you open your first Roth in April 2026 and designate it as a 2026 contribution, the clock starts January 1, 2026 — the 5-year mark is January 1, 2031.

5-Year Rule #2 (for conversions): Each Roth conversion has its own 5-year holding period. If you convert a Traditional IRA to Roth in 2026 and withdraw those converted funds before 2031 and before age 59½, you owe the 10% early withdrawal penalty on the converted amount (but not ordinary income tax, since you paid that at conversion). This rule is account-specific and applies per conversion — a conversion done in 2024 has a different clock than one done in 2026.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the 2026 Roth IRA contribution limit?

The 2026 Roth IRA contribution limit is $7,500 for savers under age 50, up from $7,000 in 2025. Savers age 50 and older can contribute up to $8,600 ($7,500 plus the $1,100 catch-up contribution, which was indexed for inflation for the first time under SECURE 2.0). The limit applies across all your IRAs combined — Traditional and Roth.

Can I contribute to a Roth IRA if I earn over $168,000 (single)?

You cannot make a direct Roth IRA contribution once your MAGI exceeds the phase-out ceiling ($168,000 for single filers in 2026). However, the backdoor Roth IRA — contributing to a nondeductible Traditional IRA then converting — remains legal and unrestricted by income. Many high earners use this strategy annually. You'll need to file Form 8606 to document the nondeductible basis and report the conversion.

What income counts toward the Roth IRA limit calculation?

The Roth IRA income limit uses Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI), which for most people equals AGI from Form 1040 line 11. MAGI adds back certain deductions: student loan interest, IRA deductions, and excluded foreign earned income. Pre-tax 401(k) and 403(b) contributions, HSA contributions, and self-employed plan contributions reduce AGI and MAGI, giving high earners a potential planning lever.

Can I contribute to both a Roth IRA and a Roth 401(k)?

Yes, and this is often the optimal strategy. The $7,500 Roth IRA limit and the $24,500 Roth 401(k) elective deferral limit are completely separate. A high earner can designate their full 401(k) deferral as Roth ($24,500), then add a backdoor Roth IRA contribution ($7,500), sheltering $32,000 in Roth accounts annually — none of which will be subject to RMDs or ordinary income tax in retirement.

What happens if I accidentally over-contribute to a Roth IRA?

Excess contributions are subject to a 6% excise tax per year under IRC Section 4973. Fix it by withdrawing the excess plus net income attributable to it before the April 15, 2027 deadline. The earnings are taxable and may be subject to the 10% early distribution penalty if you're under 59½. If you don't withdraw in time, file Form 5329, pay the 6% penalty, and remove the excess in 2027 to stop future penalties.

Does contributing to a 401(k) affect my Roth IRA limit?

A 401(k) does not reduce your Roth IRA contribution limit — the limits are independent. However, pre-tax 401(k) contributions reduce your MAGI, which can actually help you qualify for a larger Roth IRA contribution if you're in the phase-out range. A single filer earning $157,000 who contributes $6,000 to a traditional 401(k) brings MAGI to $151,000, potentially allowing a near-full Roth IRA contribution.

What is the deadline for a 2026 Roth IRA contribution?

The deadline is April 15, 2027. This is not extended by filing a tax extension. Contributions made between January 1 and April 15, 2027 must be clearly designated as 2026 contributions — most brokerages ask you to specify during the transaction. Failing to designate the tax year correctly can cause an excess contribution for the intended year and a miss for the current year.
