Sales Tax by State 2026: Complete Rate Guide

Sales tax in the United States varies dramatically from state to state, and even within states due to local taxes. Five states have no sales tax at all, while combined rates in some areas exceed 11%. This comprehensive guide covers every state's rate, local tax considerations, exemptions, and how online purchases are taxed after the Wayfair decision.

/* Article Body */

How Sales Tax Works in the U.S.

Unlike many countries that use a value-added tax (VAT) applied at each stage of production, the U.S. uses a retail sales tax collected only at the point of final sale to the consumer. There is no federal sales tax. Each state sets its own rate, and many allow cities, counties, and special districts to add additional taxes on top.

This means the total sales tax rate you pay depends on where the purchase is made, not where you live (with some exceptions for use tax). A purchase in Portland, Oregon has no sales tax, while the same item bought in Chicago, Illinois could be taxed at over 10%. Sales tax impacts cost of living — a key factor when comparing salary offers across states. The complexity of overlapping jurisdictions means there are over 13,000 distinct sales tax rates across the country.

Sales tax is calculated as a percentage of the sale price and is typically added at the register. The formula is straightforward: Sale Price x Tax Rate = Sales Tax Amount. For a $100 item in a location with an 8.25% combined rate, the total at checkout would be $108.25. Use the Sales Tax Calculator to compute exact amounts for any purchase in any state.

It is important to note that sales tax is distinct from excise taxes, which are levied on specific goods like gasoline, alcohol, tobacco, and firearms. Excise taxes are usually included in the listed price rather than added at the register. When you see the price of gas at the pump, for example, it already includes federal and state excise taxes. Sales tax may be applied on top of the excise-tax-inclusive price in some states, creating a "tax on a tax" effect.

States With No Sales Tax

Five states impose no state-level sales tax at all:

  • Alaska -- No state tax, but allows local jurisdictions to impose sales taxes up to 7.5%. The average local rate is about 1.82%.
  • Delaware -- No state or local sales tax. Delaware does impose a gross receipts tax on businesses, which may be partially passed to consumers through pricing.
  • Montana -- No general sales tax. Some resort communities can impose a small local resort tax up to 3%.
  • New Hampshire -- No general sales tax. However, it does tax prepared meals and hotel rooms at 8.5%.
  • Oregon -- No state or local sales tax. Oregon relies heavily on income tax instead.

These states make up for the lack of sales tax revenue through other means: higher property taxes, income taxes, or unique taxes like Alaska's oil revenue sharing and Delaware's corporate franchise taxes. Highest and Lowest Combined Sales Tax Rates

When factoring in local taxes, the effective rate consumers pay can differ significantly from the state rate alone. The highest combined sales tax rates in 2026 are found in:

  • Louisiana: 9.55% average combined (4.45% state + 5.10% local)
  • Tennessee: 9.55% average combined (7.00% state + 2.55% local)
  • Arkansas: 9.47% average combined (6.50% state + 2.97% local)
  • Alabama: 9.27% average combined (4.00% state + 5.27% local)
  • Washington: 9.17% average combined (6.50% state + 2.67% local)

Excluding the five no-sales-tax states, the lowest combined rates belong to Hawaii (4.50%), Wyoming (5.36%), Wisconsin (5.44%), and Maine (5.50%). These states either have low base rates, minimal local taxes, or both.

At the specific locality level, rates can be even more extreme. Certain jurisdictions in Alabama, Louisiana, and Colorado exceed 11% when all local surcharges are stacked. The city of Tacoma, Washington sees combined rates above 10.2%, and parts of Chicago, Illinois reach 10.25% with all transit and special district taxes included.

How Local Sales Taxes Layer On Top

Local sales taxes are imposed by cities, counties, transit authorities, and special taxing districts. They are added on top of the state rate at the point of sale. In states like Colorado, Louisiana, and Alabama, local taxes can more than double the base state rate.

Colorado is a particularly complex example. The state rate is just 2.90%, one of the lowest in the country. However, local jurisdictions stack their own taxes, and some areas in the Denver metro region see combined rates above 10%. The city of Winter Park, a popular ski destination, has a combined rate exceeding 11% when you include county, city, and special district taxes.

Many states cap local sales tax rates. Texas, for example, limits local taxes to 2% above the 6.25% state rate, ensuring the total never exceeds 8.25%. Other states like Alabama and Louisiana impose no such caps, which is why their combined rates rank among the nation's highest. States with uniform statewide rates and no local add-ons, such as Connecticut, Indiana, Kentucky, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, and Rhode Island, offer the most predictable pricing for consumers.

Common Sales Tax Exemptions

Most states exempt certain categories of goods from sales tax to reduce the burden on essential purchases. While exemptions vary by state, common categories include:

  • Groceries: About 32 states fully exempt grocery food from sales tax. Some states tax groceries at a reduced rate. Mississippi, Alabama, and South Dakota are among the few that tax groceries at the full state rate.
  • Clothing: Pennsylvania, New Jersey, Minnesota, and New York exempt most clothing from sales tax. New York's exemption applies to items under $110. Massachusetts exempts clothing items under $175.
  • Prescription medications: Almost all states exempt prescription drugs. Only Illinois taxes them, but at a reduced rate of 1%.
  • Medical devices: Many states exempt items like hearing aids, prosthetics, and wheelchairs.
  • Digital goods: Treatment varies widely. Some states exempt all digital goods, while others tax streaming, downloads, and SaaS differently.

Understanding exemptions matters for budgeting. A family grocery bill of $800 per month in Alabama faces the full 4% state rate plus local taxes, adding up to hundreds of dollars annually. The same family in Texas pays zero sales tax on groceries. These differences add up over time and are worth considering if you are choosing where to live or shop.

Sales Tax on Online Purchases After Wayfair

The landmark 2018 Supreme Court decision in South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc. changed how online sales are taxed. Before this ruling, states could only require sales tax collection from businesses with a physical presence (nexus) in the state. The Wayfair decision established that states can require online sellers to collect sales tax based on "economic nexus" -- a threshold of sales volume or transaction count in the state.

Most states have adopted economic nexus thresholds, typically requiring collection when a seller has over $100,000 in sales or 200 transactions in the state annually. As a result, major online retailers like Amazon now collect sales tax in all states that impose one. Marketplace facilitator laws also require platforms like Amazon, eBay, and Etsy to collect and remit sales tax on behalf of third-party sellers.

If you purchase from a seller that does not collect sales tax, you are technically responsible for reporting and paying "use tax" on your state income tax return. Use tax is equal to the sales tax rate and covers purchases from out-of-state sellers. While enforcement on individual purchases is minimal, many states have added use tax lines to their income tax returns. Some states, like California and New York, have audited individuals for large untaxed purchases such as vehicles, boats, and jewelry bought out of state.

Sales Tax Holidays

Many states offer periodic sales tax holidays, typically before the school year begins, during which certain categories of purchases are exempt from sales tax. These events vary by state but commonly cover:

  • Back-to-school: Clothing, school supplies, and computers below certain price thresholds. Texas, Florida, and Ohio are among the most popular states for these events.
  • Emergency preparedness: Flashlights, batteries, generators, and weather supplies, common in hurricane-prone states like Florida, Texas, and Virginia.
  • Energy-efficient appliances: Some states exempt Energy Star appliances during designated periods.
  • Hunting and fishing: Mississippi and other outdoor-recreation states periodically exempt sporting goods and outdoor equipment.

In 2026, approximately 20 states offer at least one sales tax holiday. Florida offers the most extensive program with multiple holidays throughout the year covering school supplies, hurricane preparedness, recreation, and skilled worker tools. Check your state's department of revenue website for specific dates and eligible items.

Origin-Based vs Destination-Based Sales Tax

States use one of two methods to determine which jurisdiction's rate applies to a sale:

Origin-based states charge sales tax based on where the seller is located. If your business is in an origin-based state, you charge the same rate to all in-state customers regardless of where they are. States using this method include Texas, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Virginia, and Arizona.

Destination-based states charge sales tax based on where the buyer receives the goods. This is the more common approach and is used by most states. It means businesses must calculate the correct combined rate for each customer's address, which can be complex in states with many local jurisdictions.

For interstate sales (online or remote sellers), most states use destination-based sourcing regardless of their in-state rules. This means an online seller typically charges the buyer's local rate, not the seller's rate. The Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement (SSUTA) provides a framework for uniform definitions and simplified filing across its 24 member states, though major states like California, Texas, and New York are not members.

Sales Tax on Services and Digital Goods

Traditionally, sales tax applied only to tangible personal property. As the economy has shifted toward services, states have increasingly expanded their tax bases. However, the approach is far from uniform.

Hawaii and New Mexico tax nearly all services under their broad-based tax systems. South Dakota, Washington, and West Virginia tax a wide range of services. Most other states are selective, taxing some services (like landscaping, pet grooming, or dry cleaning) while exempting others (like medical, legal, and accounting services).

Digital goods and services have become a major area of expansion. Over 30 states now tax digital downloads including e-books, music, movies, and software. Streaming services like Netflix and Spotify are taxed in roughly the same number of states. Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) taxation varies widely: some states tax it as tangible personal property, others exempt it as a service, and many have no clear guidance at all. This ambiguity creates compliance challenges for technology companies operating nationwide.

Sales Tax on Vehicles, Real Estate, and Big Purchases

Major purchases often have special sales tax rules that differ from everyday retail. Understanding these rules can save you significant money on the biggest transactions of your life.

Vehicles. Most states charge sales tax on vehicle purchases, but the rules vary widely. Some states (like Montana) have no vehicle sales tax. Others cap the tax amount or charge a flat fee instead. In many states, when you trade in a vehicle, you only pay sales tax on the difference between the new car price and the trade-in value. A $40,000 car with a $15,000 trade-in means you pay tax on $25,000 in most states. However, California and a few others tax the full purchase price regardless of trade-in value. Buying a car across state lines can be complicated: you typically owe tax in the state where you register the vehicle, not where you buy it.

Real estate. Real property (land and buildings) is generally exempt from sales tax in all states. Instead, real estate is subject to property taxes, transfer taxes, and recording fees. However, building materials used in construction are usually subject to sales tax, which can add thousands of dollars to the cost of a new home or major renovation.

Boats and aircraft. These are subject to sales tax in most states, and the amounts can be substantial given the high price tags. Some states cap the sales tax on boats and aircraft to encourage in-state purchases. South Carolina caps the total sales tax on boats at $500, and several states have similar provisions for aircraft. Many buyers register boats and planes in states with lower taxes, though states aggressively audit these arrangements.

Sales Tax vs Income Tax: How States Balance Revenue

States without income tax tend to rely more heavily on sales tax revenue, and vice versa. Understanding this trade-off helps explain why some states have high sales taxes while others keep them low. Use the Income Tax Calculator to see how your state's income tax compares.

No income tax, higher sales tax: Tennessee, Washington, Texas, and Florida have no state income tax and compensate with substantial sales tax revenue. Tennessee and Washington have some of the nation's highest combined sales tax rates as a direct result of this policy choice.

No sales tax, higher income tax: Oregon has no sales tax but imposes one of the highest state income tax rates in the country, with a top marginal rate of 9.9%. Montana similarly relies on income tax instead of sales tax revenue.

Neither tax: New Hampshire and Alaska have neither a broad-based income tax nor a statewide sales tax. New Hampshire funds its government primarily through property taxes (which are among the nation's highest) and business taxes. Alaska relies heavily on oil revenue and federal transfers. You can estimate property tax costs using the Property Tax Calculator.

The regressive nature of sales tax is a frequent policy debate. Because lower-income households spend a higher percentage of their income on taxable goods, sales tax takes a proportionally larger bite from their budgets. This is one reason many states exempt groceries and other essentials, and why some economists argue for broader reliance on progressive income taxes instead.

Tips for Consumers and Business Owners

For consumers, knowing your combined local rate helps you budget more accurately for major purchases. A $30,000 car in Seattle, Washington faces a combined rate above 10.25%, adding more than $3,075 in sales tax. The same car purchased in Portland, Oregon would have zero sales tax. Cross-border shopping is legal and common in border areas near tax-free states.

For businesses, sales tax compliance is a significant administrative burden. If you sell across state lines, you likely have economic nexus in multiple states and must register, collect, file, and remit in each one. The filing frequency (monthly, quarterly, or annually) depends on your sales volume in each state. Automation software like Avalara, TaxJar, or Vertex can help manage multi-state compliance.

Self-employed individuals and small business owners should also be aware that sales tax obligations are separate from income tax. You may owe both self-employment tax on your earnings and sales tax collection duties on your products. Keeping these two obligations distinct in your bookkeeping prevents costly errors.

Resale certificates are essential for businesses that purchase inventory for resale. Presenting a valid resale certificate to your supplier exempts the purchase from sales tax, since the tax will be collected from the end consumer instead. Each state has its own resale certificate form, though the Streamlined Sales Tax (SST) certificate is accepted in 24 member states.

Future Trends in Sales Tax

Several trends are shaping the future of sales tax in the United States. The expansion of tax bases to include more services and digital goods will continue as the economy becomes increasingly service-oriented. States that rely heavily on taxing physical goods face declining revenue as consumer spending shifts to untaxed services and digital products.

Remote work has created new nexus challenges. When employees work from home in a different state than their employer, it can create tax obligations for the business in states where they previously had no presence. Several states have introduced "convenience of the employer" rules that affect how income and sales taxes are allocated for remote workers.

The push for a federal sales tax or national VAT resurfaces periodically in political discussions. The FairTax proposal, which would replace the federal income tax with a national 23% retail sales tax, has been introduced in Congress multiple times. While unlikely to pass in the near term, these debates highlight the ongoing tension between consumption-based and income-based taxation.

Cryptocurrency and NFT transactions represent another emerging area. Some states have begun clarifying whether digital assets are subject to sales tax when used as payment or sold as collectibles. The IRS has issued guidance on the income tax treatment of crypto, but sales tax treatment remains a patchwork. For crypto-related tax questions, the broader tax implications should not be overlooked when filing your return.

The Streamlined Sales Tax Project continues to work toward greater uniformity among states. As of 2026, 24 states participate in the agreement, which standardizes definitions, simplifies filing procedures, and provides technology tools for rate lookups. While major states like California, Texas, New York, and Florida remain outside the agreement, the project has made significant progress in reducing compliance burdens for multi-state sellers. Whether buying everyday items or planning major purchases, understanding your state and local sales tax rate helps you make smarter financial decisions and avoid surprises at the register.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I have to pay sales tax on purchases from another state?

If the seller does not collect your state's sales tax, you are technically required to pay "use tax" equal to your state's rate when filing your state income tax return. In practice, most states now have marketplace facilitator laws that require large online platforms to collect sales tax automatically, so this situation is becoming less common for everyday purchases.

Which state has the highest sales tax rate?

California has the highest state-level rate at 7.25%. However, when combined with local taxes, Louisiana and Tennessee have the highest average combined rates at 9.55%. Some specific localities in Alabama, Louisiana, and Colorado can exceed 11% when all local surcharges are included.

Is food taxed differently than other items?

In most states, grocery food for home consumption is either fully exempt or taxed at a reduced rate. However, prepared food, restaurant meals, and candy are typically taxed at the full rate. The distinction between "grocery food" and "prepared food" varies by state and can create complex situations, such as whether a grocery store deli sandwich counts as prepared food.

Do I pay sales tax on digital products like software or streaming services?

It depends on the state. About 30 states tax digital goods such as downloaded software, music, movies, and e-books. Streaming services are taxed in most states that have sales tax. SaaS (Software as a Service) taxation is the most inconsistent area, with some states taxing it as tangible property and others exempting it entirely.

Can I deduct sales tax on my federal income tax return?

Yes, if you itemize deductions. You can deduct either state income tax or state sales tax (whichever is higher) as part of the SALT deduction on Schedule A. The total SALT deduction is capped at $10,000. This is especially beneficial for residents of states with no income tax, like Texas or Florida, who can deduct sales tax instead.
