DTI Calculator: Calculate Your Debt-to-Income Ratio

This article was originally published on amortio.com. Visit the original for interactive tools and calculators.
Let's start by debunking the most persistent myth in mortgage qualification: the idea that 43% DTI is an absolute limit that will get you rejected if crossed. It isn't. The 43% threshold originated with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's General Qualified Mortgage rule—specifically as the outer limit for the "General QM" loan category, a non-mainstream designation most borrowers never encounter. For conventional Fannie Mae loans processed through Desktop Underwriter (the automated system handling the vast majority of conforming loans), the maximum DTI approval threshold is 50%. For FHA loans with automated underwriting, it regularly exceeds 43% for borrowers with compensating factors. Knowing the real rules—not the simplified version—is the difference between qualifying and not qualifying for tens of thousands of dollars of purchase price. **Key Takeaways** - DTI is monthly debt payments divided by gross monthly income; lenders use it to measure repayment capacity, not payment behavior - Fannie Mae allows up to 50% DTI for loans approved through its Desktop Underwriter (DU) system; the 43% ceiling applies only to a narrow loan category - FHA targets 31% front-end and 43% back-end for manual underwriting, but automated approval can exceed these thresholds - Front-end DTI (housing costs only) and back-end DTI (all debt) serve different qualification purposes; back-end is almost always the binding constraint - Many items borrowers assume count as "debt" in DTI calculations don't—utilities, insurance premiums, childcare, and subscriptions are all excluded # What DTI Actually Measures Debt-to-income ratio is the percentage of your gross monthly income consumed by monthly debt obligations. "Gross" means pre-tax income—before federal, state, and FICA withholding. **The formula:** **DTI = Monthly Debt Payments ÷ Gross Monthly Income × 100** Example: \$4,200 in monthly debt obligations (including proposed mortgage) ÷ \$10,000 gross income = 42% DTI. Lenders use DTI because it directly quantifies the margin between income and debt service. A borrower with a strong payment history but whose entire income is consumed by existing obligations has limited buffer against income disruption—job loss, medical event, income reduction. DTI makes that exposure explicit. What DTI does NOT capture: assets, savings cushion, career trajectory, net worth, or the interest rate differential between debts. A borrower with 45% DTI and \$300,000 in liquid reserves is materially safer than a 45% DTI borrower with no savings—but standard DTI math treats them identically. This is where compensating factors become important. # Front-End vs. Back-End DTI: The Two Numbers Lenders Actually Use "DTI" is actually two separate calculations with different purposes: **Front-end DTI (Housing Ratio)** Front-end DTI = Proposed housing costs ÷ Gross monthly income Housing costs include: principal + interest + property taxes + homeowner's insurance + HOA dues (if applicable) + PMI (if applicable). This is your PITIA—principal, interest, taxes, insurance, association fees. **Back-end DTI (Total DTI)** Back-end DTI = All monthly debt obligations ÷ Gross monthly income All obligations = housing PITIA + car loans + student loans + minimum credit card payments + personal loans + all other installment and revolving debt. ## Example Calculation - Gross monthly income: \$9,500 - Proposed housing PITI: \$2,375 → **Front-end DTI = 25%** - Car payment: \$475, student loans: \$320, credit card minimums: \$165 - Total back-end DTI = (\$2,375 + \$475 + \$320 + \$165) ÷ \$9,500 = \$3,335 ÷ \$9,500 = **35.1%** Back-end DTI is almost always the binding constraint in mortgage qualification. Front-end ratios are reviewed but rarely disqualify borrowers who clear back-end thresholds. The traditional "28/36 rule" (front-end under 28%, back-end under 36%) reflects historical Fannie Mae manual underwriting guidance. In practice, automated underwriting systems have moved substantially beyond these numbers, and the 28% front-end ceiling is rarely enforced as a hard limit on conforming loans. # DTI Limits by Loan Type: What the Guidelines Actually Say | Loan Type | Front-End Target | Back-End: Manual UW | Back-End: Automated UW | Key Notes | |-----------|-----------------|--------------------|-----------------------|-----------| | Conventional (Fannie Mae) | 28% | 36% (to 45% with comps) | **50% max (DU)** | Fannie Selling Guide updated April 2025 | | Conventional (Freddie Mac) | 28% | 36% (to 45% with comps) | **50% max (LPA)** | LPA = Loan Product Advisor | | FHA | **31%** | **43%** | 50%+ with strong comps | Manual limits stricter than automated | | VA | No hard limit | **41% guideline** | Residual income emphasis | Residual income often overrides DTI | | USDA | 29% | **41%** | Flexible with comps | Rural properties only | | Jumbo/Non-QM | Lender-specific | Typically 43–45% | N/A | Higher credit standards typical | The 50% Fannie Mae DU threshold is significant and frequently unknown to borrowers. Per Fannie Mae's Selling Guide (B3-6-02, updated April 2, 2025), for loans run through DU, the maximum allowable DTI is 50%—with compensating factors typically required above 45%. Since the majority of conforming conventional loans are processed through DU, many borrowers who would be rejected under the "43% rule" can actually qualify. The CFPB's General QM Final Rule, effective March 2021, replaced the original 43% DTI ceiling for General QM loans with an APR-based pricing threshold. The explicit 43% limit now applies primarily as an alternative path for non-mainstream General QM loans—not the primary conventional market. VA loans present a unique case: the Department of Veterans Affairs focuses more on residual income (income remaining after all debt obligations and living expenses) than DTI percentage. A veteran with 45% DTI and sufficient residual income for their family size often qualifies where a conventional borrower would not. # What Counts as Debt in Your DTI Calculation This is where many borrowers inadvertently handicap themselves with misinformation. Here's precisely what underwriters include: **Counted in back-end DTI:** - Proposed housing PITIA (the new mortgage + taxes + insurance + HOA + PMI) - Car loan monthly payments - Student loan payments (see the income-driven repayment section below) - Personal loan minimum payments - Minimum monthly credit card payments (not balances—only required minimums) - Child support and alimony obligations as disclosed - Co-signed loan payments if you have personal liability, even if the primary borrower pays - Business debt if personally guaranteed and the business cannot demonstrate sufficient cash flow to cover it independently **NOT counted in back-end DTI:** - Utility bills (electric, gas, water, internet, phone) - Health, auto, and life insurance premiums (except homeowner's insurance within PITI) - Groceries, clothing, transportation costs - Childcare and daycare expenses - Subscriptions, gym memberships, streaming services - Medical bills not currently in collections with a required payment plan - Lease payments on a property you're vacating The credit card minimum payment distinction is particularly important: DTI uses the **minimum required payment**, not your typical payment or outstanding balance. A \$15,000 credit card balance may carry a \$400 minimum monthly payment. Your actual payments of \$800/month don't reduce your DTI—but paying the balance down far enough to lower the required minimum does. # The Student Loan DTI Problem Student loan debt creates some of the most complex DTI scenarios in mortgage underwriting, primarily because of income-driven repayment (IDR) plans that can produce \$0 or very low monthly required payments. **Conventional loans (Fannie Mae):** If you have student loans on income-driven repayment with a \$0 payment, Fannie requires lenders to use 1% of the outstanding balance as the monthly payment in DTI calculations. On \$85,000 in student loans, that's \$850/month in DTI exposure regardless of your actual payment. **Conventional loans (Freddie Mac):** Uses 0.5% of the outstanding balance for \$0-payment IDR plans—half the Fannie Mae requirement. On the same \$85,000 balance, that's \$425/month vs. \$850/month. This difference alone can swing qualifying for buyers with substantial student debt. **FHA loans:** Changed its approach in August 2021 to use the actual monthly payment if greater than zero, or 0.5% of outstanding balance if the payment is zero or undocumented. Practical implication: borrowers with large student loan balances on IDR plans may find they qualify with Freddie Mac's guidelines (processed through LPA) but not Fannie Mae's (processed through DU). Ask your lender which agency's system they use and whether running through the other system is an option. # How to Calculate Your DTI: Step by Step **Step 1: Determine gross monthly income** Add all documented income sources: - Salary/wages: annual gross ÷ 12 - Bonus/commission: 24-month average if consistent (lender-specific) - Self-employment: 2-year average from Schedule C/K-1 after business deductions - Rental income: 75% of gross rent if documented with lease agreements and 2-year history - Investment/dividend income: documented 2-year average - Social Security/pension: gross benefit amount **Step 2: Add up monthly debt obligations** List every required monthly payment: - Proposed housing PITI (from lender's estimate) - All installment debt minimums - All revolving debt minimums (credit cards, HELOCs) - Any court-ordered payments **Step 3: Calculate both ratios** Front-end = Housing PITI ÷ Gross monthly income Back-end = All obligations ÷ Gross monthly income Use the [DTI calculator](/dti-calculator/) to run these calculations instantly—input your income and debts to see both ratios and how they compare to qualification thresholds for different loan types. # How to Improve Your DTI Before Applying There are two levers: reduce monthly debt payments or increase documented income. Both take time—plan 6–12 months ahead when possible. ## Reduce Monthly Obligations **Pay off installment loans entirely.** A \$280/month car payment eliminated saves \$280/month in DTI-counted obligations. A single payoff can sometimes make the difference between qualifying and not. **Pay down revolving balances to reduce minimums.** Eliminating a \$4,000 credit card balance eliminates a \$120/month minimum payment. On a \$9,000 gross income, that's a 1.3% DTI reduction. **Avoid new debt in the 6 months before applying.** New car loans, personal loans, and new credit card accounts all add required monthly payments to your DTI. Standard advice to avoid major purchases before a home purchase exists precisely for this reason. **Get removed from co-signed loans.** Co-signed obligations count in your DTI even if you never make the payment. Removal requires the primary borrower to refinance the loan into their name alone—start this process 6–12 months before applying. ## Increase Documented Income **Document all income sources consistently.** Self-employment income on Schedule C qualifies after 2 years of consistent documentation. Rental income (75% of gross rent after documented expenses) counts with a 2-year history and current lease agreements. **Wait for a raise to document.** A written offer letter for a new position with higher salary can qualify as income if you start before closing or within 60 days. Recent W-2 raises documented in pay stubs can be used for the current year's rate rather than the trailing 24-month average. **Add a qualified co-borrower.** Adding a spouse or co-borrower with income and limited personal debt distributes the DTI calculation across a higher combined income base. Adding \$3,500/month in income while adding \$0 in debt can bring a 48% DTI down to 38% or below. # Compensating Factors That Allow Higher DTI Lenders don't apply DTI thresholds mechanically—they weigh factors indicating lower risk despite elevated ratios. **Significant cash reserves.** Fannie Mae's guidelines explicitly recognize 12+ months of liquid reserves as a compensating factor for high-DTI borrowers. A borrower with \$60,000 in savings after a \$50,000 down payment demonstrates a fundamentally different risk profile than one with no reserves. **Low payment shock.** If your proposed housing payment is similar to your current rent, the default risk that DTI concerns are designed to capture is mitigated. A borrower whose mortgage payment is \$50 more than their current rent is underwriting different behavioral risk than one whose housing cost triples. **Strong credit score.** FICO scores above 760 correlate with materially lower default rates across all DTI levels. Many DU approvals at 48–50% DTI require 740+ credit scores as a condition. The two variables—DTI and credit score—interact in automated systems. **High down payment.** A 30% down payment at 48% DTI signals meaningfully different risk than 5% down at the same DTI. Equity cushion (lower LTV) reduces lender exposure regardless of DTI level. **Stable long-term employment.** Fifteen years in the same industry with consistent income trajectory demonstrates stability that DTI ratios can't quantify. Underwriters with discretion factor this in; automated systems capture it indirectly through income documentation patterns. # When High DTI Is a Real Problem vs. a Paper Problem Not all high-DTI situations carry equal risk—and the ratio alone doesn't tell the full story. **High DTI that's manageable:** Ratio driven by low-interest, fixed-rate debt (student loans at 4.5%, car at 3.9%) with strong gross income, 12+ months reserves, and stable employment. The dollar buffer between income and obligations may be large even when the percentage looks tight. **High DTI that warrants genuine concern:** Ratio driven by high-rate revolving debt, recent debt accumulation, and minimal reserves. Here both the percentage and the underlying dollar exposure signal real repayment risk. The DTI formula treats a \$400/month student loan at 4% identically to a \$400/month credit card at 24%. The after-payment cash flow implications are completely different. Underwriters with judgment account for this; automated systems don't always. Before you talk to a lender, know your DTI numbers cold. It positions you to have a strategic conversation rather than simply accepting whatever DU returns. # FAQ: DTI Calculator and Mortgage Qualification ## What is a good debt-to-income ratio for buying a house? Under 36% is considered strong by traditional standards and rarely triggers underwriting scrutiny. Most conventional loans approve up to 45% back-end DTI comfortably; Fannie Mae's DU system approves up to 50% for qualified borrowers. FHA targets 43% but allows higher with automated approvals and compensating factors. Your goal is as low as reasonably achievable—lower DTI typically correlates with better rate pricing and fewer underwriting conditions. ## Does the proposed mortgage payment count in my DTI calculation? Yes. Back-end DTI always includes the proposed new housing payment (full PITIA: principal, interest, taxes, insurance, HOA if applicable, PMI if applicable). This is the total debt load the lender is evaluating. Front-end DTI includes only this housing cost—it's the ratio that shows how much of your income goes to housing specifically. ## Can I get approved for a mortgage with 50% DTI? Possibly. Fannie Mae's Desktop Underwriter allows up to 50% DTI for conforming conventional loans with compensating factors. VA loans have no hard DTI ceiling. FHA automated underwriting can exceed 43% for strong borrowers. Jumbo and non-QM lenders typically cap at 43–45%. Strong credit scores (740+) and significant cash reserves significantly improve high-DTI approval odds. ## What monthly expenses don't count in mortgage DTI? Utilities (electric, gas, water, internet, phone), insurance premiums (health, auto, life), groceries, childcare, transportation costs, subscriptions, and memberships are all excluded from DTI calculations. Only formal debt obligations with required monthly payments—loans, minimum credit card payments, and court-ordered obligations—count. ## How does student loan debt affect my DTI when on income-driven repayment? For Fannie Mae conventional loans, a \$0 IDR payment requires using 1% of outstanding balance as the DTI payment (e.g., \$80,000 balance = \$800/month in DTI). Freddie Mac uses 0.5% (\$400/month on the same balance). FHA uses the actual payment or 0.5% if payment is zero. Borrowers with large student debt often find Freddie Mac guidelines more favorable—ask your lender specifically which agency's system they use. ## How quickly can I meaningfully reduce my DTI? Paying off installment loans reduces DTI immediately at the next credit pull. Paying down revolving balances reduces DTI proportionally as minimums decrease. A targeted 6–12 month payoff plan focusing on high-minimum, lower-balance debts first can realistically move DTI from 48% to 40–42%. Adding documented income sources (rental income, spouse's employment) can produce faster results depending on how income is verified. ## What's the difference between front-end and back-end DTI? Front-end DTI (also called the housing ratio) includes only the proposed housing PITI payment divided by gross income. Back-end DTI includes all monthly debt obligations—housing plus car loans, student loans, credit card minimums, personal loans—divided by gross income. Lenders use both but back-end DTI is almost always the binding constraint. The traditional targets are 28% front-end and 36% back-end, though automated underwriting routinely approves substantially higher ratios. --- DTI is the mortgage qualification variable most within your control in the months before applying. Unlike credit scores (which require consistent payment behavior over time) or income (which is what it is), debt obligations can be strategically reduced.
